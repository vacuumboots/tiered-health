## ::gist::
### What are kidney stones?

A kidney stone is a hard crystal that forms in your urine. It can sit quietly in your kidney or — when it moves into the narrow tube (ureter) that drains your kidney — cause the worst pain of your life. About 1 in 10 people will get one at some point.

### What to do right now

1. **Drink water.** Lots. If you think you might have a stone, start drinking. Aim for 2–3 litres a day. This helps small stones pass.
2. **Take ibuprofen** (Advil, Motrin) or naproxen (Aleve) for the pain. NSAIDs work better than opioids for kidney stone pain and have fewer side effects.
3. **Strain your urine.** Pee through a sieve or coffee filter into a cup. If you catch the stone, keep it — knowing what it's made of helps prevent another one.
4. **Wait.** Stones smaller than 5 mm pass on their own about 80% of the time. It may take a few days to a few weeks.

::danger::
#### ⚠️ Go to the emergency room if:

- You have a fever or chills with the pain (could mean a kidney infection)
- The pain is so bad you can't sit still or find a comfortable position
- You can't keep water down (vomiting)
- You see blood in your urine (pink or red)
- You haven't been able to pee for several hours
::end::

::callout::
#### Good news

Stones are common but rarely dangerous if caught early. With proper prevention — mostly drinking enough water — most people can avoid a second stone. About half of people who get one stone will have another within 5 years, but that drops sharply if you stay hydrated.
::end::

## ::adult::
### What's happening in your body

Your kidneys filter about 180 litres of blood every day and concentrate waste into about 1–2 litres of urine. Normally, substances like calcium, oxalate, and phosphate stay dissolved in the urine. But if the urine becomes too concentrated (too many stone-forming crystals, too little water), the minerals start to crystallize — just like sugar crystals forming in honey that's been sitting too long.

The crystals grow on tiny calcium deposits on the tip of your kidney papillae called **Randall's plaques**. Over weeks to months, these crystals layer together into a solid stone. Most stones are made of **calcium oxalate** (about 80%). Others are calcium phosphate, uric acid, struvite (from infections), or cystine.

When the stone is small and sitting in the kidney, you might not feel anything. The pain starts when the stone drops into the **ureter** — the thin, muscular tube connecting your kidney to your bladder. The ureter is only about 3–4 mm wide and doesn't stretch well. The stone gets stuck, blocks urine flow, and the kidney swells (hydronephrosis). Your body responds with intense waves of cramping as the ureter tries to push the stone down. This is **renal colic** — the classic "worst pain of my life" kidney stone pain.

### Symptoms

- **Severe flank pain:** sudden, waves of pain on one side of your lower back, just under your ribs. The pain builds, peaks, and eases in cycles. People often can't sit still — they writhe, pace, and try to find a position that helps.
- **Pain radiating to the groin:** as the stone moves down the ureter, the pain moves with it — to your lower belly, groin, testicle (in men), or labia (in women).
- **Hematuria (blood in urine):** the stone scrapes the lining of the ureter. Visible blood (pink or cola-coloured urine) happens in about 30% of cases; microscopic blood in nearly all.
- **Nausea and vomiting:** the intense pain stimulates the vagus nerve, triggering nausea. Many people vomit.
- **Urinary urgency and frequency:** when the stone is near the bladder (at the ureterovesical junction — UVJ), it irritates the bladder and makes you feel like you need to pee constantly.
- **Painful urination (dysuria):** common when the stone is at the UVJ.

### Treatment

- **Conservative management (watchful waiting):** For stones <5 mm, spontaneous passage rate is 68–90%. Drink 2–3 L/day. NSAIDs (ibuprofen, diclofenac, ketorolac) for pain — they are more effective than opioids and cause less nausea. Most stones pass within 2 weeks of leaving the kidney. Stones >5 mm are less likely to pass spontaneously and may need intervention.
- **Medical expulsive therapy (MET):** Tamsulosin (Flomax), an alpha-blocker, relaxes the ureter smooth muscle and may help stones >5 mm pass faster. A large Cochrane review (2018) found it reduced time to passage by about 3–4 days and reduced the need for surgery. For stones <5 mm, the benefit is marginal.
- **Lithotripsy (ESWL — Extracorporeal Shock Wave Lithotripsy):** Shock waves from outside the body break the stone into sand-sized pieces that pass in the urine. Best for stones <2 cm in the kidney or upper ureter. No incision needed. Success rate ~70–90% for appropriate stones. Complications: bruising, blood in urine, possible need for multiple sessions.
- **Ureteroscopy with laser lithotripsy:** A thin scope is passed up the ureter to the stone. A laser (holmium:YAG) fragments it. This is the preferred method for stones in the mid/lower ureter and for stones >1.5 cm. Success rate >90%. Often requires a temporary ureteral stent afterward.
- **PCNL (Percutaneous Nephrolithotomy):** A small incision in the back and a direct tunnel into the kidney. For very large stones (>2 cm), staghorn calculi, or stones that failed ESWL. Most effective for large stones but most invasive. Hospital stay 1–2 days.

::callout::
#### What NOT to do

- Don't drink cranberry juice — it increases oxalate levels and can make stones worse.
- Don't take high-dose vitamin C supplements (>1000 mg/day) — vitamin C is metabolized to oxalate.
- Don't decrease your calcium intake — this is a common myth. Dietary calcium actually *reduces* stone risk by binding oxalate in the gut. It's *calcium supplements* (without food) that may increase risk.
- Don't wait indefinitely with a known stone. If it hasn't passed after 4–6 weeks, see a urologist.
- Don't ignore fever with flank pain — this is a medical emergency (obstructive pyelonephritis).
::end::

## ::full::
### Pathophysiology

Nephrolithiasis is a multi-step process of **supersaturation → nucleation → growth → aggregation → retention**.

**Supersaturation:** Urine concentration of stone-forming solutes (calcium, oxalate, phosphate, uric acid) exceeds their solubility limit. This is the thermodynamic driver for all stone formation. The key protective factors are urine volume (dilution) and urinary inhibitors — citrate (chelates calcium), magnesium, pyrophosphate, and Tamm-Horsfall protein (uromodulin). When inhibitors are deficient or the solute load is excessive, the metastable zone is breached.

**Nucleation:** Crystal nuclei form on **Randall's plaques** — subepithelial deposits of calcium apatite that form at the tip of the renal papilla. These plaques are found in virtually all kidneys of recurrent calcium stone formers, and their presence correlates with stone activity. The mechanism involves interstitial deposition of calcium phosphate at the basement membrane of the thin loop of Henle, followed by extension into the interstitium and eventual erosion through the papillary urothelium, where the plaque is exposed to urine.

**Growth and aggregation:** Once a crystal nucleus is exposed to supersaturated urine, it grows by epitaxial crystal addition — calcium oxalate monohydrate (COM, whewellite) crystals layer onto the plaque surface. Aggregation of multiple crystals accelerates growth. Inhibitors like citrate and osteopontin normally block aggregation; their dysfunction or deficiency promotes rapid stone enlargement.

**Retention:** A stone must be retained in the collecting system long enough to become clinically significant. Anatomical factors (calyceal diverticula, horseshoe kidney, ureteropelvic junction obstruction), papillary tip abnormalities, and the stone's size/roughness all affect retention. Once the stone reaches ~2–3 mm, it may dislodge and migrate into the ureter — at which point the clinical presentation shifts from incidental to acute.

**Stone composition determines approach:**

| Composition | Frequency | Key features |
|-------------|-----------|--------------|
| Calcium oxalate (whewellite/weddellite) | ~80% | Radiopaque. Monohydrate (COM, whewellite) is denser and harder to fragment with ESWL than dihydrate (COD, weddellite). |
| Calcium phosphate (apatite/brushite) | ~10% | Radiopaque. Associated with renal tubular acidosis, hyperparathyroidism, and medullary sponge kidney. Brushite is very ESWL-resistant. |
| Uric acid | ~5–10% | Radiolucent (not visible on plain X-ray — diagnosed by CT or ultrasound). Forms in acidic urine (pH <5.5). Treat with urine alkalinization (K-citrate). |
| Struvite (magnesium ammonium phosphate) | ~5–10% | "Infection stones" — form only in urine containing urease-producing bacteria (Proteus, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas). Can grow into large staghorn calculi. |
| Cystine | <1% | Autosomal recessive defect in dibasic amino acid transport (SLC3A1/SLC7A9). Causes hexagonal crystals. |

### Differential diagnosis

1. **Acute pyelonephritis:** Fever, flank pain, dysuria. Key difference: fever is present from the start, pain is constant (not colicky). Urinalysis shows WBC casts, nitrites, leukocyte esterase.
2. **Ruptured AAA (abdominal aortic aneurysm):** Sudden severe abdominal or flank pain radiating to the back in an older patient (>60) with vascular risk factors. The pain is constant, not colicky. May have hypotension, pulsatile mass. CT with contrast is diagnostic.
3. **Testicular torsion:** Acute scrotal/testicular pain with nausea. The pain originates in the scrotum, not the flank. The testicle is swollen, tender, and may be horizontally oriented. Doppler ultrasound shows absent blood flow.
4. **Ovarian torsion:** Acute lower abdominal/pelvic pain in women. Pain is constant, worsens with movement. Ultrasound with Doppler shows ovarian enlargement and absent venous flow.
5. **Acute appendicitis:** Right lower quadrant pain with rebound tenderness, fever, anorexia. The pain migrates from the periumbilical region — it's not colicky and doesn't radiate to the groin. Positive psoas/obturator signs.
6. **Diverticulitis:** Left lower quadrant pain, fever, change in bowel habits. Pain is constant, not colicky. CT shows diverticula with pericolonic inflammation.
7. **Renal infarction:** Sudden flank pain from renal artery thromboembolism. Atrial fibrillation is a risk factor (source of emboli). Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) is markedly elevated. CT with contrast shows a wedge-shaped perfusion defect.

### Diagnostic workup

| Test | What it shows | When to order |
|------|---------------|---------------|
| **Urinalysis** | Hematuria (≥3 RBC/hpf), pH (low in uric acid stones, high in struvite/calcium phosphate), crystals, WBCs (infection), nitrites, specific gravity | Every patient — first-line, cheap, fast |
| **Non-contrast CT scan (CT KUB)** | Stone location, size (mm), density (HU), number of stones, secondary signs (hydronephrosis, perinephric stranding). Sensitivity ~97%, specificity ~96%. Detects radiolucent stones (uric acid). | Gold standard for acute renal colic — highest sensitivity and specificity |
| **Kidney ultrasound** | Hydronephrosis, large stones (>5 mm) in the kidney, ureteral jets (asymmetry suggests obstruction). Less sensitive for small stones and mid-ureter stones. | First-line in pregnancy, children, or to reduce radiation exposure. Use with plain abdominal X-ray (KUB) to improve detection |
| **Plain abdominal X-ray (KUB)** | Radiopaque stones (calcium-based, struvite, cystine). Radiolucent stones (uric acid) are invisible. ~50–60% sensitivity. Useful for follow-up of known radiopaque stones. | Monitoring stone burden over time in known calcium stones. Poor sensitivity as a stand-alone test for acute colic |
| **CT IVP (CT urogram)** | Detailed renal anatomy, stone location, and collecting system opacification. Better for surgical planning (PCNL access) and suspected anatomical abnormalities | Pre-surgical planning, suspected ureteropelvic junction obstruction, medullary sponge kidney |
| **Serum creatinine** | Baseline renal function. Elevated in obstruction (especially bilateral or solitary kidney), or pre-existing CKD | Every patient — essential before IV contrast or surgery |
| **Serum calcium + PTH** | Primary hyperparathyroidism (↑Ca²⁺, ↑PTH, ↓PO₄). Found in ~5–8% of calcium stone formers. Corrected calcium >10.5 mg/dL or ionized calcium elevated | First-time calcium stone former or recurrent stones |
| **24-hour urine collection** | Volume, calcium, oxalate, citrate, uric acid, sodium, magnesium, phosphate, pH, supersaturation profiles. Best test for identifying metabolic risk factors | Recurrent stone formers, high-risk first-time formers (children, solitary kidney, family history), all patients after a second stone |
| **Stone analysis (infrared spectroscopy or X-ray diffraction)** | Composition — determines medical management (e.g., uric acid → alkalinization; struvite → antibiotics) | Every patient who passes or has a stone removed — this is essential |
| **CT stone density (Hounsfield units)** | Predicts ESWL success: <500 HU = good fragmentation; >1000 HU = ESWL-resistant (better with ureteroscopy/laser). Uric acid stones are ~300–500 HU | Planning treatment for stones >5 mm that may need intervention |

### Evidence summary

**Epidemiology:** The prevalence of kidney stones in the US is ~8.8% (10.6% men, 7.2% women), according to the 2007–2010 NHANES analysis by Scales et al. (2012). Prevalence has increased nearly 3-fold since the 1970s, driven by rising obesity, diabetes, and dietary changes (higher sodium, lower calcium intake). Lifetime risk: ~12% in men, ~6% in women. Recurrence rate without prevention: ~50% at 5 years, ~80% lifetime.

**Medical expulsive therapy (MET):** The Cochrane review by Campschroer et al. (2018), including 55 RCTs (n=6,606), found that alpha-blockers (tamsulosin) increased stone passage rate compared to controls (RR 1.44, 95% CI 1.23–1.69) and reduced time to passage by ~3.4 days. The effect was strongest for stones >5 mm in the distal ureter (RR 1.57). For stones <5 mm, benefit was marginal (RR 1.16, 95% CI 0.95–1.40). The 2020 UK multicentre trial (SUSPEND, n=1,167) found no benefit of tamsulosin over placebo for all-comers — but subgroup analysis confirmed benefit for larger distal stones. Current EAU guidelines recommend MET for distal ureteral stones >5 mm.

**ESWL vs ureteroscopy:** The Cochrane review by Srisubat et al. (2014) found that ureteroscopy achieved a higher stone-free rate for distal ureteral stones than ESWL (stone-free rate 93% vs 84%; RR 1.08, 95% CI 1.01–1.15) but had a higher complication rate (mostly minor: ureteral perforation, stricture). For renal stones <2 cm, ESWL remains first-line in many guidelines due to its non-invasive nature. Stone-free rates for ESWL are 70–90% depending on stone density and location.

**Prevention — fluid intake:** A landmark prospective study (Curhan et al., 1998) of the Health Professionals Follow-up Study (n=45,821) found that men who drank the most fluid (≥2.5 L/day) had a 29% lower risk of stone formation compared to those drinking the least (<1.3 L/day) (RR 0.71, 95% CI 0.57–0.88). The effect was dose-dependent: each 200 mL increase in fluid intake reduced risk by ~5%.

**Prevention — dietary calcium and oxalate:** Contrary to historical belief, dietary calcium *reduces* stone risk. Curhan et al. (1993) showed in the Nurses' Health Study (n=91,731) that dietary calcium intake was inversely associated with stone risk (RR 0.65 for highest vs lowest quintile). The mechanism: calcium binds oxalate in the gut, reducing oxalate absorption and urinary oxalate excretion. High supplemental calcium (>1,000 mg/day) without food, however, increased risk (RR 1.20).

**Uric acid stones:** Urine alkalinization with potassium citrate (30–60 mEq/day) dissolves existing uric acid stones (success rate ~80%) and prevents recurrence. Target urine pH 6.5–7.2. Urinary pH <5.5 is the single strongest risk factor. A 2022 meta-analysis confirmed that allopurinol reduces uric acid stone recurrence but only when urine pH is simultaneously corrected.

**Shock wave lithotripsy safety:** A 2021 systematic review found that ESWL carries a small risk of perinephric hematoma (<1%) and may be associated with a long-term increase in new-onset hypertension in some cohorts, though causality remains debated. The risk of diabetes mellitus after ESWL (from pancreatic injury) has been suggested but not consistently replicated.

**Struvite stones:** Management requires complete stone clearance (PCNL is the treatment of choice) combined with eradication of the causative organism. Without complete removal, recurrence rates exceed 50% at 1 year. Acetohydroxamic acid (a urease inhibitor) may reduce growth but has significant side effects (headache, tremor, thrombosis).

### Surgical options

- **ESWL (Extracorporeal Shock Wave Lithotripsy):** Non-invasive. Shock waves generated by an electromagnetic or piezoelectric source fragment stones into passable debris. Best for stones <2 cm, in the kidney or upper ureter, with HU <1000. Typically outpatient. Limitations: obesity (BMI >35 increases distance from skin to stone, reduces efficacy), cystine stones (ESWL-resistant), distal ureteral stones (poor acoustic window).
- **Ureteroscopy (URS) with holmium laser lithotripsy:** A 7.5–11 Fr semi-rigid or flexible ureteroscope advanced to the stone. The holmium:YAG laser (2100 nm) fragments stones by photothermal ablation, producing dust (<1 mm) or fragments for basket retrieval. Success rate >90% for stones <2 cm. Requires ureteral stent in ~60% of cases (for 3–14 days). Complications: ureteral perforation (~2%), stricture (~1%), infection (~2%).
- **PCNL (Percutaneous Nephrolithotomy):** A 1 cm flank incision; percutaneous renal access under fluoroscopic or ultrasound guidance; tract dilation to 24–30 Fr; stone fragmentation with ultrasound, pneumatic, or laser lithotripsy. Treatment of choice for stones >2 cm, staghorn calculi, and cystine stones. Complete stone-free rate for staghorn stones ~75–85% with a single procedure (up to 95% with staged procedures or second-look nephroscopy). Hospital stay 1–2 days. Complications: bleeding (transfusion rate 3–5%), pleural injury (if supracostal access, 5–10%), infection/sepsis (~3%).
- **Laparoscopic/open ureterolithotomy:** Rarely needed. Reserved for very large impacted stones (>2 cm) in the proximal ureter, or when ESWL and URS have failed. Rare in the endourology era.

### Open questions / active research

- What is the optimal medical prevention protocol for recurrent calcium oxalate stone formers? The role of thiazide diuretics, citrate supplementation, and dietary modification alone vs in combination is debated.
- Can gut microbiome manipulation (Oxalobacter formigenes) reduce oxalate absorption and stone risk? O. formigenes colonizes the gut of ~60% of the population and degrades dietary oxalate; its absence is a risk factor, but recolonization trials have been disappointing.
- What is the role of high-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU) or burst-wave lithotripsy as a non-invasive alternative to ESWL? Early human studies show promising fragmentation with less tissue injury.
- Does the growing use of GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide) affect kidney stone risk? The rapid weight loss and gut dysmotility may alter oxalate absorption, potentially increasing risk.
- Can artificial intelligence interpretation of CT KUB predict stone composition (uric acid vs calcium) from Hounsfield units and texture analysis? Proof-of-concept studies show >85% accuracy, but prospective validation is needed.
- Does the "obesity epidemic" shift stone composition toward uric acid stones? The strong association of metabolic syndrome with uric acid stones may drive a compositional shift.

## ::timeline::
- time: Day 1–3
  text: Acute renal colic. Severe pain, nausea, hematuria. Diagnosis via CT KUB or ultrasound. Start NSAIDs, tamsulosin if stone >5 mm and distal. Push fluids 2–3 L/day. Strain urine.
- time: Day 3–10
  text: Pain subsides in waves. Stone may pass — 60–80% of stones <5 mm pass by this point. The "pain-free gap" can be misleading — revisit care if pain returns.
- time: Week 2–4
  text: If stone hasn't passed, reassess. Persistent stones >5 mm in the ureter should be referred to urology for intervention (ESWL or ureteroscopy). Stones <5 mm still have a reasonable chance of spontaneous passage up to 6 weeks.
- time: Week 4–6
  text: Spontaneous passage becomes unlikely after 6 weeks for ureteral stones. Surgical intervention (ureteroscopy with laser, ESWL) is indicated. Collect passed stone for analysis — this determines the prevention plan.
- time: After stone removal/passage
  text: Metabolic evaluation: 24-hour urine collection (after 3–4 weeks), serum calcium/PTH in calcium stone formers. Start prevention: increase fluids to maintain urine output ≥2.5 L/day, reduce sodium, maintain adequate dietary calcium, limit oxalate-rich foods (spinach, rhubarb, nuts, chocolate, tea).
