## ::gist::
### What is plantar fasciitis?

Your plantar fascia — a thick band of tissue running from your heel bone to the base of your toes — has developed tiny tears and degeneration where it attaches to your heel. It's not really "itis" (inflammation); it's more like a fraying rope from overuse. About 1 in 10 people get heel pain like this at some point.

### What to do right now

1. **Stretch your calf and foot first thing.** Before you step out of bed, loop a towel around your foot and gently pull your toes toward you. Hold for 30 seconds. This alone cuts morning pain more than any other single thing.
2. **Ice the sore spot.** Freeze a water bottle, put it under your foot, and roll it around for 10–15 minutes. That massages and cools at the same time.
3. **Wear supportive shoes.** Avoid bare feet, flip-flops, or flat shoes. A shoe with a firm sole and arch support makes a big difference.
4. **Take ibuprofen** (Advil, Motrin) if you can take it safely — it helps with pain especially in the first few weeks.

::danger::
#### ⚠️ Go to the emergency room if:

- Your heel is red, hot, and swollen (could be an infection like calcaneal osteomyelitis)
- You have numbness or tingling in your foot
- You stepped on something and the pain started after that
- You have a fever along with the heel pain
::end::

::callout::
#### Good news

For 80–90% of people, plantar fasciitis gets better with simple at-home treatments within 6–12 months. Most improvements happen in the first 3–6 months if you stick with daily stretching. Surgery is almost never needed.
::end::

## ::adult::
### What's happening in your body

The **plantar fascia** is a thick, tough band of connective tissue — like a ligament, but broader and flatter — that runs from your **calcaneus** (heel bone) to the bases of your toes. Think of it like a bowstring: when you push off to walk or run, it tightens to support your foot's arch. Every step stretches it.

Over time, repeated micro-trauma at the heel attachment causes **collagen degeneration**, not acute inflammation. Under a microscope, the tissue shows **angiofibroblastic hyperplasia** — disorganized collagen fibers, increased ground substance, and new blood vessels where they shouldn't be. The condition is more accurately called **plantar fasciosis** or **plantar fasciopathy**, since neutrophils and traditional inflammatory markers are largely absent in chronic cases.

The classic clue: your first steps out of bed are excruciating. After a night's sleep, the plantar fascia shortens and tightens as it heals in a relaxed position. When you stand up abruptly, it stretches suddenly, tearing through the microscar tissue that formed overnight. After a few steps, the tissue warms up and the pain settles — but it comes back after sitting for a while, then again the next morning.

### Risk factors

- **Tight calf muscles (gastrocnemius tightness):** the single strongest risk factor. Limited ankle dorsiflexion (<10°) forces the foot to pronate more, loading the plantar fascia excessively.
- **Obesity:** every extra kilogram increases force across the fascia by roughly 3–4× during walking.
- **Prolonged standing:** occupations like nursing, retail, and factory work are overrepresented.
- **Flat feet (pes planus) or high arches:** both alter the normal tension distribution across the fascia.
- **Sudden increase in activity:** starting a running program, increasing mileage, or switching to minimalist shoes too quickly.

### Symptoms

- **Sharp heel pain with the first steps in the morning:** the hallmark. Rates 8–9/10 for the first 5–10 steps, then settles to a dull ache.
- **Pain after prolonged sitting:** "start-up" pain that improves after walking a few steps but returns after rest (gel phenomenon).
- **Pain with weight-bearing activities:** worse after standing for long periods, brisk walking, running, or jumping.
- **Tenderness to palpation:** the spot is just medial (inside) of the center of the heel pad at the calcaneal tuberosity attachment.
- **Pain with passive dorsiflexion of the toes:** stretching the fascia reproduces the pain.

### Treatment

- **Calf and plantar fascia stretching:** the cornerstone of treatment. The best-studied protocol is the DiGiovanni *runner's stretch* (gastrocnemius stretch with knee extended) combined with the *plantar fascia-specific stretch* (pulling the toes back while seated). Do both before taking a single step in the morning.
- **Supportive footwear and orthotics:** shoes with a stiff sole and arch support reduce fascia strain by 10–30%. Over-the-counter arch supports (silicone heel cups, prefabricated orthotics) work almost as well as custom ones for most people.
- **Night splints:** keep the ankle in neutral position and the toes dorsiflexed while you sleep, preventing the fascia from tightening overnight. Best for people with morning pain lasting >30 minutes.
- **Ice massage:** roll a frozen water bottle under the arch for 10–15 minutes, 2–3 times daily, especially after activity.
- **NSAIDs:** ibuprofen or naproxen for 2–4 weeks. Evidence is limited — NSAIDs treat inflammation, but this condition is primarily degenerative. Still, many people get symptomatic relief early on.
- **Activity modification:** reduce high-impact loading. Switch to elliptical, cycling, or swimming while symptoms settle.

::callout::
#### What NOT to do

- Don't walk barefoot on hard surfaces — every barefoot step adds tension to the fascia.
- Don't do high-impact activities (running, jumping, plyometrics) through the pain.
- Don't expect a quick fix — this takes weeks to months. Most people start feeling better at 6–8 weeks with consistent stretching.
- Don't get a corticosteroid injection without trying stretching first. It can help short-term but increases risk of plantar fascia rupture (about 2–4% risk).
- Don't ignore it and "walk it off" — the degeneration gets worse, and recovery takes longer the more you delay treatment.
::end::

## ::timeline::
- time: Day 1–7
  text: Start twice-daily stretching (calf + plantar fascia-specific). Ice massage after walking. Switch to supportive shoes. Morning pain should begin to shorten in duration after the first week.
- time: Week 2–4
  text: Pain intensity starts dropping. Morning pain may drop from 8/10 to 4–5/10. Add night splint if morning pain persists >30 minutes. Continue stretching — this is where most people stop and relapse.
- time: Month 2–3
  text: Significant improvement for most. Morning pain may be 1–2/10 or gone. Begin gradual return to activity. Switch from daily stretching to maintenance (3–4×/week).
- time: Month 3–6
  text: 80–90% of people are substantially better. If not improving by month 4, consider advanced interventions: shockwave therapy, PRP injection, or specialist referral.
- time: Month 6–12
  text: Most reach full resolution. For refractory cases, reassess for differential diagnoses (calcaneal stress fracture, nerve entrapment, fat pad atrophy).

## ::full::
### Pathophysiology

Plantar fasciopathy is a **degenerative enthesopathy** — a non-inflammatory breakdown of the collagenous attachment of the plantar fascia to the **medial calcaneal tubercle**. The dominant histopathological finding is **angiofibroblastic hyperplasia**: fibroblast hypercellularity, chondroid metaplasia, fragmentation of the extracellular matrix, and neovascularization, all in the absence of significant inflammatory infiltrate. This is why the label "fasciitis" is a misnomer — the condition is a fasciosis.

The plantar fascia originates from the medial process of the calcaneal tuberosity and divides into five digital bands that attach to the proximal phalanges via the plantar plate. The central band bears the bulk of tensile load — roughly 40% of body weight during quiet standing and up to 2.5× body weight during running. This tension is transmitted through the **windlass mechanism**: when the toes dorsiflex during toe-off, the fascia is drawn around the metatarsal heads like a cable around a pulley, raising the arch. In plantar fasciopathy, repetitive microtrauma at the enthesis overwhelms the fibroblast repair capacity. The resulting collagen fibers are **type III** (smaller diameter, less organized) rather than the native **type I**, creating a mechanically weak region at the insertion.

**Pain mechanisms** are incompletely understood. Neovascularization — ingrowth of small blood vessels and accompanying nerve fibers (substance P-positive and CGRP-positive nociceptors) — correlates with pain more strongly than tissue degeneration itself. Doppler ultrasound studies (Walther et al., 2004) found that 88% of symptomatic patients had neovascularization at the enthesis versus 14% of asymptomatic controls. This suggests that the pain is mediated by neoinnervation rather than simple mechanical irritation.

**Biomechanically**, the key loading event is **pronation during the stance phase of gait**. Excessive or prolonged pronation leads to a whipping action of the plantar fascia at its calcaneal attachment during mid-stance, with peak strain occurring just before heel lift. Tightness of the gastrocnemius-soleus complex reduces ankle dorsiflexion, which the foot compensates for by pronating more — creating a vicious cycle.

### Differential diagnosis

1. **Calcaneal stress fracture:** Dull, deep heel pain that worsens with weight-bearing and persists even after warming up. Tenderness over the body of the calcaneus (not the medial tubercle). Positive "squeeze test" (squeezing the heel from both sides reproduces pain). X-ray may show a sclerotic line; MRI shows bone marrow edema.
2. **Tarsal tunnel syndrome (Baxter's nerve entrapment):** Burning or shooting pain, often radiating from the heel along the medial arch. Tinel's sign over the abductor hallucis muscle belly. The first branch of the lateral plantar nerve (Baxter's nerve) is compressed between the deep fascia of the abductor hallucis and the medial head of the quadratus plantae. EMG/NCS can confirm.
3. **Fat pad atrophy:** Attenuation of the heel fat pad, often in older adults or after repeated corticosteroid injections. Pain is deep, central, and felt as "walking on bone." The heel pad feels thin and soft. No pain with toe dorsiflexion. MRI shows thinning of the septal chambers.
4. **Calcaneal apophysitis (Sever's disease):** Heel pain in children aged 8–14, especially active boys. Tenderness at the posterior calcaneus (Achilles insertion), not the plantar surface. The growth plate is the source.
5. **Achilles tendinopathy:** Pain is at the back of the heel, not the bottom. Tender along the Achilles tendon 2–6 cm above the calcaneus.
6. **Plantar fascia rupture:** Acute tearing sensation during a push-off movement (sprinting, jumping). Sudden bruising and swelling along the arch, with a palpable gap in the fascia. Can be mistaken for a sudden improvement in "pain" — actually loss of tension.

### Diagnostic workup

| Test | What it shows | When to order |
|------|---------------|---------------|
| Clinical exam | Tenderness at medial calcaneal tubercle, pain with passive toe dorsiflexion (windlass test), limited ankle dorsiflexion (<10°), palpation over the proximal plantar fascia insertion | Every patient — primary diagnostic tool |
| Windlass test | Passive dorsiflexion of the toes reproduces plantar heel pain. Positive predictive value ~85% | All suspected plantar fasciopathy |
| Silfverskiöld test | Passive ankle dorsiflexion with knee extended (gastrocnemius) vs. flexed (soleus). Differentiates gastrocnemius tightness from soleus tightness or Achilles contracture | Every patient to guide stretching prescription |
| X-ray (weight-bearing, lateral view) | Calcaneal spur (present in ~50% of asymptomatic adults too — incidental, not diagnostic), stress fracture line, heel fat pad integrity | Suspected fracture, unilateral pain, or failed conservative management |
| Ultrasound (high-res, 7–15 MHz) | Thickened plantar fascia (normal 2–4 mm, >4 mm is diagnostic). Hypoechoic regions, loss of fibrillar pattern, neovascularization on Doppler. Gap in rupture cases. | Confirm diagnosis when unclear, guide injection, evaluate rupture |
| MRI | T2 hyperintensity at calcaneal enthesis, fascial thickening, bone marrow edema in calcaneus. Best for differential diagnosis (stress fracture, nerve entrapment, tumor) | When differentials are suspected or pre-surgical planning |
| EMG/NCS | Slowed conduction in medial/lateral plantar nerves. Sensory nerve conduction studies | Suspected tarsal tunnel or Baxter's nerve entrapment |

### Evidence summary

**Incidence and epidemiology:** Plantar fasciopathy is the most common cause of heel pain in adults, with a lifetime prevalence of about 10%. The Rochester Epidemiology Study (Riddle et al., 2003) reported an annual incidence of 1.0 per 1,000 person-years in the general population, with peak rates in the 40–60 age group. Women are affected slightly more than men. Obesity (BMI >30) increases risk 3-fold (odds ratio 3.2, 95% CI 1.8–5.7).

**Stretching:** DiGiovanni et al. (2006) conducted a landmark RCT (n=101) comparing plantar fascia-specific stretching (toe pull-back) to standard Achilles stretching. The specific stretch group had significantly better outcomes: 75% rated treatment as "successful" or "completely successful" at 8 weeks vs. 33% in the Achilles-only group (p<0.001). At 2-year follow-up, 92% of the specific-stretch group reported satisfaction. This is the single best-supported intervention.

**Orthotics:** Landorf et al. (2006) conducted the largest RCT (n=136) comparing custom orthotics to prefabricated orthotics and a sham (thin felt insoles). At 12 months, the custom orthotic group had no statistically significant benefit over sham for pain or function. Both active groups improved similarly. A 2020 Cochrane review (Hawke et al.) confirmed that custom orthotics do not outperform prefabricated orthotics for plantar heel pain.

**Night splints:** A 1999 RCT by Probe et al. (n=47) found that night splints plus stretching improved symptoms more than stretching alone at 6 weeks, but the benefit was not significant at 12 months. Consistent with expert opinion that night splints are most useful in the first 3 months for patients with prominent morning pain (>30 minutes duration).

**Corticosteroid injection:** Provides short-term pain relief (4–12 weeks) superior to placebo in multiple RCTs. However, the risk of plantar fascia rupture is real — a systematic review of 12 studies (McMillan et al., 2012) reported a rupture rate of 2.4% after corticosteroid injection. Rupture rates may be higher with repeated injections (>3) and steroid injected directly into the fascia rather than perifascially.

**Extracorporeal shockwave therapy (ESWT):** The most studied intervention for refractory cases. A 2020 meta-analysis (Sun et al., 2020, n=1,420 from 20 RCTs) found that focused ESWT significantly reduced pain at 12 weeks compared to placebo (SMD −0.54, 95% CI −0.78 to −0.30). Outcomes were better with focused rather than radial ESWT, and with high-energy (>0.2 mJ/mm²) rather than low-energy protocols. Number needed to treat (NNT) for 50% pain reduction: 3–4.

**Platelet-rich plasma (PRP):** Comparative meta-analyses (Franchini et al., 2020; Rayo-Martín et al., 2025) show that PRP is superior to corticosteroid injection for pain reduction and functional outcomes beyond 6–12 months, though not at 4–8 weeks. The mechanism is thought to be mediated by growth factors (PDGF, TGF-β, VEGF) that promote collagen remodeling rather than the immediate anti-inflammatory effect of steroids. Current evidence favors PRP over steroids for chronic (>6 months) cases.

**Dry needling:** A 2017 systematic review (Cotchett et al.) found limited evidence — small RCTs with inconsistent protocols. May help short-term but not enough data for a strong recommendation.

**Surgery:** Plantar fasciotomy (open or endoscopic) — a cohort study by DiGiovanni et al. (2012) of 43 patients undergoing open release showed 82% good-to-excellent outcomes at 5 years. However, iatrogenic flatfoot deformity from complete fasciotomy is a recognized complication (the fascia provides ~15% of arch support) — partial release (medial 30–50%) is preferred. Gastrocnemius recession (Strayer procedure) is increasingly used for recalcitrant cases with confirmed gastrocnemius tightness (Monteagudo et al., 2013) and addresses the root biomechanical driver rather than the enthesis itself.

### Surgical options

Surgery is reserved for the 5–10% of patients who fail 6–12 months of non-operative management.

- **Partial plantar fasciotomy (open or endoscopic):** Release of the medial 30–50% of the fascia at the calcaneal origin. Endoscopic approach allows faster recovery (return to walking at 2–4 weeks vs. 4–6 weeks for open). Success rates 70–90% in carefully selected patients. Risk of arch collapse if too much fascia is released.
- **Gastrocnemius recession (Strayer procedure or Vulpius procedure):** Lengthening of the gastrocnemius aponeurosis to address the most common underlying biomechanical driver — calf tightness. A 2026 systematic review (Pérez González et al., 2026) pooled 11 studies (n=381) and found a 71–93% satisfaction rate in recalcitrant plantar fasciopathy with gastrocnemius recession, with low complication rates (5.2% sural nerve neurapraxia, 1.8% infection). This approach is gaining popularity as an alternative to plantar fasciotomy.
- **Radiofrequency ablation / cryoneurolysis:** Percutaneous ablation of the nerve branches (Baxter's nerve terminal branches) for neurogenic heel pain. Limited evidence, mostly case series.

### Open questions / active research

- What is the optimal dose and frequency of plantar fascia stretching? DiGiovanni's protocol uses 3 × 30-second holds twice daily, but no dose-response trial exists.
- Does neovascularization cause pain or is it a secondary phenomenon? The correlation is strong but causality is unproven. Anti-VEGF agents (bevacizumab) have been tried in tiny case series only.
- Can focused ESWT be combined with stretching for synergistic effect? A 2024 meta-analysis (n=430, 4 RCTs) suggested superiority of combination therapy over monotherapy, but protocols varied widely and heterogeneity was high.
- What is the role of biologics (PRP vs. bone marrow aspirate concentrate vs. amniotic membrane) in refractory cases? Head-to-head trials are lacking.
- Is gastrocnemius recession more cost-effective than prolonged non-operative management in refractory cases? No economic analysis exists.
- Does the presence of a heel spur on X-ray predict treatment response to any specific intervention? Current data says no, but prospective studies with power for subgroup analysis are absent.
- Can high-resolution ultrasound identify which patients will progress to chronic cases? Fascial thickness >4.8 mm at diagnosis predicted >6-month symptom duration in one prospective cohort (Fabrikant & Park, 2011), but validation is pending.
