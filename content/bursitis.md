## ::gist::
### What is bursitis?

Bursitis is inflammation of a **bursa** — a small, fluid-filled sac that cushions your muscles, tendons, and bones near joints. When the bursa gets irritated, it fills with extra fluid and becomes painful, swollen, and tender. It most often happens at the elbow (olecranon), hip (trochanteric), knee (prepatellar), or shoulder (subacromial).

### What to do right now

1. **Rest the joint.** Stop the activity that hurts. If it's your knee or hip, avoid kneeling, squatting, or walking long distances.
2. **Ice it.** Apply an ice pack wrapped in a towel for 15–20 minutes every 2–3 hours. This reduces swelling and pain.
3. **Take an anti-inflammatory.** Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) or naproxen (Aleve) help with both pain and inflammation if you can take them safely.
4. **Protect the area.** Use elbow pads, knee pads, or a cushion when sitting or kneeling. Avoid direct pressure on the sore spot.

::danger::
#### ⚠️ Go to the emergency room if:

- The area is hot, bright red, and you have a fever (could be **septic bursitis** — an infection)
- The swelling came on very fast and the skin is shiny and tight
- You can't move the nearby joint at all
- You have a cut or scrape over the swollen area that looks infected
::end::

::callout::
#### Key fact

Most cases of bursitis are **not infected** and get better with rest, ice, and anti-inflammatories within 1–4 weeks. But septic bursitis — when bacteria get inside the bursa — is a medical emergency that needs antibiotics and possibly drainage. If the area feels hot and you have a fever, don't wait.
::end::

## ::adult::
### What's happening in your body

Your body has about 160 **bursae** — thin, flattened sacs lined with **synovial cells** that produce a small amount of lubricating fluid. Think of a bursa as a tiny fluid-filled pillow that sits between structures that rub against each other: tendon over bone, skin over bone, or muscle over tendon.

The bursa's job is to **reduce friction**. When it becomes inflamed, the synovial lining cells produce more fluid — sometimes dramatically more. A normal bursa contains a few drops of fluid; an inflamed olecranon bursa can hold 10–20 mL or more. The fluid distends the bursal sac, causing the characteristic soft, ballotable swelling that can feel like a water balloon under the skin.

The most common sites and what causes them:

- **Olecranon bursitis** (elbow): Leaning on your elbow for long periods, repetitive pressure from desk work, or a direct blow. Also called "student's elbow" or "draftsman's elbow."
- **Prepatellar bursitis** (knee): Frequent kneeling — flooring, gardening, roofing. Known as "housemaid's knee" or "carpet layer's knee."
- **Trochanteric bursitis** (hip): Overuse from running, lying on one side in bed, or IT band friction. More common in middle-aged women and runners.
- **Subacromial/subdeltoid bursitis** (shoulder): Repetitive overhead activities — painting, swimming, throwing. Often part of **subacromial pain syndrome** (impingement).
- **Retrocalcaneal bursitis** (heel): Between the Achilles tendon and heel bone. Caused by running, jumping, or poorly fitting shoes.
- **Ischial bursitis** (buttock): Sitting on hard surfaces for long periods. Called "weaver's bottom."

### Symptoms

- **Swelling:** soft, fluctuant mass over the bursa. In olecranon bursitis, you can see a visible "goose egg" on the back of the elbow.
- **Pain:** dull ache that worsens with direct pressure or moving the joint through its range. Trochanteric bursitis hurts when lying on the affected side. Subacromial bursitis hurts with overhead reaching.
- **Tenderness:** touching the bursa directly is painful. But the joint itself is usually not tender — this helps distinguish bursitis from arthritis.
- **Warmth:** the skin over the bursa may feel warm, especially if there's inflammation or infection.
- **Stiffness:** you may not want to move the joint fully because it hurts at the end ranges.

### Treatment

- **Conservative care (first-line):** Rest from the aggravating activity, ice packs (15–20 min, 2–3 times daily), and NSAIDs (ibuprofen 600–800 mg tid or naproxen 500 mg bid for 5–7 days). Most cases respond to this alone.
- **Padding and protection:** Elbow sleeves, knee pads, gel cushions, or donut-shaped pads relieve direct pressure. Trochanteric bursitis: sleep with a pillow between your knees to prevent side-lying compression.
- **Activity modification:** Avoid the specific motion that triggers pain. For subacromial bursitis, avoid reaching overhead or behind the back. For trochanteric, cut back on running mileage and hill work.
- **Physiotherapy:** Strengthening of surrounding muscles reduces load on the bursa. For subacromial: rotator cuff and scapular stabilizer exercises. For trochanteric: hip abductor and gluteal strengthening.
- **Corticosteroid injection:** For persistent cases that don't respond to 2–4 weeks of conservative care. A mixture of local anaesthetic and corticosteroid (e.g., 1 mL lidocaine 1% + 40 mg methylprednisolone) injected into the bursal space. Provides relief in 48–72 hours, lasting weeks to months. Studies show 60–80% short-term efficacy for trochanteric bursitis. Limit to 2–3 injections per year — repeated steroid injections weaken local tissues.

::callout::
#### What NOT to do

- Don't keep doing the activity that caused it — resting the bursa is the treatment, not optional.
- Don't try to drain the bursa yourself with a needle. This can introduce bacteria and cause septic bursitis.
- Don't apply heat — it increases blood flow and inflammation. Use ice instead.
- Don't ignore signs of infection (redness spreading, fever, chills, increasing pain). Septic bursitis can progress quickly.
- Don't get more than 3 corticosteroid injections in the same bursa per year — risk of skin atrophy, tendon rupture, and infection increases with each injection.
- Don't confuse bursitis with tendinopathy. They can occur together, but treatment differs. Tendinopathy needs eccentric loading exercises, not just rest.
::end::

## ::timeline::
- time: Day 1–3
  text: Acute phase. Rest the joint, ice, and NSAIDs. Avoid direct pressure. Swelling may increase before it improves. Most people notice improvement within 48 hours of resting.
- time: Day 4–14
  text: Pain and swelling gradually subside. Start gentle range-of-motion exercises. If aseptic (non-infected), most cases resolve. If symptoms persist beyond 2 weeks, consider physiotherapy or a corticosteroid injection.
- time: Week 2–4
  text: Return to normal activities gradually. For trochanteric and subacromial bursitis, begin targeted strengthening exercises. Continue activity modification. If not improved by week 4, seek medical evaluation.
- time: Week 4–8
  text: If still symptomatic: consider imaging (ultrasound or MRI) to rule out tears, tendinopathy, or other pathology. Corticosteroid injection if not already done. Physiotherapy referral.
- time: 3+ months
  text: Chronic bursitis: consider aspiration, aspiration + injection, or (rarely) bursectomy. Evaluate for underlying causes (gout, RA, infection, overuse syndrome). If recurrent, address ergonomics and biomechanics.

## ::full::
### Pathophysiology

A bursa is a synovium-lined cavity containing a thin film of **hyaluronan-rich fluid** that functions as a **low-friction gliding surface** between musculoskeletal planes. Bursae are classified as **adventitious** (develop at sites of mechanical friction), **subcutaneous** (between skin and bone — e.g., prepatellar, olecranon), **subfascial** (beneath fascia), **subtendinous** (beneath tendons — e.g., subacromial), and **submuscular** (beneath muscles — e.g., trochanteric).

**Aseptic bursitis** results from repetitive mechanical trauma or excessive pressure. The initiating event is microtearing of the synovial lining and the underlying connective tissue. This triggers an **acute inflammatory cascade**: damaged cells release **prostaglandins (especially PGE₂), bradykinin, and histamine**, causing vasodilation and increased capillary permeability. Synovial fibroblasts respond by secreting **pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-6)**. The synovial lining cells proliferate (synovial hyperplasia) and increase fluid production. The accumulated fluid is typically **serous or serosanguinous** in aseptic cases, with a low white cell count (<1,000 cells/μL, predominantly mononuclear).

Histologically, the bursal wall shows **synovial hypertrophy**, **villous formation**, and **chronic inflammatory infiltrate** (lymphocytes, plasma cells, occasional neutrophils). In chronic cases, the bursal wall becomes fibrotic and thickened, and the fluid becomes more viscous.

**Septic bursitis** occurs when bacteria penetrate the bursa, most commonly through a break in the overlying skin (abrasion, laceration, puncture), or via haematogenous spread. The most common pathogen is ***Staphylococcus aureus*** (80–90% of cases), followed by **streptococci** (Group A, Group B). Less common: **coagulase-negative staphylococci**, ***Pseudomonas*** (immunocompromised, IV drug users), and **anaerobes**.

The infected bursa fills with **purulent fluid** — neutrophil-dominated (>10,000 cells/μL), with high protein content. The inflammatory response is amplified through **TLR2/NLRP3 signaling** in response to bacterial cell wall components. If untreated, the infection can spread to adjacent tissues causing **cellulitis**, **septic arthritis** (if the bursa communicates with the joint — particularly the subacromial bursa, which can communicate with the glenohumeral joint in ~15% of people), or **systemic sepsis**. The pre-patellar and olecranon bursae are most commonly infected because they are superficial and vulnerable to minor trauma.

**Crystal-induced bursitis** occurs when monosodium urate (gout) or calcium pyrophosphate (pseudogout) crystals precipitate within the bursal fluid, triggering the same **NLRP3 inflammasome–IL-1β pathway** as in crystal arthropathies of joints. The olecranon bursa is a classic site for gouty bursitis.

### Differential diagnosis

1. **Septic bursitis:** The critical distinction. Redness spreading beyond the bursal margin, systemic symptoms (fever, chills, malaise), aspirate with high WCC (>10,000 cells/μL) and positive Gram stain/culture. Must be ruled out before injecting corticosteroids.
2. **Cellulitis:** Diffuse skin infection without the well-defined fluctuant swelling of bursitis. Look for portal of entry (wound, insect bite).
3. **Tendinopathy/tendon tear:** Pain localized to the tendon, not the bursal space. Pain with resisted movement (e.g., resisted wrist extension in lateral epicondylitis) rather than compression.
4. **Arthritis (inflammatory, septic, or crystal):** Joint-line tenderness, limited passive range of motion in the joint itself, effusion. Bursitis usually preserves full passive range of the adjacent joint.
5. **Fracture (stress or acute):** Bone tenderness, history of trauma, inability to bear weight. X-ray distinguishes.
6. **Rheumatoid nodule or tophus:** Firm, not fluctuant. Aspiration shows characteristic cells (rheumatoid) or crystals (gout).
7. **Lipoma or soft-tissue tumour:** Firm, slow-growing, not acutely painful or warm. Ultrasound or MRI distinguishes.
8. **Venous thrombosis (rare mimic):** In prepatellar or olecranon region, a thrombosed vein can mimic bursal swelling. Doppler ultrasound differentiates.

| Bursal aspiration (arthrocentesis) | Gross appearance: serous (aseptic), serosanguinous (traumatic), purulent (septic). | Ultrasound (high-resolution) | Bursal distension, wall thickening, septations, fluid characteristics (anechoic = simple, echogenic = complex/septic). | X-ray (plain radiograph) | Soft tissue swelling, calcification (calcific bursitis), underlying fracture, or joint pathology | Trauma, chronic symptoms, or suspected underlying bone lesion |

### Evidence summary

**Epidemiology:** Bursitis is one of the most common causes of musculoskeletal pain in primary care. Trochanteric bursitis (greater trochanteric pain syndrome, GTPS) affects 10–25% of the general population at some point, with a female predominance (4:1) and peak incidence at age 40–60. Olecranon bursitis accounts for approximately 0.1% of emergency department visits and is more common in men (occupational kneeling, sports). Subacromial bursitis is present in 40–60% of patients presenting with shoulder pain (associated with subacromial impingement syndrome).

**Septic vs. aseptic:** The landmark retrospective review by Garcia-Porrua et al. (*Semin Arthritis Rheum* 1999;28(6):417-425) found that 30–40% of olecranon bursitis cases presenting to emergency departments are septic. Fever, skin temperature >1.5°C above the contralateral side, and surrounding cellulitis are strong predictors of infection (positive LR ~6.0). Aspiration and culture remain the diagnostic gold standard. The prospective study by Stell et al. (*J Accid Emerg Med* 1996;13(1):47-49) found that clinical assessment alone has limited accuracy — aspiration is recommended when infection is even mildly suspected.

**Conservative treatment:** A Cochrane review on corticosteroid injections for shoulder pain (subacromial bursitis) by Buchbinder et al. (2003, updated 2014) found subacromial corticosteroid injections provided modest short-term benefit (4–6 weeks) compared to placebo, with no advantage over physiotherapy at 6–12 months. No high-quality RCT specifically compares rest + ice + NSAIDs to injection for acute bursitis — the evidence for conservative care is extrapolated from clinical experience and observational studies.

**Corticosteroid injections for trochanteric bursitis:** A randomized trial by Brinks et al. (*Rheumatology* 2011;50(9):1633-1642) found corticosteroid injection was superior to placebo at 1 month (NNT 3) but the benefit was lost by 3 months, with no difference at 6 or 12 months. The systematic review by Slawaska-Eng et al. (*J ISAKOS* 2025;11:100846) assessed the fragility index of RCTs for GTPS and found most trials were underpowered, with fragility indices of 2–3 (meaning adding 2–3 event reversals could change the significance of results).

**PRP for trochanteric bursitis:** A systematic review by Ahmed et al. (*Cureus* 2024;16(10):e72597) of 6 studies found limited evidence to support platelet-rich plasma injections for GTPS. Two of six studies reported statistically significant improvement in pain scores compared to control, but overall quality was low and protocols varied widely.

**Septic bursitis management:** A 12-year retrospective analysis by El Zein et al. (*J Bone Jt Infect* 2024;9(2):107-115; n=1,043) found that antibiotic durations of 7–14 days after surgical drainage for septic olecranon bursitis were sufficient, with no increased risk of recurrence compared to longer courses. The randomized trial by Uçkay et al. (*Mayo Clin Proc* 2017;92(7):1061-1069; n=60) compared one-stage vs. two-stage bursectomy for septic olecranon and prepatellar bursitis, finding that a single-stage procedure (bursectomy + primary closure + antibiotics) had similar cure rates (93% vs. 97%) with shorter hospital stays.

**Surgery for aseptic bursitis:** Evidence is limited to retrospective case series. A series by Quayle et al. (*J Shoulder Elbow Surg* 2013;22(3):420-425) on subacromial bursectomy found that 85% of patients reported good-to-excellent outcomes, but fusion of the subacromial bursa (bursal-side acromioplasty vs. bursectomy alone) showed no difference in outcomes. Huang et al. (*Orthopedics* 2011;34(12):e870-874) reported a 90% success rate for open bursectomy in chronic, recalcitrant olecranon bursitis.

### Surgical options (if indicated)

Surgery is rarely needed for bursitis and is reserved for chronic, recalcitrant cases that have failed conservative management for 3–6 months:

- **Bursal aspiration (therapeutic):** Simple needle drainage of fluid can provide immediate relief of pressure symptoms. Often combined with corticosteroid injection (if septic bursitis has been excluded). Recurrence rate after aspiration alone is ~50%, lower with concomitant injection.
- **Open bursectomy (excision):** Complete excision of the bursal sac. Indicated for: chronic, symptomatic bursitis unresponsive to ≥3 months of conservative therapy; recurrent septic bursitis; or suspicion of underlying pathology (neoplasm, chronic infection). Healing time: 2–4 weeks. Complications: wound dehiscence (up to 10% in olecranon), seroma formation, hematoma, scar sensitivity.
- **Endoscopic/arthroscopic bursectomy:** For subacromial bursitis, arthroscopic bursectomy is performed as part of subacromial decompression. Smaller incisions, faster recovery. The subacromial bursa is resected using a shaver, and any bony impingement is addressed with acromioplasty if present.
- **Septic bursitis — incision and drainage:** For infected bursitis that fails to respond to medical therapy (antibiotics + serial aspiration) or presents with extensive necrosis. El Zein et al. (2024) recommend formal I&D with primary closure and 7–14 days of directed antibiotics. Uçkay et al. (2017) showed single-stage bursectomy is as effective as two-stage with shorter hospital stay.

### Open questions / active research

- Can ultrasound-guided corticosteroid injection improve outcomes over landmark-guided injection for deep bursae? Existing studies show mixed results; a large multicentre RCT (NCT05628727) is ongoing.
- What is the optimal number of corticosteroid injections before considering surgery? Practice varies from 2–4, with no evidence base.
- Does subacromial bursectomy add benefit to acromioplasty alone in subacromial impingement? The current trend is toward bursectomy-only (no acromioplasty) for isolated bursitis, but comparative data are sparse.
- Can PRP or other biologics (amniotic membrane, hyaluronic acid) outperform corticosteroids for chronic bursitis? Current evidence is insufficient for recommendation.
- What is the role of the microbiome in septic vs. aseptic bursitis? Recent 16S sequencing studies have identified bacterial DNA in "aseptic" bursal fluid, raising questions about subclinical infection as a driver of chronic inflammation.
- Can focused shock wave therapy benefit chronic calcific bursitis of the shoulder? Preliminary data suggest symptom improvement, but RCTs comparing to sham are needed.
