## ::gist::
### What is gout?

Gout is a form of arthritis where tiny needle-shaped uric acid crystals build up in your joints — most often the big toe — causing sudden, intense pain, redness, and swelling. It affects about 4% of adults and is linked to diet, genetics, and how your body handles uric acid.

### What to do right now

1. **Stop and rest the joint.** Don't walk on it if it hurts. Any movement of the affected joint will make the pain worse.
2. **Ice it.** Wrap ice in a towel and hold it on the joint for 15–20 minutes every 2–3 hours. This helps with swelling and pain.
3. **Take an anti-inflammatory.** Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) or naproxen (Aleve) work well if you can take them safely. Higher doses work better — follow the package directions for acute pain.
4. **Elevate the joint.** Prop your foot up on pillows to help drain swelling.
5. **Call your doctor.** You may need a prescription for stronger medication (colchicine or corticosteroids) to stop the flare quickly.

::danger::
#### ⚠️ Go to the emergency room if:

- You have a fever with joint pain and swelling (could be a joint infection — septic arthritis)
- The joint is hot, red, and you can't move it at all
- The pain is unbearable despite rest and over-the-counter medication
- You have multiple joints affected at the same time with fever
- You're on chemotherapy and develop sudden joint pain (tumor lysis syndrome)
::end::

::callout::
#### Key fact

Gout flares usually peak within 12–24 hours and begin to settle on their own within 3–10 days — even without treatment. But untreated gout can lead to permanent joint damage and hard lumps called tophi. The good news: gout is one of the most treatable forms of arthritis. Once you get the right diagnosis, you can stop flares and prevent long-term damage.
::end::

## ::adult::
### What's happening in your body

Gout is caused by **hyperuricemia** — too much uric acid in your blood. Uric acid is a normal waste product when your body breaks down **purines** (chemical compounds found in many foods and also produced naturally by your cells). Normally, your kidneys filter uric acid out through urine. But if you produce too much or your kidneys can't excrete enough, uric acid builds up.

When blood uric acid levels stay above about 6.8 mg/dL for long enough, the uric acid can crystallize — forming microscopic, needle-shaped **monosodium urate (MSU) crystals**. These crystals are like tiny shards of glass in your joint. Your immune system recognizes them as foreign and launches an all-out inflammatory attack.

The big toe joint (first metatarsophalangeal joint) is the classic site — this is called **podagra** (Greek for "foot trap"). Why the toe? It's the coolest part of the body (lower temperature makes crystals more likely to form) and it bears the most weight and pressure. But gout can affect the ankle, knee, wrist, elbow, and fingers too.

About half of first flares happen in the big toe. Other common sites include the midfoot (tarsal joints), ankle, and knee.

### Symptoms

- **Sudden onset:** Gout is famous for waking people up in the middle of the night with excruciating pain. The joint goes from fine to agony in 6–12 hours.
- **Intense pain:** Often described as "someone put a hot poker in my joint" or "like broken glass grinding." Even the weight of a bedsheet on the toe is unbearable.
- **Redness and swelling:** The joint looks inflamed — bright red, shiny, swollen, and warm to the touch. The redness can look like cellulitis (a skin infection).
- **Peak at 12–24 hours:** The pain is worst about 12–24 hours after it starts, then gradually improves over 3–10 days.
- **Desquamation (peeling skin):** As the flare resolves, the skin over the joint may peel — a classic sign that it was gout.

### Treatment

- **NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, indomethacin):** First-line treatment for acute flares. Take at full anti-inflammatory doses (e.g., naproxen 500 mg twice daily, ibuprofen 800 mg three times daily) but only for a few days. Start as soon as you feel a flare coming on. A Cochrane review found strong evidence that NSAIDs are effective for acute gout pain.
- **Colchicine:** A prescription medication that works best if taken within 12–24 hours of flare onset. It blocks microtubule assembly in inflammatory cells, stopping the immune response. The modern dosing is much safer: 1.2 mg (two 0.6 mg tablets) at the first sign, then 0.6 mg one hour later. Don't take more — higher doses cause severe diarrhea and vomiting without better pain relief. Avoid or reduce dose if you have kidney disease or take certain other medications.
- **Corticosteroids:** Prednisone (oral) or corticosteroid injections into the joint. Used when NSAIDs and colchicine aren't safe (e.g., kidney disease, blood thinners) or don't work. Oral prednisone 30–35 mg daily for 5 days works as well as NSAIDs.
- **Urate-lowering therapy (ULT):** For people with recurrent flares (2+ per year), tophi, or joint damage. **Allopurinol** is the first choice — it reduces uric acid production. Start at 100 mg daily and increase slowly over weeks to months, targeting serum urate below 6.0 mg/dL (or below 5.0 mg/dL if tophi are present). **Febuxostat** is an alternative. Starting ULT during a flare is now recommended (it doesn't prolong the flare), and all patients starting ULT should also have flare prophylaxis (low-dose colchicine or NSAIDs) for 3–6 months.
- **Lifestyle:** Weight loss if overweight, limit purine-rich foods (red meat, organ meats, shellfish, beer), avoid sugary drinks (especially those sweetened with high-fructose corn syrup — fructose directly raises uric acid), and stay well hydrated with water.

::callout::
#### What NOT to do

- Don't take low-dose aspirin every day if you have gout — it raises serum uric acid levels and can trigger flares. If you need aspirin for heart protection, talk to your doctor about alternatives.
- Don't drink beer or spirits during a flare — alcohol both dehydrates you and raises uric acid. Wine is less problematic but still best avoided during flares.
- Don't use a heating pad on the joint — heat increases inflammation and makes it worse. Use ice instead.
- Don't suddenly stop your urate-lowering medication — rapid changes in uric acid levels can trigger a flare. This is why ULT is started slowly and increased gradually.
- Don't eat high-purine foods during a flare — save the steak and shrimp for when the joint has settled.
- Don't assume it's "just a sprain" — if you have recurrent episodes of a hot, swollen big toe with no clear injury, it's almost certainly gout, not an injury.
::end::

- time: Week 2+
  text: Joint returns to normal. Now is the time to see your doctor for long-term planning. If this was your first flare and uric acid is very high, discuss starting urate-lowering therapy. If you've had multiple flares, you likely need ULT.

## ::full::
### Pathophysiology

Gout is a **crystal-deposition arthropathy** caused by the precipitation of **monosodium urate (MSU) monohydrate crystals** in synovial fluid and periarticular tissues. The sine qua non of gout is **hyperuricemia** — serum urate concentration exceeding the solubility threshold of ~6.8 mg/dL (~404 μmol/L) at physiological pH and 37°C.

**Uric acid metabolism.** Uric acid is the terminal oxidation product of **purine metabolism** in humans (and other primates lacking the enzyme **uricase**, which degrades urate to allantoin in most mammals). Purines originate from three sources: dietary intake (red meat, organ meats, shellfish), endogenous nucleotide turnover, and de novo purine synthesis. Two enzymes — **hypoxanthine-guanine phosphoribosyltransferase (HGPT)** and **phosphoribosylpyrophosphate (PRPP) synthetase** — are key regulators; their dysfunction causes inherited forms of hyperuricemia (Lesch-Nyhan syndrome, HGPT deficiency).

Two-thirds of uric acid excretion is renal; one-third is gastrointestinal (gut bacteria degrade urate). In most people with gout, **renal urate underexcretion** is the dominant mechanism (80–90% of cases), driven by impaired function or reduced expression of urate transporters in the proximal tubule — particularly **URAT1 (SLC22A12)** and **GLUT9 (SLC2A9)** which reabsorb urate, and **ABCG2** and **OAT1/OAT3** which secrete it. Genetic variants in these transporters are strongly associated with hyperuricemia. A minority (10–20%) have **urate overproduction**, often dietary or due to high cell turnover (myeloproliferative disorders, psoriasis, chemotherapy).

**Crystal formation and the inflammatory cascade.** When supersaturation persists, MSU crystals nucleate in the joint — favoured by cooler temperature (the distal extremities are ~30–33°C, below the solubility threshold), low pH, low proteoglycan concentration, and repeated joint microtrauma. The crystals are needle-shaped, strongly **negatively birefringent** under polarized light (a key diagnostic feature; they appear yellow when aligned parallel to the compensator axis).

The crystal triggers an innate immune response via two main pathways:

1. **NLRP3 inflammasome activation.** MSU crystals are phagocytosed by joint-resident macrophages. Inside the phagolysosome, the crystals cause lysosomal rupture, releasing cathepsin B and reactive oxygen species. This activates the **NLRP3 (NALP3) inflammasome** — a multiprotein complex that cleaves pro-caspase-1 to active caspase-1, which in turn cleaves pro-IL-1β to active **interleukin-1β (IL-1β)**. This is the central molecular event in acute gout (Martinon et al., *Nature* 2006).

2. **IL-1β–driven amplification.** IL-1β binds to the IL-1 receptor on synovial fibroblasts and endothelial cells, triggering NF-κB–dependent transcription of chemokines (CXCL8/IL-8, CCL2/MCP-1) and adhesion molecules (E-selectin, ICAM-1). This recruits massive numbers of **neutrophils** into the joint space — the hallmark of an acute gout flare. Neutrophils phagocytose MSU crystals and release proteases, reactive oxygen species, and additional pro-inflammatory mediators, amplifying tissue damage and pain.

The flare is self-limiting — within 7–10 days, the inflammatory response resolves through several mechanisms: neutrophil extracellular trap (NET) formation, macrophage switching from pro-inflammatory (M1) to anti-inflammatory (M2) phenotype, and crystal coating by apolipoprotein B which reduces surface reactivity.

**Tophi and chronic gout.** If hyperuricemia persists for years, MSU crystals aggregate into macroscopic deposits called **tophi** — chalky white, nodular collections of crystalline urate surrounded by a foreign-body granulomatous response (giant cells, epithelioid histiocytes, fibrosis). Tophi erode bone, destroy cartilage, and can cause chronic pain, deformity, and functional impairment. They can also deposit in soft tissues (olecranon bursa, Achilles tendon, ear helix) and rarely in organs (kidney, heart valves).

**Renal implications.** Chronic hyperuricemia can cause **urate nephropathy** (crystal deposition in the medullary interstitium) and **urolithiasis** (uric acid kidney stones, accounting for 10–15% of all kidney stones). Acute urate nephropathy occurs in tumor lysis syndrome.

### Differential diagnosis

1. **Septic arthritis:** The critical mimic. A hot, swollen, red joint with fever, inability to bear weight, and systemic illness. Requires urgent joint aspiration — if the Gram stain shows bacteria and culture is positive, it's septic until proven otherwise. Gout and septic arthritis can coexist (rare but reported). Never assume a hot swollen joint is gout without aspiration, especially in hospitalized or immunocompromised patients.
2. **Pseudogout (calcium pyrophosphate deposition disease, CPPD):** Presents identically to acute gout but affects larger joints (knee > wrist > shoulder). Crystals are rhomboid-shaped and weakly positively birefringent (blue when parallel to the compensator). Can coexist with gout. Aspiration and crystal analysis are the only way to distinguish.
3. **Cellulitis:** Diffuse skin infection with redness, warmth, and swelling that extends beyond the joint. Key difference: cellulitis doesn't cause severe pain with passive range of motion, and the redness is more diffuse and spreading.
4. **Acute inflammatory arthritis (reactive, psoriatic, palindromic rheumatism):** These can present with monoarticular flares. Clues against gout: involvement of atypical joints (sacroiliac, DIPs), skin changes (psoriasis, nail pitting), preceding infection (reactive), and absence of typical crystal findings.
5. **Trauma / stress fracture:** History of injury and X-ray findings usually distinguish. But acute gout can be triggered by joint trauma — don't be misled by a history of a minor bump.
6. **Osteomyelitis:** Bone infection with localized pain, fever, and elevated inflammatory markers. MRI or bone scan can differentiate.
7. **Rheumatoid arthritis (palindromic phase):** Palindromic RA presents as episodic monoarticular flares lasting days. Clues: symmetrical involvement over time, positive rheumatoid factor/anti-CCP, and absence of crystals.

### Diagnostic workup

|------|---------------|---------------|
| Joint aspiration (arthrocentesis) — the gold standard | Negatively birefringent needle-shaped MSU crystals under polarized light microscopy. Also rules out septic arthritis via Gram stain, culture, and cell count | Any undiagnosed acute monoarthritis — essential to confirm gout and exclude infection |
| Serum uric acid | Hyperuricemia >6.8 mg/dL. However, during an acute flare, serum urate may be normal (false negative in up to 25% of flares due to acute-phase uricosuria). A normal level during a flare does NOT rule out gout | Every patient with suspected gout; follow serially during ULT |
| 24-hour urinary uric acid | >800 mg/day indicates overproduction phenotype. <800 mg/day suggests underexcretion. Helps guide ULT choice | Recurrent gout, young-onset (<25), family history, or tophaceous gout to classify phenotype |
| X-ray (plain radiograph) | Early: normal or mild soft tissue swelling. Late: well-defined erosions with overhanging edges (rat-bite lesions), punched-out lytic lesions, tophaceous soft tissue masses, joint space preservation until late stages | Chronic gout, suspected joint damage, tophaceous disease |
| Dual-energy CT (DECT) | Color-coded mapping of MSU crystal deposits (green) vs. calcium (white). Sensitivity ~85%, specificity ~90% for gout. Can detect subclinical crystal deposition | Atypical presentation, crystal-negative by aspiration, tophus characterization, pre-surgical planning |
| Ultrasound (musculoskeletal) | **Double-contour sign:** hyperechoic linear deposition of MSU crystals on the superficial margin of hyaline cartilage — a specific sign of gout (sensitivity 69–95%, specificity 76–91%). Also shows tophi as hyperechoic aggregates with posterior acoustic shadowing and erosions | First-line imaging for suspected gout when available; useful for monitoring crystal load during ULT |
| Serum creatinine / eGFR | Assesses renal function — critical for ULT dosing (allopurinol dose adjustment in CKD) and colchicine safety | All patients before starting ULT |
| Inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) | Elevated during acute flares but non-specific. Normalizes between flares | To monitor response to treatment; helps distinguish from infection if mild elevation |

### Evidence summary

**Epidemiology:** Gout is the most common inflammatory arthritis in adults, with a global prevalence of 1–4% (Kuo et al., *Nat Rev Rheumatol* 2015). Prevalence is higher in men (3–6%) than women (1–2%), increases with age, and is rising worldwide due to dietary changes, obesity, and increased longevity. The incidence in men peaks at age 40–50 and in women after menopause (when oestrogen's uricosuric effect drops). In the US, prevalence exceeds 8.3 million adults (NHANES 2007–2008).

**Diagnostic gold standard:** Joint aspiration with polarized light microscopy remains the diagnostic standard. The 2020 ACR guideline strongly recommends synovial fluid analysis for definitive diagnosis (FitzGerald et al., *Arthritis Care Res* 2020). DECT and ultrasound have emerged as valuable non-invasive alternatives, though neither replaces crystal confirmation in uncertain cases.

**Acute treatment:** The Cochrane review by van Durme et al. (2021) found high-certainty evidence that NSAIDs provide effective pain relief for acute gout. The Cochrane review by McKenzie et al. (2021) confirmed that colchicine reduces pain at 24–36 hours compared to placebo but has a higher rate of gastrointestinal adverse effects (diarrhoea in up to 40% with high-dose regimens). The 2016 EULAR guidelines (Richette et al., *Ann Rheum Dis* 2017) recommend initiating treatment within 12–24 hours of flare onset, with NSAIDs or colchicine as first-line therapy and intra-articular corticosteroids as an alternative.

**Urate-lowering therapy:** Allopurinol is the first-line ULT. The 2020 ACR guideline recommends starting allopurinol at low dose (≤100 mg daily, with dose adjustment in CKD) and titrating to a serum urate target of <6.0 mg/dL (<5.0 mg/dL if tophaceous). The CARES trial (White et al., *NEJM* 2018) compared febuxostat with allopurinol in 6,190 patients with gout and established CVD; febuxostat was non-inferior for the primary composite cardiovascular endpoint but was associated with higher all-cause and cardiovascular mortality — leading to an FDA black-box warning for febuxostat. The subsequent FAST trial (NCT01994798) found no significant difference in cardiovascular outcomes between febuxostat and allopurinol, but allopurinol remains first-line per ACR/EULAR guidelines.

**Dietary management:** Yokose et al. (*Diabetes Care* 2020) found that a Mediterranean-style low-carbohydrate diet was associated with lower serum urate levels than a low-fat diet. Wang et al. (*Am J Clin Nutr* 2025) validated an empirical dietary index for lower urate concentrations. The 2020 ACR guideline recommends dietary counselling emphasizing limitation of high-purine foods (red meat, organ meat, shellfish), alcohol (especially beer and spirits), and sugar-sweetened beverages, while encouraging coffee (moderate protective effect), dairy (mild uricosuric), and vitamin C supplements (modest urate-lowering, ~0.5 mg/dL).

**Clinical practice guidelines:** Conley et al. (*BMC Rheumatol* 2023) conducted a systematic review of clinical practice guidelines for gout and found consistent recommendations across ACR, EULAR, and the British Society for Rheumatology: treat-to-target serum urate, first-line allopurinol with appropriate dosing, flare prophylaxis during ULT initiation, and joint aspiration for definitive diagnosis.

### Surgical options

Surgery is rarely needed for gout and is reserved for complications of chronic tophaceous disease:

- **Tophus excision:** Indicated for tophi causing nerve compression (carpal tunnel, ulnar nerve), joint deformity, functional impairment, skin ulceration with sinus tract formation, or persistent infection. Simple excision is usually sufficient.
- **Joint debridement and synovectomy:** For chronic gouty arthropathy with severe synovitis and joint destruction. Urate crystal-laden synovium is removed to reduce pain and improve function.
- **Arthrodesis (joint fusion):** For end-stage destruction of small joints (toe, finger, wrist), where joint replacement is not feasible. Fusion provides pain relief at the cost of range of motion.
- **Arthroplasty (joint replacement):** Rarely indicated. Hip or knee replacement may be considered for advanced gouty destruction of large joints.

All surgical patients should have serum urate controlled to target before elective surgery to reduce the risk of post-operative gout flares (triggered by perioperative uric acid fluctuations).

### Open questions / active research

- Can aggressive urate-lowering (target <5.0 mg/dL) dissolve established tophi faster than standard targets? The CRYSTAL dissolution study is ongoing.
- What is the optimal duration of flare prophylaxis when starting ULT? Current guidelines recommend 3–6 months, but individual risk stratification data are lacking.
- Why does the first MTP joint have such a high predilection? Lower temperature, higher mechanical stress, and local pH are implicated, but the exact molecular trigger for site-specific crystal nucleation is unknown.
- Can IL-1β inhibitors (canakinumab, anakinra) be used more broadly for acute gout, especially in patients with contraindications to NSAIDs/colchicine/steroids? Currently cost and access are limiting factors.
- Does lowering serum urate reduce cardiovascular risk independently of gout flares? Observational data suggest benefit, but no large randomized trial has shown this definitively.
- What is the role of the gut microbiome in urate metabolism and gout pathogenesis? Emerging data show that certain gut bacteria degrade urate, and dysbiosis is associated with hyperuricemia.
