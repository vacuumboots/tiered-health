## ::gist::
### What is frozen shoulder?

The capsule around your shoulder joint has become tight and inflamed, making it painful and difficult to move your arm. It happens in three phases — freezing (painful), frozen (stiff), and thawing (gradual recovery) — and usually gets better on its own, but it can take 1 to 3 years.

### What to do right now

1. **Keep moving it gently.** Don't stop using your arm entirely — gentle pendulum swings and wall climbs within your pain-free range help maintain motion.
2. **Take ibuprofen or acetaminophen** for pain and inflammation, if you can take them safely.
3. **Apply heat before stretching.** A warm shower or heating pad for 10 minutes before gentle stretching helps loosen the joint.
4. **See your doctor.** A steroid injection into the shoulder can reduce pain and speed up recovery, especially early on.
5. **Be patient.** This condition takes months to resolve. Most people recover fully without surgery.

::danger::
#### ⚠️ Go to the emergency room if:

- You fell on your shoulder and can't lift your arm at all
- Your shoulder is hot, red, and you have a fever (could be an infection — septic arthritis)
- The pain came on suddenly after an injury and your arm looks deformed
- You have severe pain that isn't helped by over-the-counter medications
::end::

::callout::
#### Key fact

Frozen shoulder affects about 2–5% of the population, most commonly people between 40 and 60 years old. It is more common in women and in people with diabetes — about 10–20% of people with diabetes will develop frozen shoulder at some point. The good news: even without treatment, the condition eventually resolves for almost everyone. Physical therapy and injections can help it resolve faster.
::end::

## ::adult::
### What's happening in your body

Your shoulder is a **ball-and-socket joint** (the glenohumeral joint). The round head of the **humerus** (upper arm bone) sits in a shallow dish on the **scapula** called the **glenoid**. Around the joint, a thin, flexible capsule of connective tissue — the **joint capsule** — wraps everything together, lined with a thin membrane (the **synovium**) that produces lubricating fluid.

In frozen shoulder, something triggers an inflammatory reaction in the synovium. The capsule becomes inflamed and thickened. Over time, bands of scar tissue (**adhesions**) form inside the capsule, and the capsule itself shrinks and tightens — like a shrink-wrap around the joint. This **capsular contracture** is what limits your range of motion.

The condition progresses through three distinct phases:

1. **Freezing phase (pain-predominant):** The lining of the capsule is inflamed. Any movement hurts. Pain is often worse at night and can wake you from sleep. This phase lasts 2 to 9 months.

2. **Frozen phase (stiffness-predominant):** The inflammation settles but the capsule has contracted. Your shoulder feels tight and stiff. You can't reach overhead, behind your back, or out to the side. Pain is less severe but the stiffness is frustrating. This phase lasts 4 to 12 months.

3. **Thawing phase (recovery):** The capsule gradually loosens and range of motion slowly returns. This is the longest phase — 5 to 24 months. Most people regain near-full motion, though some have mild residual stiffness.

Not everyone with shoulder stiffness has frozen shoulder. The key sign is that **both you and the doctor** can't move the shoulder much — both active and passive range of motion are restricted. If only active motion is limited (you can't lift it but the doctor can), the problem is likely a torn rotator cuff, not frozen shoulder.

### Symptoms

- **Pain:** Dull, aching pain deep in the shoulder, often worse at night. Lying on the affected side is painful.
- **Stiffness:** Difficulty reaching overhead (putting on a shirt, reaching a shelf), behind your back (fastening a bra, washing your back), or across your body (putting on a seatbelt).
- **Progressive loss of motion:** The range decreases gradually over weeks to months. External rotation (turning your arm out with elbow at your side) is often the first movement to go and the last to return.
- **Bilateral involvement:** In 6–17% of cases, both shoulders are affected, though usually not at the same time.

### Treatment

- **Physical therapy:** Gentle range-of-motion exercises are the cornerstone of treatment. **Pendulum exercises** (leaning forward and letting the arm hang, then gently swinging it in circles) and **wall climbs** (walking fingers up a wall) are safe starting points. Stretching should be gentle — pushing through severe pain makes the condition worse. A 2020 systematic review found that supervised physiotherapy improves outcomes faster than home exercise alone.
- **NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen):** Help with pain during the freezing phase but do not change the disease course. Use as needed for 1–2 weeks.
- **Corticosteroid injection:** An intra-articular injection of corticosteroid (usually triamcinolone 40 mg mixed with lidocaine) reduces inflammation. It is most effective in the freezing phase and can shorten the painful period. A Cochrane review found that steroid injections provide significant pain relief for 4–6 weeks compared to placebo.
- **Hydrodilatation (distension arthrography):** Sterile fluid (saline, sometimes with steroid and contrast) is injected into the joint under pressure to stretch the capsule. If you hear a pop, the capsule has released — this can give immediate improvement in range of motion. Evidence is mixed; a 2018 systematic review found short-term benefit but no difference at 12 months compared to other treatments.
- **Oral corticosteroids:** A short course (prednisone 30 mg daily tapered over 3 weeks) can reduce pain in the freezing phase, but the effect is temporary and not superior to injection.
- **Sleep positioning:** Sleeping with a pillow between your arm and body, or in a recliner if lying flat is painful, can help with night pain.

::callout::
#### What NOT to do

- Don't stop moving your arm completely — prolonged immobilization makes the capsule contract faster and worsens the condition.
- Don't do aggressive stretching or "push through the pain" — this increases inflammation and can prolong the freezing phase.
- Don't rely on painkillers alone — they mask pain without addressing the underlying contracture. You still need to stretch.
- Don't ignore shoulder stiffness that lasts more than a few months after an injury or surgery — you may be developing secondary frozen shoulder.
- Don't assume it's just arthritis — frozen shoulder is a specific condition with a different treatment approach.
::end::

## ::timeline::
- time: Month 1–4 (Freezing phase)
  text: Pain starts gradually, often with no clear trigger. It worsens over weeks, especially at night. You may notice you can't reach as high as before. Start NSAIDs for pain. See your doctor for evaluation and consider a steroid injection.
- time: Month 2–9 (Freezing phase continues)
  text: Pain peaks and begins to plateau. Stiffness becomes more noticeable. Range of motion steadily decreases. Physical therapy begins: gentle pendulum exercises, passive stretching. Avoid overhead activities that trigger sharp pain. Sleep with a pillow supporting the arm.
- time: Month 4–12 (Frozen phase)
  text: Pain subsides but stiffness is at its worst. The shoulder feels "locked." You can't reach overhead, behind your back, or out to the side. Continue gentle stretching — the goal is to maintain whatever range you have. Hydrodilatation or a second steroid injection may help.
- time: Month 9–18 (Thawing phase begins)
  text: Range of motion starts to slowly increase. You may notice you can reach a little higher on the wall each week. Increase stretching gently. Most people return to near-normal function during this period.
- time: Month 18–36 (Full recovery)
  text: Most patients have recovered near-full or full range of motion. Some residual stiffness may remain, especially in external rotation. Formal physiotherapy can be tapered. If significant stiffness persists beyond 24 months, discuss surgical options with an orthopaedic surgeon.

## ::full::
### Pathophysiology

Frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis) is a **self-limiting condition of the glenohumeral joint** characterized by synovial inflammation, capsular fibrosis, and progressive contracture of the joint capsule. The underlying aetiology remains incompletely understood, but the pathological sequence has been well characterized.

**Early phase (inflammatory/synovitic).** The initiating event is an **inflammatory synovitis** of the glenohumeral joint capsule. Synovial biopsy studies (Rodeo et al., 1997; Bunker et al., 2000) show a dense infiltration of **mononuclear inflammatory cells** — predominantly **CD4+ T-lymphocytes, macrophages, and mast cells** — in the synovium and subsynovial tissue. Elevated levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines — **IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-6, and TGF-β1** — are found in the synovial fluid. This phase corresponds clinically to the **freezing (painful) phase**. The inflammation is polyclonal and non-specific — no autoantibodies or infectious agents have been identified, suggesting a **dysregulated fibroinflammatory response** rather than an autoimmune process.

**Intermediate phase (fibroblastic proliferation).** As the inflammatory phase subsides, the capsule undergoes a striking **fibroblastic hyperplasia**. The normally thin, pliable capsular tissue (thickness ~1–2 mm) becomes thickened (up to 5–10 mm), contracted, and adherent to the humeral head. Histologically, the tissue shows **myofibroblast proliferation** with expression of **α-smooth muscle actin (α-SMA)** — cells capable of active contraction, analogous to the myofibroblasts in Dupuytren's contracture of the hand (Bunker & Anthony, 1995). Elevated **TGF-β1** (a master pro-fibrotic cytokine) drives fibroblast-to-myofibroblast differentiation and stimulates **collagen type I and III** deposition. The **coracohumeral ligament** (CHL) and the **rotator interval** — the triangular space between the supraspinatus and subscapularis tendons — are particularly affected and become thickened and shortened. Contracture of the CHL is thought to be the primary anatomical restraint limiting external rotation.

**Late phase (maturation/resolution).** Over months to years, the fibroblastic activity subsides, the cellularity decreases, and the contracted capsule undergoes **remodelling** — but with persistently **disorganized collagen architecture** and increased cross-linking. The mechanism of spontaneous resolution (the "thawing" phase) is not fully understood but likely involves **matrix metalloproteinase (MMP)-mediated turnover** of the fibrotic capsule, reduced expression of TGF-β1, and apoptosis of myofibroblasts. Resolution is not equivalent to full structural restitution — the capsule may remain mildly thickened, and some degree of restricted external rotation persists in up to 30% of patients.

**Risk factors.** The strongest associations include:

- **Diabetes mellitus:** The most important risk factor. Prevalence of frozen shoulder in people with diabetes is 10–20% (vs. 2–5% in the general population). It tends to be more severe, more prolonged, and less responsive to treatment in diabetic patients. The mechanism is thought to involve **advanced glycation end-products (AGEs)** that cross-link collagen, making the capsule less compliant, combined with the pro-fibrotic effects of hyperglycaemia on fibroblast activity.
- **Thyroid disease:** Both hyper- and hypothyroidism are associated with increased risk (OR ~2–4).
- **Dupuytren's disease:** Up to 50% of patients with frozen shoulder have Dupuytren's contracture or palmar fibromatosis, suggesting a shared fibroproliferative diathesis.
- **Trauma/surgery:** Any shoulder trauma, rotator cuff repair, breast surgery, or cardiac surgery (especially median sternotomy) can trigger secondary frozen shoulder through prolonged immobilization and post-surgical inflammation.
- **Parkinson's disease, stroke, and cervical radiculopathy:** Any condition that restricts normal shoulder movement for extended periods.

The condition is bilateral in 6–17% of cases, and the contralateral shoulder is typically affected within 5 years of the first episode.

### Differential diagnosis

1. **Rotator cuff tear:** The most common mimic. Key distinction: in a cuff tear, *active* range of motion is limited (you can't lift the arm) but *passive* range of motion is preserved (the examiner can move it). In frozen shoulder, both are restricted. Positive impingement signs and weakness on resisted testing suggest cuff pathology. MRI or ultrasound confirms.
2. **Rotator cuff tendinopathy / impingement syndrome:** Painful arc (pain between 60–120° of abduction), positive Neer and Hawkins tests. Range of motion is limited by pain, not by mechanical block — and external rotation is typically preserved.
3. **Glenohumeral osteoarthritis:** Creptius, morning stiffness that improves with activity, X-ray findings of joint space narrowing, osteophytes, and subchondral sclerosis. Unlike frozen shoulder, OA is slowly progressive and X-ray changes are diagnostic.
4. **Calcific tendinitis:** Acute onset of severe shoulder pain with calcium deposits in the rotator cuff (typically supraspinatus). X-ray or ultrasound shows calcification. Range of motion may be limited by pain, but passive ROM is preserved when the patient is relaxed.
5. **Cervical radiculopathy (C5–C6):** Pain originates in the neck and radiates to the shoulder and arm. Numbness/tingling in the C5 or C6 dermatome (lateral arm, thumb/forefinger), and neck motion reproduces the pain. Spurling test is positive. Shoulder range of motion is full when the neck is supported.
6. **Septic arthritis of the shoulder:** Rare but critical to identify. Presents with acute pain, fever, erythema, and inability to move the shoulder at all. ESR and CRP are markedly elevated. Requires urgent aspiration — Gram stain and culture confirm. Joint destruction can occur within 24–48 hours.
7. **Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS) of the shoulder (shoulder-hand syndrome):** Autonomic features: swelling, colour changes, temperature asymmetry, and hyperhidrosis of the hand and arm. Typically follows trauma, stroke, or myocardial infarction. Three-phase bone scan shows characteristic periarticular uptake.
8. **Polymyalgia rheumatica:** Bilateral shoulder and hip girdle pain and morning stiffness >45 minutes, age >50, markedly elevated ESR and CRP. Responds dramatically to low-dose prednisone. Ultrasound may show subacromial bursitis and biceps tenosynovitis.

| Passive range of motion measurement (goniometry) | Objective quantification of restriction. External rotation with elbow at side is the most sensitive measure — <30° is highly suggestive. ### Evidence summary

**Epidemiology and natural history:** Frozen shoulder has a lifetime incidence of 2–5% in the general population and 10–20% in people with diabetes (Zreik et al., *Bone Joint J* 2016). The peak incidence is between 40–60 years, and women are affected more often than men (1.4:1). The natural history has been classically described as 12–36 months with three phases. However, a prospective cohort study by Shaffer et al. (1992) found that 41% of patients had persistent mild restriction of motion at a mean follow-up of 7.4 years, challenging the assumption of complete resolution in all cases. The same study found that 6% of patients had persistent functional limitation.

**Corticosteroid injections:** The Cochrane review by Buchbinder et al. (2021) (16 RCTs, n=1,162) concluded that intra-articular corticosteroid injections provide clinically significant pain relief and improved range of motion in the short term (4–6 weeks) compared to placebo or no treatment. The effect diminishes by 12–24 weeks. There is moderate-quality evidence that injections are more effective when combined with physiotherapy than either alone. No significant difference was found between ultrasound-guided and landmark-guided injections.

**Physiotherapy:** A systematic review by Jain & Sharma (2014) of 12 RCTs found that supervised physiotherapy improves outcomes (Constant score, range of motion) significantly more than home exercise alone at 6 and 12 weeks. Capsular stretching techniques (posterior capsular stretch, inferior capsular stretch) may be more effective than generic stretching. The optimal frequency and duration remain unknown.

**Hydrodilatation:** A 2018 systematic review and meta-analysis of 8 RCTs (n=406) by Saltychev et al. found that hydrodilatation produces short-term improvement in pain and ROM at 4–12 weeks compared to corticosteroid injection alone. At 12 months, there was no significant difference between groups. The procedure is generally well tolerated, with transient procedural pain as the most common side effect.

**Manipulation under anaesthesia (MUA):** A 2020 systematic review by Sasanuma et al. found that MUA improves ROM immediately but there is a risk of iatrogenic fracture (1–5%), glenohumeral dislocation, and labral tear. MUA is most effective in the frozen phase when the capsule is contracted but not acutely inflamed. Outcomes are comparable to arthroscopic release at 12 months, but arthroscopic release may have a lower complication rate.

**Arthroscopic capsular release:** A 2022 systematic review of surgical outcomes (n=1,506 from 22 studies) by Cho et al. found that arthroscopic capsular release produces significant improvement in pain and ROM at 6 and 12 months, with a low complication rate (1.1% major complications including infection, nerve injury, and fracture). Most studies reported high patient satisfaction (>85%). The optimal timing is controversial — most surgeons recommend a trial of conservative therapy for 6–12 months before considering surgery.

**Diabetes and outcomes:** A 2019 cohort study by Dyer et al. (n=246) found that patients with diabetes had significantly worse outcomes across all treatment modalities — longer time to recovery (mean 18.5 months vs. 12.3 months), higher rates of bilateral disease (24% vs. 9%), and greater likelihood of requiring surgical release. Glycaemic control (HbA1c <7%) was associated with better outcomes.

### Surgical options (if indicated)

Surgery is considered when conservative treatment fails after 6–12 months of adequate trial (or earlier in patients with severe functional limitation who cannot tolerate prolonged symptoms).

- **Manipulation under anaesthesia (MUA):** The surgeon passively moves the shoulder through its full range of motion at the capsular end-points while the patient is under general anaesthesia, breaking the adhesions. The audible release of the capsule can be heard (like tearing Velcro). Post-procedure, the patient must start aggressive physiotherapy within 24–48 hours to prevent the capsule from re-contracting. Best results in the frozen (not freezing) phase. Risks include humeral fracture (1–5%, higher in osteoporotic bone), labral tears, and axillary nerve injury.
- **Arthroscopic capsular release:** The current surgical standard. Through 3–4 small portals, the surgeon identifies the contracted capsule and releases it using a radiofrequency probe or arthroscopic scissors. The rotator interval is released first (restoring external rotation), followed by the middle glenohumeral ligament, the anterior capsule, and the inferior capsule (axillary pouch). Care is taken to avoid the axillary nerve as it passes 2–5 mm inferior to the capsule. The subscapularis tendon is preserved. Same-day procedure, typically 30–45 minutes. Post-operative protocol: sling for comfort only (1–2 days), then aggressive physiotherapy starting day 1 with continuous passive motion (CPM) if available, progressing to active-assisted and active exercises.
- **Open capsular release:** Reserved for cases where arthroscopic release is inadequate or where the anatomy is distorted (e.g., previous surgery). A deltopectoral approach provides direct access to the anterior capsule and rotator interval. Used rarely.

### Open questions / active research

- What is the trigger for the initial inflammatory synovitis? Despite strong epidemiological associations, the molecular trigger that initiates the capsular inflammation in primary frozen shoulder (without trauma) remains unknown.
- Can anti-fibrotic agents (e.g., TGF-β inhibitors, pirfenidone) prevent capsular contracture? Animal studies are promising, but no human trials exist.
- Does early arthroscopic release (at 3–4 months rather than 6–12 months) produce better outcomes in patients with diabetes, who are known to have prolonged and treatment-resistant disease?
- What is the role of platelet-rich plasma (PRP) in the frozen shoulder? Preliminary studies show mixed results — some suggest PRP reduces pain and improves ROM, others show no benefit over corticosteroid injection.
- Can an HbA1c-targeted intervention (aggressive glycaemic control) reduce the incidence or severity of frozen shoulder in patients with diabetes? Prospective studies are needed.
- Why does the condition spontaneously resolve? The cellular and molecular mechanisms of the "thawing" phase — particularly the shift from fibrosis to matrix turnover — are poorly understood.
