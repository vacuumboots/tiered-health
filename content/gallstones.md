## ::gist::
### What are gallstones?

Gallstones are hardened deposits of bile that form in your gallbladder — a small pouch under your liver. About 10–15% of adults have them, but most never know it because they cause no symptoms. Trouble starts when a stone gets stuck and blocks the flow of bile.

### What to do right now

1. **If you have pain but no fever, rest and watch.** Biliary colic (pain after a fatty meal) usually goes away on its own in a few hours. Avoid fried or greasy food for 24 hours.
2. **If you have pain with fever, get medical attention** — this could be cholecystitis (infected gallbladder). You may need antibiotics and surgery.
3. **Don't try to "pass" a gallstone.** Unlike kidney stones, gallstones don't pass through urine. They're not going anywhere on their own once formed.
4. **If you're symptom-free, you don't need treatment.** Most people with silent gallstones never develop problems.

::danger::
#### ⚠️ Go to the emergency room if:

- You have severe right upper belly or right shoulder pain that won't go away
- You have a fever with the pain (over 38°C / 100.4°F)
- Your skin or the whites of your eyes turn yellow (jaundice)
- You have chills or shivering with the pain
- Your vomit looks dark or coffee-ground, or your stool looks pale or clay-colored
::end::

::callout::
#### Key fact

Only about 1–4% of people with silent (asymptomatic) gallstones develop complications each year. Most gallstones are discovered accidentally on an ultrasound done for another reason. If yours don't bother you, they can stay.
::end::

## ::adult::
### What's happening in your body

Your gallbladder is a pear-shaped organ tucked under your liver on the right side of your upper abdomen. It stores **bile** — a digestive fluid your liver makes that helps you break down fats. Between meals, the gallbladder fills up and concentrates the bile. When you eat a fatty meal, it squeezes the bile out through the **cystic duct** into the **common bile duct**, which empties into your small intestine.

Gallstones form when the chemical balance of bile goes off. Bile contains cholesterol, bile salts, and bilirubin (a breakdown product of old red blood cells). If there's too much cholesterol for the bile salts to keep dissolved, the cholesterol crystallizes — like sugar crystals forming in supersaturated honey. These crystals grow into stones over time.

About 80% of gallstones are **cholesterol stones** (yellow-green). The other 20% are **pigment stones** (dark, made of bilirubin), more common in people with liver cirrhosis, hemolytic anemia, or chronic infections.

**Who is at risk?** The classic "5 Fs" still teaches well: **F**emale, **F**orty, **F**ertile (or pregnant), **F**at (obese), and **F**amily history. Rapid weight loss — from crash dieting or bariatric surgery — also triggers stone formation because the liver dumps extra cholesterol into bile as it breaks down fat stores. Other risk factors include diabetes, metabolic syndrome, Crohn's disease, and some medications (octreotide, ceftriaxone, hormonal contraceptives).

### Symptoms

Most gallstones are silent. When they cause trouble, it's because a stone has obstructed the cystic duct or common bile duct.

- **Biliary colic:** A steady, gripping pain in the right upper belly or the center of your upper abdomen, often radiating to the right shoulder blade. It comes on 30–60 minutes after a fatty meal, builds to a peak, and lasts 1–5 hours before fading. Between episodes you feel fine. This is the classic symptom of transient stone obstruction.
- **Nausea and vomiting:** Common during a colic attack — the gallbladder's distress reflex triggers the vagus nerve.
- **Acute cholecystitis (inflamed gallbladder):** If a stone stays stuck, the gallbladder becomes swollen, red, and infected. The pain becomes constant and severe, often with fever and tenderness when a doctor presses on your right upper belly (Murphy's sign — you catch your breath when they push).
- **Jaundice (yellow skin/eyes):** A stone has left the gallbladder and lodged in the common bile duct (choledocholithiasis), blocking bile flow. This is a serious sign.
- **Dark urine and pale stool:** Another sign of bile duct obstruction — bilirubin builds up in the blood and gets filtered by kidneys (dark urine), while the stool lacks the pigment that gives it color (pale stool).

### Treatment

- **Observation (no treatment):** For asymptomatic stones — and that's most of them. The risk of complications is too low to justify surgery. Once symptoms start, though, the risk of more problems goes up (about 30–50% of people who have one colic episode will have another within a year). At that point, treatment is usually recommended.
- **Laparoscopic cholecystectomy:** This is the gold standard — removing the gallbladder through 3–4 small incisions in the belly. It's done under general anesthesia. Most people go home the same day or next morning and return to normal activity in 1–2 weeks. Your body adapts: bile flows directly from the liver to the small intestine in a steady trickle rather than in concentrated bursts. Some people get looser stools at first, especially after fatty meals, but this usually improves over months.
- **Open cholecystectomy:** The older approach — a larger incision under the ribs. Used when the gallbladder is too inflamed, scarred from prior surgeries, or when anatomy is tricky. Recovery takes 4–6 weeks. Done in about 5% of cases now.
- **Antibiotics:** If you have cholecystitis, you'll get IV antibiotics before surgery to control the infection. Common choices include piperacillin-tazobactam or a cephalosporin plus metronidazole.
- **ERCP (endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography):** If a stone is stuck in the common bile duct, a gastroenterologist can pass a camera down your throat, into the duodenum, and snip open the duct's opening to let the stone pass or pull it out with a tiny basket. This is often done before or after gallbladder removal.
- **Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA):** An oral bile acid that can slowly dissolve small cholesterol stones. It works in about 30–40% of cases but takes months and stones often return. It's mainly used for people who can't have surgery.

::callout::
#### What NOT to do

- Don't try a "gallbladder flush" with olive oil and lemon juice. It doesn't work — the "stones" people pass afterwards are just soap droplets formed by the oil mixing with digestive juices. You can end up in the ER with pancreatitis.
- Don't keep eating high-fat meals hoping the pain will go away with a different position. If you have symptoms, get evaluated.
- Don't ignore jaundice or fever with belly pain. Cholangitis (infected bile duct) is life-threatening without treatment.
- Don't avoid surgery because you're afraid of losing your gallbladder. You don't need it to live well, and leaving an inflamed gallbladder can lead to rupture, abscess, or sepsis.
- Don't take NSAIDs if you have suspected cholecystitis without a doctor's ok — if surgery is needed, bleeding risk increases.
::end::

  text: Pain lasts 1–5 hours, then resolves as the stone falls back. Rest, avoid food. If pain persists >6 hours with fever, seek emergency care.
- time: Day 1–2 after diagnosis
  text: If acute cholecystitis: hospital admission, IV fluids, IV antibiotics, NPO (nothing by mouth), surgical consult. Ultrasound confirms diagnosis.
- time: Within 72 hours (early cholecystectomy)
  text: For mild-moderate cholecystitis, early laparoscopic cholecystectomy (within 72 hours of admission) is superior to delayed — shorter hospital stay, lower conversion to open, fewer complications.
- time: 1–2 weeks post-op
  text: Return to normal activities and desk work. Avoid heavy lifting (>10 lbs) for 4–6 weeks. Some loose stools, especially after fatty meals — this is normal and improves.
- time: 4–6 weeks post-op (open surgery)
  text: Full recovery from open cholecystectomy. Incision healing, lifting restrictions lifted.
- time: 3–6 months post-op
  text: Bowel habits usually normalize. Most people have no dietary restrictions. A small percentage have chronic diarrhea (post-cholecystectomy diarrhea) that may need treatment.

## ::full::
### Pathophysiology

Gallstone formation (cholelithiasis) is a multifactorial process driven by three key mechanisms: **cholesterol supersaturation of bile**, **gallbladder dysmotility**, and **nucleation-promoting factors**.

**Cholesterol supersaturation.** Bile is an aqueous solution that must keep insoluble cholesterol in a micellar suspension using bile salts and phospholipids (primarily lecithin). When the molar ratio of cholesterol exceeds the solubilizing capacity of bile salts and lecithin, bile becomes **supersaturated** — the thermodynamic driving force for crystal formation. Hepatic hypersecretion of cholesterol is the dominant cause: women have higher baseline biliary cholesterol saturation than men (driven by estrogen-induced upregulation of HMG-CoA reductase, the rate-limiting enzyme in cholesterol synthesis). Supersaturation alone isn't sufficient for stone formation — many people with supersaturated bile never form stones — but it's the necessary first step.

**Gallbladder dysmotility.** A healthy gallbladder empties 50–80% of its volume in response to a meal. In gallstone formers, the gallbladder's contractile response is impaired — emptying fraction drops to 20–40%. Stagnant bile gives crystals time to aggregate and grow. The causes of dysmotility are incompletely understood but involve reduced sensitivity to cholecystokinin (CCK), the hormone that triggers gallbladder contraction, and possibly damage to the smooth muscle from recurrent low-grade inflammation. Pregnancy, rapid weight loss, and total parenteral nutrition all exacerbate stasis.

**Nucleation and crystal growth.** Once bile is supersaturated, cholesterol precipitates as **anhydrous cholesterol monohydrate crystals** — the characteristic rhomboid plates seen on polarized microscopy (they appear as Maltese cross birefringent forms). **Mucin** (mucous glycoprotein secreted by the gallbladder epithelium) acts as a scaffold: crystals adhere to the mucin gel layer, aggregate, and grow into macroscopic stones over months to years. The gallbladder's inability to clear mucin during stasis accelerates growth. Pigment stones (calcium bilirubinate) form when unconjugated bilirubin precipitates — this happens in hemolytic anemias (excess bilirubin production), cirrhosis (altered bilirubin metabolism), and biliary infections (bacterial β-glucuronidase deconjugates bilirubin).

**Natural history of the stone.** A stone may remain silent indefinitely, or it may migrate. The most consequential event is impaction in the **cystic duct**, obstructing outflow. Bile trapped in the gallbladder becomes concentrated and irritates the mucosa; prostaglandin-mediated inflammation leads to **acute cholecystitis** (edema, neutrophil infiltration, ischemic injury). If the cystic duct obstruction persists, the gallbladder wall can become gangrenous in 10–15% of cases, and perforation occurs in 2–10% (higher in diabetics and the elderly). Stones that pass the cystic duct and lodge in the **common bile duct** cause **choledocholithiasis** — obstruction of the common channel produces jaundice, bile duct dilation, and risk of **ascending cholangitis** (bacterial superinfection of the obstructed bile duct) or **gallstone pancreatitis** (if the stone transiently obstructs the pancreatic duct at the ampulla of Vater).

### Differential diagnosis

1. **Peptic ulcer disease (PUD):** Epigastric pain that may radiate to the back. Relieved by food or antacids. No fever, no jaundice. Endoscopy confirms.
2. **Acute pancreatitis:** Severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, elevated serum lipase (>3× upper limit of normal). Often from gallstones or alcohol. Gallstone pancreatitis typically resolves after stone passes or is removed via ERCP.
3. **Hepatic abscess:** Right upper quadrant pain, fever, weight loss. Imaging shows a hypoechoic/ hypodense liver lesion. Blood cultures positive in 50% of cases.
4. **Right lower lobe pneumonia:** Pleuritic right upper quadrant or shoulder pain, cough, fever. CXR shows infiltrate. The diaphragmatic pleura is irritated by the infected lung.
5. **Renal colic (right-sided):** Flank pain radiating to the groin, hematuria. Unlikely to be triggered by meals. CT or ultrasound shows a kidney stone, not gallbladder pathology.
6. **Budd-Chiari syndrome / hepatic congestion:** RUQ pain with hepatomegaly, ascites, and elevated bilirubin. Doppler ultrasound shows absent or reversed hepatic vein flow.
7. **Acute coronary syndrome:** Epigastric pain that can mimic biliary colic in atypical presentations. Check ECG and troponin in elderly patients or those with cardiac risk factors and atypical pain.

### Diagnostic workup

|------|---------------|---------------|
| Abdominal ultrasound | Gallstones (sensitivity >95%), gallbladder wall thickening (>4 mm suggests cholecystitis), pericholecystic fluid, sonographic Murphy sign (probe tenderness) | First-line. Best initial test for suspected gallstones or cholecystitis. No radiation, low cost |
| Liver function tests (LFTs) | Elevated ALT, AST, ALP, GGT, and bilirubin suggest common bile duct obstruction. ALP and GGT rise earliest. ALT/AST may spike in acute obstruction | Every patient with suspected gallstone disease. Normal LFTs do not rule out gallbladder pathology |
| CBC with differential | Leukocytosis (WBC >10,000/μL) with left shift suggests cholecystitis or cholangitis | Every patient with pain and fever |
| Lipase / amylase | Elevated lipase (>3× ULN) indicates gallstone pancreatitis | RUQ pain to rule out concomitant pancreatitis |
| MRCP (Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography) | Non-invasive visualization of the bile and pancreatic ducts. Stones in the common bile duct appear as filling defects. Sensitivity ~93% for CBD stones | Suspected choledocholithiasis (elevated LFTs, dilated CBD on US). Pre-cholecystectomy planning if intermediate risk for CBD stone |
| EUS (Endoscopic Ultrasound) | High-resolution probe placed in the duodenum visualizes the distal bile duct and ampulla. Sensitivity ~95–98% for CBD stones — the highest of any non-invasive test | Alternative to MRCP (preferred if patient has ferromagnetic implants, claustrophobia, or when direct tissue sampling is beneficial) |
| ERCP | Both diagnostic and therapeutic. Cannulation of the ampulla, contrast injection, stone extraction via sphincterotomy or basket | Confirmed choledocholithiasis or high probability + need for immediate therapy (cholangitis, ongoing obstruction) |

### Evidence summary

**Epidemiology:** Gallstones affect 10–15% of the adult population in Western countries, with higher prevalence in women (20–25% by age 60 vs. 10–15% in men). The NHANES III analysis (Everhart et al., 1999) of 14,000+ US adults found a prevalence of 8.6% in non-Hispanic whites, with Hispanic Americans at highest risk (12.6%) and Black Americans at lowest (5.2%). The incidence is ~0.6–1.4 new cases per 100 person-years (Lammert et al., 2016).

**Asymptomatic gallstones — watchful waiting is safe:** The Cochrane review by Gurusamy & Samraj (2007) found no RCTs comparing cholecystectomy vs. observation for silent stones — the evidence base is observational. Gracie & Ransohoff's landmark study (1982; n=123, 15-year follow-up) reported that only ~18% of asymptomatic patients ever developed symptoms, at a rate of ~2% per year in the first 5 years, declining thereafter. The risk of serious complications (cholecystitis, cholangitis, pancreatitis) was ~0.1–0.3% per year. Current guidelines (EASL 2016, Japanese 2021) unanimously recommend observation for asymptomatic gallstones — the number needed to treat is too high to justify prophylactic surgery.

**Early vs. delayed cholecystectomy for acute cholecystitis:** The landmark meta-analysis by Gurusamy et al. (Cochrane Review 2013, updated 2023) pooled 8 RCTs (n=1,419) comparing early laparoscopic cholecystectomy (within 7 days of symptom onset) vs. delayed (6–12 weeks after a "cooling" period with antibiotics). Results: early surgery reduced total hospital stay by 3.5 days (MD -3.50, 95% CI -5.49 to -1.52), with no significant difference in bile duct injury, conversion to open surgery, or mortality. The Tokyo Guidelines 2018 (TG18) and EASL 2016 both recommend early surgery for mild-moderate cholecystitis (Grade I–II). For severe cholecystitis (Grade III, organ dysfunction), delayed surgery after medical optimization is preferred.

**Medical dissolution therapy:** Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA, 10–15 mg/kg/day) can dissolve small (<5 mm), radiolucent cholesterol stones in select patients. The British Gallstone Study Group (1991) reported complete dissolution in ~30% at 6 months and ~40% at 12 months. However, stone recurrence rates are 30–50% at 4 years after stopping therapy (Lammert et al., 2016). UDCA is rarely used in modern practice outside patients deemed unfit for surgery, as it requires protracted treatment with uncertain durability. A 2021 randomized trial (n=140) found UDCA plus lifestyle modification reduced gallstone formation during rapid weight loss after bariatric surgery (OR 0.43, 95% CI 0.22–0.86).

**Laparoscopic vs. open cholecystectomy:** A Cochrane review by Keus et al. (2006) of 45 RCTs (n=4,576) comparing laparoscopic vs. open cholecystectomy found that laparoscopic approach reduced hospital stay by 3.4 days (95% CI 2.5–4.3), postoperative pain, and time to return to work, with comparable bile duct injury rates (~0.3–0.5%). Conversion rate to open was 5–10% in early series but <5% in high-volume centers today.

**Cholecystectomy and post-cholecystectomy syndrome:** A systematic review by Latenstein et al. (2024) found that 10–40% of patients report persistent abdominal symptoms after cholecystectomy, though only 5–10% have symptoms severe enough to seek further medical care. Pre-operative atypical symptoms (non-biliary dyspepsia, bloating, heartburn) were strong predictors of persistent post-operative symptoms, emphasizing the importance of careful patient selection.

### Surgical options

- **Laparoscopic cholecystectomy (standard):** 3–4 small incisions. Pneumoperitoneum with CO₂ insufflation. Dissection of Calot's triangle: cystic duct and cystic artery are clipped and divided, the gallbladder is detached from the liver bed and extracted through the umbilical port. Same-day discharge or 23-hour admission. Open conversion rate <5% in experienced hands. The critical view of safety (CVS) technique — only two structures (cystic duct and artery) entering the gallbladder, with the hepatocystic triangle cleared — is the standard method to prevent bile duct injury.
- **Laparoscopic cholecystectomy (difficult):** For acute cholecystitis, empyema, or gangrenous gallbladder. A "fundus-down" (domed-first) approach can help. Bailout strategies include subtotal cholecystectomy (leaving the posterior wall of Hartmann's pouch) to avoid the inflamed hilum, with drains placed — this reduces bile duct injury risk in hostile anatomy.
- **Open cholecystectomy:** Larger right subcostal (Kocher) incision. Used when laparoscopy is unsafe or anatomy is unrecognizable. Higher wound infection rate (~5% vs. 1–2%) and longer recovery. Incidence has declined to ~5% of all cholecystectomies.
- **ERCP with sphincterotomy (for choledocholithiasis):** The endoscope is passed through the stomach and duodenum to the ampulla of Vater. A wire-guided sphincterotome cuts the biliary sphincter, and stones are extracted with a balloon or basket. Success rate >90% for CBD stone clearance. Risks include pancreatitis (~3–5%), bleeding (~1–2%), perforation (~0.3–1%), and cholangitis (~1%). Yellow-tip: place a prophylactic pancreatic stent if the pancreatic duct is repeatedly cannulated or difficult to access — this halves the post-ERCP pancreatitis rate.
- **Cholecystostomy tube (percutaneous drainage):** Image-guided (US or CT) catheter placed directly into the gallbladder. Used as a temporizing measure in critically ill patients (septic shock, severe cholecystitis Grade III in elderly/frail patients) who cannot tolerate general anesthesia. The tube drains pus and infected bile, and the patient returns for cholecystectomy after recovery (typically 6–8 weeks). Some frail patients may keep the tube as a definitive treatment, but recurrence is ~20%.

### Open questions / active research

- What is the optimal management of common bile duct stones discovered during laparoscopic cholecystectomy? Pre-operative ERCP vs. intra-operative laparoscopic CBD exploration (LCBDE) vs. post-operative ERCP — the debate continues, with recent network meta-analyses suggesting similar outcomes for LCBDE and pre-op ERCP.
- Does the rising rate of cholecystectomy for "biliary dyskinesia" (low gallbladder ejection fraction without stones) reflect a real disease or diagnostic overuse? RCTs are lacking.
- Can gallstones be prevented in high-risk populations? The UDCA bariatric surgery data is promising, but no validated primary prevention strategy exists for the general population.
- What is the long-term metabolic impact of cholecystectomy? Observational data associates cholecystectomy with increased risk of metabolic syndrome, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), and colorectal cancer — but confounding is difficult to disentangle.
- Role of artificial intelligence in ultrasound interpretation — can AI match or exceed expert sonographer sensitivity for small (<3 mm) stones?
