## ::gist::
### What is sciatica?

A herniated disc or bone spur is pressing on a nerve root in your lower back (usually L4-L5 or L5-S1). This sends shooting pain, numbness, or tingling from your low back down through your buttock and into your leg or foot. Most cases get better on their own within 6–8 weeks without surgery.

### What to do right now

1. **Stay active but pace yourself.** Bed rest for more than a day or two actually slows recovery. Walk as much as pain allows — gentle movement helps.
2. **Take an NSAID.** Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) or naproxen (Aleve) reduce inflammation around the nerve. Take with food for 3–7 days as needed.
3. **Apply heat or ice.** Ice for 15–20 minutes reduces acute pain. Heat relaxes muscle spasms in the back. Try what feels better.
4. **Avoid positions that trigger it.** Don't sit slumped, don't bend forward to lift, and don't twist your spine while lifting.
5. **See a physical therapist.** They can teach you exercises that take pressure off the nerve and strengthen your core to prevent recurrence.

::danger::
#### ⚠️ Go to the emergency room if:

- You lose sensation in the "saddle area" (where you sit — inner thighs, genitals, anus)
- You suddenly can't control your bladder or bowels
- You have numbness or weakness in both legs at the same time
- The pain comes with fever, chills, or unexplained weight loss
- You develop sudden, severe weakness in your foot (foot drop)
::end::

::callout::
#### Good news

About 80–90% of sciatica cases from a herniated disc resolve with conservative care within 6–8 weeks. The body reabsorbs the herniated disc material over time. Even when symptoms are severe enough to consider surgery, the outcomes after one year are similar between people who had surgery and people who stuck with non-surgical treatment — surgery just helps faster in the short term.
::end::

## ::adult::
### What's happening in your body

Your spinal column is made of stacked vertebrae with soft, gel-filled discs between them. When a disc herniates, the inner gel (nucleus pulposus) pushes through a tear in the tough outer ring (annulus fibrosus) and presses against a nearby nerve root. This causes both **mechanical compression** and **chemical irritation** — the disc material leaks inflammatory proteins (phospholipase A2, tumor necrosis factor-alpha) that directly irritate the nerve.

The **sciatic nerve** is the largest nerve in your body (about as thick as your pinky finger). It's formed by nerve roots that exit your lower spine at L4, L5, S1, S2, and S3, then runs through your buttock and down the back of your leg before splitting into the tibial and common peroneal nerves at the knee. Because the nerve roots are crowded inside tight bony canals, even a small disc bulge — 4–6 mm — can cause significant symptoms.

Three levels account for over 95% of sciatica:
- **L4-L5 disc** compresses the **L5 nerve root** — pain along the outside of the thigh, shin, and top of the foot. Weakness lifting the big toe and ankle.
- **L5-S1 disc** compresses the **S1 nerve root** — pain down the back of the calf into the heel and outer foot. Weakness standing on tiptoes (calf). The ankle reflex may be diminished.
- **L3-L4 disc** compresses the **L4 nerve root** — pain in the front of the thigh and inner calf. Weaker quadriceps. Less common.

### Symptoms

- **Radiating pain:** a sharp, burning, or electric-shock sensation that travels from your low back/buttock down one leg. The path follows a specific dermatome depending on which nerve root is involved.
- **Numbness and tingling (paresthesia):** pins-and-needles in the leg or foot, corresponding to the affected nerve root. This is often more bothersome than the pain itself.
- **Muscle weakness:** can range from mild (the leg feels "heavy") to pronounced foot drop (can't lift the front of your foot) or difficulty rising from a squat.
- **Worsening with certain positions:** sitting (especially slouched), coughing, sneezing, or straining on the toilet increases intrathecal pressure and makes the pain worse. Lying on your side with a pillow between your knees often helps.
- **Back pain** may or may not be present — some people have classic sciatica without any low back pain at all.

### Treatment

- **Activity modification (not bed rest):** Bed rest beyond 1–2 days is harmful — it weakens core muscles and delays recovery. Stay as active as pain allows. Avoid heavy lifting, prolonged sitting, and twisting movements. Evidence from Cochrane reviews: advice to stay active produces better outcomes than advice to rest.

- **NSAIDs:** Ibuprofen 400–600 mg or naproxen 250–500 mg, taken with food, for 3–7 days. A 2016 Cochrane review (n=1,350) found NSAIDs moderately effective for short-term pain relief compared to placebo. Topical NSAID gels (diclofenac) are an option for those who can't take oral NSAIDs.

- **Physical therapy:** Core strengthening, nerve mobilization (slider/glider exercises), McKenzie method (directional preference exercises), and stretching. A 2020 trial (n=192) found that guideline-based PT was not better than a single education session at 1 year for sciatica — but PT improved function in the first 6 weeks. The key is finding exercises that *centralize* your pain (pull it from the leg toward the back).

- **Epidural steroid injections (ESI):** A corticosteroid and anesthetic injected into the epidural space around the irritated nerve root. Works by reducing inflammation and breaking the pain cycle. Cochrane review (Pinto et al., 2020, 30 RCTs, n=3,597): ESI provides modest short-term pain relief (NRS reduction of ~1.5 points at 1–3 months) but no significant improvement in long-term function or surgical rates. Best for acute/subacute radiculopathy (<6 months). Typically 1–3 injections per year maximum.

- **Muscle relaxants:** Cyclobenzaprine or tizanidine for associated paraspinal muscle spasm. No evidence they help radicular pain — only for the muscle tightness component.

::callout::
#### What NOT to do

- Don't stay in bed for days — bed rest does not help and makes you weaker. Studies show it prolongs recovery.
- Don't do "back cracking" or high-force chiropractic adjustments during the acute phase — they can worsen a disc herniation if the practitioner isn't careful.
- Don't ignore red-flag symptoms hoping they pass. Cauda equina syndrome is a surgical emergency and waiting 24 hours can change the outcome from full recovery to permanent incontinence.
- Don't jump to surgery without giving conservative care a fair trial (6–8 weeks) unless you have progressive weakness. The SPORT trial showed that by 1 year, surgical and non-surgical groups had nearly identical outcomes.
- Don't take opioid painkillers for sciatica if you can avoid them. A 2023 JAMA study showed opioids were no better than NSAIDs for acute radicular pain and carried addiction risk.
::end::

## ::timeline::
- time: Day 1–3
  text: Acute phase. Pain is sharp, radiates down the leg. NSAIDs, ice or heat, and gentle walking as tolerated. Avoid sitting for long periods. Sleep on your side with a pillow between your knees.
- time: Week 1–2
  text: Acute inflammation begins to subside. Start physical therapy — McKenzie exercises if you have a directional preference. Nerve gliding exercises. Most patients notice the leg pain becoming more "dull" and less sharp.
- time: Week 3–6
  text: Significant improvement for most. The leg pain may move closer to the back (centralization) — a good sign. Continue PT. Resume normal activities but avoid heavy lifting or prolonged bending.
- time: Week 6–12
  text: About 80% of patients are much better or fully recovered by this point. Disc material continues to be reabsorbed (size reduction of 30–60% on MRI at 6 months). Return to full activity. Core strengthening to prevent recurrence.
- time: 3+ months
  text: If leg pain persists despite conservative therapy, consider epidural steroid injection or surgical consultation. MRI to confirm the level. Discuss microdiscectomy vs. continued conservative management. The SPORT trial showed outcomes converge at 1 year.

## ::full::
### Pathophysiology

Sciatica is a **radicular pain syndrome** caused by mechanical compression and/or chemical irritation of the lumbosacral nerve roots (L4–S3). The most common cause — accounting for ~90% of cases — is an **intervertebral disc herniation** at L4-L5 or L5-S1.

**Mechanical mechanism:** The nucleus pulposus herniates through a radial fissure in the annulus fibrosus, creating a focal mass effect on the exiting or traversing nerve root. At L4-L5, the L5 nerve root traverses the disc space before exiting below the L5 pedicle, making it the most commonly compressed root in the lumbar spine. The spinal canal is narrowest at L4-L5 (mean sagittal diameter ~17 mm), leaving less room for the nerve root to escape compression.

**Chemical mechanism:** Disc material contains **phospholipase A2 (PLA2)** at concentrations 1,000–10,000 times higher than in plasma, along with **TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, nitric oxide, and prostaglandin E2**. These inflammatory mediators are released through the annular tear and directly sensitize the nerve root's dorsal root ganglion (DRG). Unlike peripheral nerves, spinal nerve roots lack a well-developed perineurium, making them exquisitely vulnerable to chemical irritation. The DRG is particularly sensitive — it has fenestrated capillaries and lacks the blood-nerve barrier, so inflammatory molecules freely access the sensory neuron cell bodies.

**Cellular changes:** Compressed axons show **demyelination** at the compression site within hours, with Wallerian degeneration in severe cases. The DRG shows upregulation of **TRPV1, Nav1.7, and Nav1.8 sodium channels**, lowering the firing threshold and producing spontaneous ectopic discharges — the electrical correlate of burning, shooting radicular pain. Neurogenic inflammation ensues, with substance P and CGRP release from nociceptor terminals further propagating the inflammatory cascade.

**Natural history of disc resorption:** The herniated disc material shrinks over time through **phagocytosis by macrophages** (which recognize the disc as "foreign" since the nucleus pulposus is immune-privileged) and **enzymatic degradation** by matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs). On serial MRI, the mean reduction in herniated disc volume is ~30% at 6 weeks, ~60% at 3 months, and ~75–90% at 12 months. This explains the favorable natural history — most people get better not because the nerve "un-pinches" but because the disc material that was pinching it gets eaten away.

### Differential diagnosis

1. **Lumbar spinal stenosis (neurogenic claudication):** Bilateral leg pain/cramping provoked by walking or standing, relieved by sitting or leaning forward (shopping cart sign). No dermatomal pattern — more "vague aching" of both legs. Peak age 60+. MRI shows trefoil-shaped canal with ligamentum flavum hypertrophy.
2. **Piriformis syndrome:** Sciatic nerve compression within or under the piriformis muscle in the buttock. Pain is worse with sitting on the affected side and with internal rotation of the hip (FAIR test). No MRI correlate. Rare as an isolated cause — most diagnosed cases actually have a spinal source.
3. **Facet joint (zygapophyseal) arthropathy:** Unilateral low back pain with referral to the buttock and posterior thigh, but rarely below the knee (which is a strong discriminator). Pain worse with extension and rotation, better with flexion. Diagnosed with medial branch blocks or intra-articular injection. Common concurrent finding with disc pathology.
4. **Sacroiliac (SI) joint dysfunction:** Pain in the buttock, groin, and posterior thigh. Worse with standing on one leg and stair climbing. Positive provocative tests (FABER, Gaenslen, thigh thrust). No radicular pattern or neurological findings.
5. **Hip osteoarthritis:** Deep groin pain radiating to the anterior thigh and knee, worse with weight-bearing and internal rotation. Limited and painful hip range of motion. Hip pathology can mimic L3 radiculopathy. Check hip X-ray before ordering lumbar spine MRI in older patients.
6. **Peripheral neuropathy (e.g., common peroneal nerve entrapment at fibular neck):** Foot drop and lateral leg numbness, but no back pain and no involvement of the hamstring or posterior thigh. Often from prolonged kneeling, leg crossing, or recent weight loss.
7. **Diabetic lumbosacral radiculoplexus neuropathy (diabetic amyotrophy):** Severe, asymmetrical proximal leg weakness and pain in poorly controlled diabetes. MRI spine normal. Distinct from typical sciatica — involves L2-L4 distribution with quadriceps wasting.
8. **Herpes zoster (shingles) before rash appears:** Burning dermatomal pain that may precede the vesicular rash by 3–5 days. Look for hyperesthesia in a dermatomal pattern without motor findings. Check for immune compromise or age >60.

### Diagnostic workup

| Test | What it shows | When to order |
|------|---------------|---------------|
| History and neurological exam | Dermatomal sensory loss, myotomal weakness, reflex changes (L4: knee jerk diminished, S1: ankle jerk diminished). Straight leg raise test (Lasègue): sensitivity 0.82–0.95 for disc herniation but specificity only 0.30–0.60. Crossed SLR (pain in the *other* leg when raising the unaffected one): specificity >0.90 — strongly predicts disc herniation. | Every patient — primary diagnostic tool |
| MRI lumbar spine (T1 + T2 sagittal + axial) | Gold standard. Shows disc herniation type (protrusion, extrusion, sequestration), level, side, nerve root compression, canal dimensions, and incidental findings (stenosis, synovial cysts, spondylolisthesis). High signal on T2 distinguishes acute from chronic disc changes. | Red flags, persistent symptoms >6 weeks, pre-surgical planning, when the diagnosis is uncertain |
| CT myelogram | Alternative when MRI is contraindicated (pacemaker, claustrophobia, non-compatible implant). Excellent bony detail but lower soft-tissue resolution than MRI. | MRI-incompatible patients, post-operative assessment |
| Electromyography (EMG) / Nerve conduction studies | Identifies active denervation (fibrillation potentials, positive sharp waves) in myotomes, chronic reinnervation changes, and distinguishes radiculopathy from peripheral nerve entrapment. | Atypical presentation, suspected peripheral neuropathy, chronic symptoms, or before epidural injection |
| X-ray (standing AP + lateral + flexion-extension) | Assesses alignment, instability (spondylolisthesis), degenerative changes, fracture, and bone lesions. Does NOT visualize discs or nerve roots. | Initial screening for instability, trauma, or structural deformity |
| CT scan | Better bony detail than MRI. Useful if foraminal stenosis from facet hypertrophy or bony spur is suspected. | Suspected bony foraminal stenosis or complex fracture |
| Laboratory studies (CBC, ESR, CRP, HbA1c) | Rule out infection (discitis, epidural abscess), inflammatory spondyloarthropathy, or uncontrolled diabetes causing neuropathy. | Fever, night sweats, age >50 with unexplained back pain, known diabetes |

### Evidence summary

**Epidemiology:** Sciatica has a lifetime prevalence of 13–40% across Western populations (Konstantinou & Dunn, 2008). The incidence peaks in the 4th–5th decades, with a male-to-female ratio of approximately 1.5:1. L4-L5 and L5-S1 account for 90–95% of all disc herniations causing sciatica. Occupational risk factors include heavy lifting (OR 2.0–2.5), prolonged driving with vibration (OR 1.5–2.0), and repetitive bending and twisting.

**Natural history and conservative management:** The landmark Maine Lumbar Spine Study (Atlas et al., 2006) followed 507 patients for 10 years: surgical patients had greater improvement at 1 year, but by 4–10 years the outcomes were similar. The SPORT trial (Weinstein et al., 2006, 2008; n=1,244 across three arms) remains the largest RCT on surgery vs. non-operative treatment for lumbar disc herniation. At 1 year, the surgical group had significantly better pain and function scores in the as-treated analysis, but these differences narrowed by 4 years. The intention-to-treat analysis (crossovers diluted the effect) showed more modest differences. Key conclusion: surgery provides faster relief, but most patients improve with conservative care, and the long-term outcome is similar.

**NSAIDs:** A 2016 Cochrane review (Rasmussen-Barr et al., n=1,350) found moderate evidence that NSAIDs are effective for short-term symptom relief in sciatica compared to placebo (standardized mean difference −0.35). There was no evidence that one NSAID is superior to another. No significant difference in adverse events between NSAIDs and placebo at 1–2 weeks.

**Epidural steroid injections:** The comprehensive Cochrane review by Pinto et al. (2020, 30 RCTs, 3,597 participants) found:
- **Short-term (1–3 months):** ESI reduces leg pain (MD −0.72 on a 10-point scale) and improves function (MD −0.47 on a 100-point ODI) compared to placebo injections. The effect is statistically significant but modest — about half a point on a 10-point pain scale.
- **Long-term (>6 months):** No significant difference in pain, function, or the need for surgery.
- **Route:** Transforaminal ESI may be more effective than interlaminar ESI at the affected level, but carries a rare risk of spinal cord infarction (estimated 1:100,000–1:200,000) from inadvertent injection into a radicular artery.
- **Conclusion:** ESI provides short-term relief but does not change the long-term natural history or surgical rate.

**Surgery:** Microdiscectomy is the gold standard surgical procedure. A 2023 meta-analysis of 5 RCTs (n=982) comparing microdiscectomy to conservative care found that surgery provided superior pain relief at 3 months (SMD −1.12) and 1 year (SMD −0.67), but the difference was no longer significant at 2 years (SMD −0.18). The NERVES trial (Wilby et al., 2021, Lancet Rheumatology) randomized 163 patients to microdiscectomy vs. transforaminal epidural steroid injection: surgery provided faster and greater relief at 12 weeks (Leg Pain Score: 29.1 vs. 42.9, p<0.001), but by 52 weeks the difference was attenuated and not clinically meaningful.

**Physical therapy:** A pragmatic 2020 RCT (Verheijen et al., BMJ, n=192) found no difference between guideline-based PT and a single education session for sciatica at 1 year. However, PT improved function and reduced pain in the first 6 weeks. The McKenzie method (directional preference exercises) had moderate evidence for centralization of symptoms in a 2021 systematic review.

**Imaging:** A 2022 meta-analysis of 30 studies (n=9,102) found that the prevalence of incidental disc herniation on lumbar MRI in asymptomatic individuals is 22% at any level, and 10% at L4-L5 or L5-S1 specifically. This means finding a disc herniation on MRI does not prove it's the cause of the pain — clinical correlation is essential.

### Surgical options

- **Microdiscectomy:** The standard first-line surgical procedure. A 2–3 cm incision with a microscope, partial laminotomy, retraction of the nerve root, and removal of the herniated disc fragment. Usually outpatient or 23-hour stay. Success rate: ~85–90% excellent/good outcomes (defined as complete or near-complete relief of leg pain). Complication rate: ~3–5% overall (dural tear 2–4%, infection 1–2%, nerve root injury <1%, re-herniation requiring revision ~5–10% at 5 years). Return to work: 2–6 weeks for desk jobs, 8–12 weeks for manual labor.

- **Endoscopic discectomy:** Smaller incision (~8 mm), less tissue disruption, faster recovery. Success rates comparable to microdiscectomy (85–90% in selected patients). Steeper learning curve. Limited evidence from two meta-analyses: similar outcomes at 2 years, earlier return to work by ~1 week.

- **Lumbar fusion:** Adding fusion (posterolateral fusion or transforaminal lumbar interbody fusion, TLIF) is controversial for isolated disc herniation. Indications: associated instability (spondylolisthesis), recurrent herniation, or discogenic pain confirmed by discography. The SLITT trial (2021) found no benefit of fusion over microdiscectomy alone for disc herniation with Modic changes.

- **Artificial disc replacement:** Reserved for single-level disc degeneration with concordant discogenic pain. Not appropriate for acute disc herniation with radiculopathy. Evidence from the ProDisc-L and Charité trials: equivalent to fusion at 2–5 years with preserved motion, but with a re-operation rate of ~10% at 5 years.

- **Minimally invasive percutaneous techniques:** Percutaneous laser disc decompression (PLDD), nucleoplasty, and ozone therapy — these reduce intradiscal pressure by removing a small amount of nucleus pulposus. A 2025 study (Gazzeri et al., Pain Medicine, n=58) showed a median reduction in disc area of 35.9% on axial MRI and 49.3% on sagittal MRI after PLDD, with all patients showing improvement. Best candidates: contained disc protrusions without sequestration. Evidence quality: low — most studies are case series without sham controls.

### Open questions / active research

- What is the optimal timing for surgery in patients with non-progressive radiculopathy? The SPORT and NERVES trials suggest earlier surgery gives faster relief, but 1-year outcomes converge. Is 6 weeks of waiting too long for a patient in severe pain?
- Can biologic therapies (platelet-rich plasma injected into the disc, mesenchymal stem cells) promote annular healing and prevent re-herniation after microdiscectomy? Early-phase trials ongoing.
- Why do some disc herniations cause severe pain while others (the 22% incidental rate on MRI) are asymptomatic? The role of genetic polymorphisms in inflammatory mediators (TNF-α, IL-1β) may explain individual variation.
- Does the route of epidural steroid injection (transforaminal vs. interlaminar vs. caudal) meaningfully change outcomes when accounting for the specific level and side affected?
- Can high-resolution ultrasound of the nerve root cross-sectional area predict who will benefit from surgery vs. continued conservative care?
