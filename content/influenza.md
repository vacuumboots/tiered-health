## ::gist::
### What is influenza?

Influenza (the flu) is a viral infection that attacks your nose, throat, and lungs. It comes on fast — high fever, body aches, headache, and a dry cough. Unlike the common cold, the flu hits you like a truck and usually puts you in bed for several days. Most people get better on their own within a week, but for some groups it can be dangerous.

### What to do right now

1. **Rest and hydrate.** Stay home, drink fluids (water, broth, electrolyte drinks), and sleep as much as you can. Your body needs energy to fight the virus.
2. **Take fever reducers if needed.** Acetaminophen (Tylenol) or ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) can lower your fever and ease the aches. Follow the label dosing.
3. **Call your doctor if you're in a high-risk group.** People over 65, pregnant women, young children, or anyone with a chronic medical condition may benefit from antiviral medication (oseltamivir/Tamiflu). It works best if started within 48 hours of symptoms.
4. **Stay home and avoid others.** You're contagious from 1 day before symptoms start through about 5–7 days after. Don't go to work, school, or social events.

::danger::
#### ⚠️ Go to the emergency room if:

- You have trouble breathing or feel short of breath at rest
- You have chest pain or pressure
- You feel confused or disoriented
- You can't keep any fluids down (risk of dehydration)
- Symptoms improve then come back with a worse fever and cough (could be secondary pneumonia)
- Your lips or face turn bluish
- You have a seizure
::end::

::callout::
#### Most people recover at home

The flu is unpleasant, but for otherwise healthy people it's self-limiting. The fever usually breaks in 2–3 days, though the cough and fatigue can linger for weeks. Antibiotics won't help — this is a virus. Antiviral medication is available but works best in the first 48 hours and is mainly recommended for high-risk patients.
::end::

## ::adult::
### What's happening in your body

Influenza is caused by **influenza A** or **influenza B** viruses — two of the four types in the *Orthomyxoviridae* family. You catch it when someone nearby coughs or sneezes, releasing tiny virus-laden droplets into the air. You can also pick it up from contaminated surfaces and then touching your eyes, nose, or mouth.

Once the virus gets into your respiratory tract, it attaches to epithelial cells using a surface protein called **hemagglutinin (HA)**. The virus enters the cell, hijacks its machinery to make thousands of copies of itself, and then bursts out — killing the host cell and spreading to neighboring cells. Another surface protein, **neuraminidase (NA)**, helps the newly formed viruses escape the infected cell to keep infecting more cells.

Your immune system responds with a bang. Within 24–48 hours, the innate immune system releases a flood of inflammatory cytokines (interferons, interleukins, TNF-α). These chemicals are what make you feel awful — fever, muscle aches, fatigue, headache. The fever is actually part of your defense: many viruses replicate less efficiently at higher body temperatures.

The infection peaks around day 2–3. By day 5–7, the adaptive immune system has produced enough antibodies (especially against HA and NA) to clear the virus. The cough and fatigue can linger for 1–3 weeks as your airways repair the damaged epithelium.

### Symptoms

- **Sudden high fever** (101–104°F / 38–40°C): comes on fast, often within hours. Peak fever typically lasts 2–3 days.
- **Severe body aches and muscle pain (myalgias):** often the most prominent symptom. Your muscles feel deep, heavy, and sore. Worse in the legs and back.
- **Headache:** often severe, bilateral, behind the eyes. Driven by cytokine release in the brain's blood vessels.
- **Dry cough:** hacking, non-productive cough. The cough reflex is triggered by damaged epithelial cells in the trachea and bronchi. This is the symptom that lasts the longest — often 2 weeks or more.
- **Sore throat:** common but usually not as severe as in strep throat or adenovirus.
- **Fatigue and weakness:** profound. Even getting up to go to the bathroom can feel exhausting. This can persist for weeks after other symptoms resolve.
- **Nasal congestion and runny nose:** more common in children than adults.
- **Nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea:** more common in children with influenza B. In adults, GI symptoms usually point to a different infection (like norovirus), but influenza can cause them too.

### Treatment

- **Rest and fluids:** the cornerstone of management. The goal is to support your immune system while it clears the virus. Dehydration is a common complication because of fever (increased fluid loss) and reduced oral intake.
- **Antipyretics and analgesics:** acetaminophen or ibuprofen for fever and muscle aches. Alternating between the two is safe for adults but not usually necessary.
- **Antiviral medications:** oseltamivir (Tamiflu, oral) is the most widely used. Also available: zanamivir (Relenza, inhaled), peramivir (Rapivab, IV), and baloxavir marboxil (Xofluza, single oral dose).
  - **Who should get them:** high-risk groups (age ≥65, pregnancy, chronic medical conditions, immunocompromised, extreme obesity, age <5 especially <2 years). Also consider for hospitalized patients or anyone with severe or progressive illness.
  - **When:** within 48 hours of symptom onset for maximum benefit. Oseltamivir reduces symptom duration by about 0.6–1.0 day in adults and may reduce hospitalizations in high-risk groups.
  - **Effect size:** modest but clinically meaningful in high-risk populations. The benefit is larger the earlier it's started.
- **Cough suppressants:** not routinely recommended. The cough is helping clear mucus and debris. If it's interfering with sleep, a small dose of dextromethorphan may help.

::callout::
#### What NOT to do

- **Don't ask for antibiotics.** The flu is viral. Antibiotics don't work and contribute to resistance.
- **Don't go back to work or school too early.** You're contagious until your fever is gone for 24 hours without fever-reducing meds. Pushing through makes you sicker and infects others.
- **Don't give aspirin to children or teenagers** with flu symptoms — it can trigger Reye's syndrome, a rare but serious condition that causes liver and brain swelling.
- **Don't take more than one product containing acetaminophen** at the same time — many cold/flu combo products already contain it, and exceeding 3,000–4,000 mg/day can damage your liver.
- **Don't smoke or vape** while recovering. Your respiratory epithelium is already damaged; smoke delays healing.
::end::

## ::timeline::
- time: Day 0–1
  text: Exposure. Incubation period averages 1–4 days. You're contagious starting 1 day before any symptoms appear.
- time: Day 1–3
  text: Abrupt symptom onset. Fever peaks (101–104°F), severe body aches, headache, dry cough. Bed rest essential. Start antivirals if high-risk and within 48-hour window.
- time: Day 2–5
  text: Peak illness. Fever may start breaking. Cough persists. Fatigue is profound. Stay home, hydrate, rest. Most contagious during this period.
- time: Day 5–7
  text: Fever usually resolves. Cough and fatigue remain. Start feeling human again but energy is low. Still contagious — fever should be gone for 24 hours before returning to normal activities.
- time: Day 7–14
  text: Most symptoms resolved. Cough may persist. Fatigue can linger 2–3 weeks. Return to gradual activity, don't jump back into intense exercise.
- time: Week 3–6
  text: Full recovery for most people. Post-viral cough may still be present. If fever returns with worsening cough, see a doctor — could be secondary bacterial pneumonia.

## ::full::
### Pathophysiology

Influenza is an acute viral infection of the respiratory tract caused by **Orthomyxoviridae** viruses, primarily influenza A and influenza B. The virus has a segmented, single-stranded negative-sense RNA genome, which enables genetic reassortment (antigenic shift) in influenza A — the mechanism behind pandemic emergence.

**Viral entry:** Hemagglutinin (HA) binds to sialic acid receptors on respiratory epithelial cells. HA is cleaved by host proteases (e.g., TMPRSS2, matriptase) to expose the fusion peptide, enabling viral-endosomal membrane fusion. Once inside the nucleus (unique among RNA viruses), the viral RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp) replicates the genome and transcribes mRNA.

**Cytopathic effects:** Direct cytotoxicity in type I and II pneumocytes, ciliated columnar epithelial cells, and alveolar macrophages. Infected cells undergo apoptosis or are killed by cytotoxic T lymphocytes. The resulting epithelial denudation — from the trachea down to the bronchioles — exposes basement membrane, impairs mucociliary clearance, and increases susceptibility to secondary bacterial infection.

**Immune response:** The innate response is a double-edged sword. Type I interferons (IFN-α/β) and pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β) are essential for viral clearance, but the resulting **cytokine storm** is responsible for the systemic symptoms — fever (pyrogenic cytokines acting on the hypothalamus), myalgias (PGE₂-mediated muscle catabolism), and headache (vasodilation and trigeminal nerve activation). Severe influenza can cause a hyperinflammatory state with elevated ferritin, CRP, and D-dimer resembling cytokine release syndrome.

**Viral evasion:** Influenza A evades immunity through antigenic drift (point mutations in HA and NA epitopes, necessitating annual vaccine updates) and antigenic shift (reassortment of genome segments between human and animal influenza A strains, producing novel subtypes that can cause pandemics). The NS1 protein suppresses host interferon production, directly binding RIG-I and inhibiting IRF-3 activation.

**Complications of severe disease:**
- **Primary viral pneumonia:** diffuse alveolar damage, hyaline membrane formation, hemorrhage. High mortality — up to 50% in some series of H1N1 pdm09 and H5N1.
- **Secondary bacterial pneumonia:** typically 5–10 days after initial illness. Most common pathogens: *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Staphylococcus aureus* (including MRSA), *Haemophilus influenzae*. Influenza damages the respiratory epithelium, exposing binding sites for bacterial adherence and impairing alveolar macrophage function.
- **Myocarditis:** rare but underdiagnosed. Direct viral invasion of cardiac myocytes. Can present with arrhythmia, heart failure, or cardiogenic shock.
- **Encephalitis/encephalopathy:** rare, more common in children. Includes acute necrotizing encephalopathy (ANE), associated with RANBP2 mutations.
- **Myositis and rhabdomyolysis:** benign acute myositis of the calves in children; severe rhabdomyolysis with acute kidney injury in adults.
- **Reye's syndrome:** in children given aspirin during influenza or varicella. Mitochondrial injury causing hepatic steatosis and cerebral edema.

### Differential diagnosis

1. **COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2):** Cannot be distinguished from influenza by symptoms alone. Both present with fever, cough, myalgias, and fatigue. Testing (PCR or rapid antigen) is required during co-circulation season. Loss of taste/smell (anosmia) is more specific to COVID-19.
2. **Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV):** More prominent rhinorrhea, wheezing, and bronchiolitis pattern. Common in young children and older adults. Testing by PCR.
3. **Common cold (rhinovirus, coronavirus, adenovirus):** Gradual onset, low-grade or no fever, prominent nasal congestion and runny nose. Rarely causes systemic symptoms like body aches and severe fatigue.
4. **Streptococcal pharyngitis:** Fever and sore throat, but lacks cough, myalgias, and nasal congestion. Rapid strep test or throat culture.
5. **Bacterial pneumonia:** Focal chest pain, productive cough with purulent sputum, lobar consolidation on exam. Distinguished by focal findings on chest X-ray and Gram stain/culture.
6. **Human metapneumovirus (hMPV):** Clinically indistinguishable from RSV and influenza. Requires PCR testing.

### Diagnostic workup

| Test | What it shows | When to order |
|------|---------------|---------------|
| Clinical diagnosis (influenza-like illness — ILI) | Fever ≥100°F + cough and/or sore throat, with abrupt onset during influenza season | During peak season — high positive predictive value; testing may not change management |
| Rapid antigen test (RIDT) | Qualitative detection of influenza A/B nucleoprotein | Point-of-care, 15 min. Low sensitivity (50–70%), high specificity (>95%). Positive = rule-in; negative = does not rule out |
| RT-PCR (nasopharyngeal swab) | Quantitative detection of influenza A/B RNA. Can subtype influenza A (H1N1, H3N2) | Gold standard. Sensitivity >95%, specificity >98%. Order when result will change management (hospitalization, high-risk, outbreak investigation) |
| Direct fluorescent antibody (DFA) | Viral antigen detection in respiratory epithelial cells | Moderate sensitivity (70–90%). Faster than PCR (2–4 h) but requires lab expertise |
| Multiplex PCR (respiratory panel) | Simultaneous detection of influenza A/B, RSV, COVID-19, adenovirus, hMPV, parainfluenza, rhinovirus | First test of choice during respiratory season when multiple viruses are circulating |
| Complete blood count (CBC) | Leukopenia (common), relative lymphopenia. If WBC >15,000 or bandemia → suspect bacterial co-infection | Moderate-severe illness, hospitalization |
| Procalcitonin (PCT) | Low (<0.25 ng/mL) in pure viral infection. Elevated in bacterial co-infection | Guide antibiotic de-escalation in hospitalized patients |
| Chest X-ray (2 views) | Normal in uncomplicated influenza. Interstitial or bilateral patchy opacities in viral pneumonia. Lobar consolidation in secondary bacterial pneumonia | Dyspnea, hypoxia, suspected pneumonia, or hospital admission |
| Serum CRP | Markedly elevated (often >50–100 mg/L) but non-specific | Marker of inflammation; cannot distinguish viral from bacterial alone |

### Evidence summary

**Antivirals — neuraminidase inhibitors:** The Cochrane review by Jefferson et al. (2014) of 26 RCTs (n=11,877) found that oseltamivir reduced time to first symptom alleviation by 16.8 hours in adults (95% CI 8.4–25.2 hours) and by 29 hours in untreated-confirmed influenza. Risk of hospitalization was reduced in adults (OR 0.85, 95% CI 0.73–0.99). Oseltamivir increased nausea (OR 1.57, 95% CI 1.14–2.15) and vomiting (OR 2.43, 95% CI 1.75–3.38). The Cochrane review of zanamivir (n=8,491) found a reduction of 0.60 days (95% CI 0.39–0.81) in symptom duration. The evidence for reduction in pneumonia or other complications was weaker in adults but stronger in high-risk populations.

**Antivirals — baloxavir marboxil:** The CAPSTONE-1 (Hayden et al., 2018, NEJM) and CAPSTONE-2 (Ison et al., 2020, Lancet Infect Dis) phase 3 trials showed baloxavir (a cap-dependent endonuclease inhibitor, single oral dose) reduced time to symptom alleviation comparable to oseltamivir (53.7 vs. 53.8 hours in CAPSTONE-1). Baloxavir demonstrated greater reduction in viral load at 24 hours (lower limit of quantification achieved faster) and was associated with less viral shedding. Resistance-conferring mutations (I38T in PA gene) emerged in 2.2% of adults and up to 10% of children in CAPSTONE-1.

**Vaccine effectiveness:** Annual influenza vaccine effectiveness (VE) varies by season, typically 40–60% when circulating strains are well-matched to vaccine strains. A 2023 meta-analysis of test-negative design studies found VE of 41% (95% CI 36–46%) against outpatient illness and 54% (95% CI 46–61%) against hospitalization in adults. VE is lower in elderly adults due to immunosenescence; high-dose and adjuvanted vaccines improve immunogenicity. The live attenuated influenza vaccine (LAIV, FluMist) showed variable effectiveness in recent seasons — current ACIP recommendations favor LAIV for children aged 2–8 years when available.

**Complications and hospitalization:** A CDC systematic review found that influenza-associated hospitalization rates are highest in adults ≥65 years (approximately 300–500 per 100,000) and children <5 years (100–200 per 100,000). The 1918 H1N1 pandemic killed 50 million people globally. The 2009 H1N1 pandemic disproportionately affected younger adults, with mortality rates highest in those aged 20–49 years — a pattern attributed to pre-existing cross-reactive immunity in older cohorts.

**WHO clinical practice guidelines (2026):** The 2026 WHO guideline update (BMJ, PMID 41760115) recommends oseltamivir for all patients with confirmed or suspected influenza who are hospitalized, and for high-risk outpatients. Baloxavir is recommended as an alternative to oseltamivir for non-pregnant adults without severe immunosuppression. The guidelines recommend against routine use of corticosteroids unless indicated by other conditions (e.g., asthma exacerbation, septic shock), and against the use of intravenous immunoglobulin outside clinical trials.

### Complications

- **Primary influenza pneumonia:** develops 1–3 days after onset, progresses rapidly. Diffuse infiltrates, acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS). High mortality.
- **Secondary bacterial pneumonia:** develops 5–10 days after initial illness. "Double-sickening" — fever returns after improvement. Most common: *S. pneumoniae*, *S. aureus*, *H. influenzae*.
- **Myocarditis and pericarditis:** chest pain, arrhythmias, heart failure. ECG may show diffuse ST-segment elevation. Treat supportively.
- **Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS):** diffuse alveolar damage, refractory hypoxemia. Requires mechanical ventilation. Lung-protective ventilation strategy. Mortality 30–60%.
- **Acute kidney injury:** from rhabdomyolysis, dehydration, or multi-organ failure.
- **Neurologic:** febrile seizures (children), encephalitis, Guillain-Barré syndrome (rare, ~1 per million).
- **Sepsis and multi-organ failure:** in severe cases, especially immunocompromised and elderly.

### Open questions / active research

- Is baloxavir's faster viral clearance profile clinically meaningful for reducing transmission? Real-world cluster studies are ongoing.
- Can universal influenza vaccines (targeting the conserved HA stalk domain, M2e, or neuraminidase) provide durable protection across seasons? Several candidates are in phase 1–2 trials.
- What is the optimal timing of antiviral therapy in hospitalized patients beyond the 48-hour window? Observational data suggests benefit regardless of duration, but RCT data is limited.
- Does combination antiviral therapy (oseltamivir + baloxavir) improve outcomes over monotherapy? Early trials show no additive benefit and potential antagonism in animal models.
- What is the role of anti-inflammatory therapy (baricitinib, tocilizumab, etc.) in severe influenza? Therapeutics targeting the cytokine storm component need more data.
- Can host-directed therapies (interferon lambda, nafamostat) prevent progression to severe disease?
