## ::gist::
### What is radial nerve palsy?

You pressed on a nerve in your arm for too long. Now you can't lift your wrist or straighten your fingers. People call this "Saturday night palsy" because it often happens after falling asleep with your arm draped over a chair — or over someone.

### What to do right now

1. **Stop leaning on that arm.** Take pressure off it.
2. **Wear a wrist splint.** This keeps your wrist from flopping down while the nerve heals. You can buy one at any pharmacy.
3. **Rest it.** Don't do heavy lifting with that arm.
4. **Wait.** Most people get better on their own in a few weeks.

::danger::
#### ⚠️ Go to the emergency room if:

- You can't move your arm at all (not just the wrist)
- You have a fever with the weakness
- You fell or hit your arm hard before this started
- It's getting worse, not better, after a few days
::end::

::callout::
#### Good news

About 90% of people with radial nerve palsy from compression recover fully. It just takes time — usually 4 to 8 weeks. The nerve grows back at about 1 millimeter per day.
::end::

## ::adult::
### What's happening in your body

The radial nerve runs from your neck (C5–T1 nerve roots), through your armpit, and down the back of your upper arm. It wraps around the humerus in a groove called the *spiral groove*. This is where it's most vulnerable — there's very little padding between the nerve and the bone.

When you compress this area (arm over chair back, partner's head, crutches pressing into armpit), the nerve's blood supply gets cut off. If the compression lasts hours, the myelin sheath — the insulation around the nerve — gets damaged. The nerve can still send signals, but they're slower and weaker. This is called **neuropraxia**, the mildest form of nerve injury.

### Symptoms

- **Wrist drop:** can't extend your wrist. This is the hallmark sign.
- **Finger drop:** can't straighten your fingers at the knuckles (metacarpophalangeal joints). You can still straighten them at the last two joints — that's the ulnar nerve's job.
- **Numbness/tingling:** back of the hand, thumb side, and first two fingers (but only the back, not the palm).
- **Weak grip:** because you can't stabilize your wrist, your grip is weaker even though the flexors work fine.
- Triceps is usually *spared* — its nerve branch comes off before the spiral groove.

### Treatment

- **Wrist splint:** cock-up splint keeping wrist in 20–30° extension. Wear during the day and while sleeping. This prevents contractures and lets you use your hand.
- **Physiotherapy:** passive range-of-motion exercises to prevent stiffness. Electrical stimulation may help maintain muscle mass but evidence for faster recovery is mixed.
- **NSAIDs:** if there's inflammation. Not always needed — compression palsy isn't typically inflammatory.
- **Surgery:** only if no recovery after 3–6 months, or if the palsy was from a fracture or laceration. Surgical options include neurolysis (freeing the nerve from scar tissue) or tendon transfers.

::callout::
#### What NOT to do

- Don't keep sleeping in the position that caused this.
- Don't do heavy wrist curls or extensions to "work it out" — the nerve needs rest, not stress.
- Don't ignore it for months hoping it goes away. If it's not improving by 8 weeks, get evaluated.
::end::

## ::full::
### Pathophysiology

Radial nerve palsy from external compression is a **neuropraxic** injury — the mildest grade in the Seddon classification (grade I). The mechanism is primarily ischemic: sustained pressure above capillary perfusion pressure (~30 mmHg) causes local hypoxia in the vasa nervorum, the small blood vessels that supply the nerve. This leads to **segmental demyelination** at the compression site while the axon itself remains intact.

Why the spiral groove? The radial nerve exits the posterior compartment of the upper arm by piercing the lateral intermuscular septum. At this point it lies directly against the humeral shaft with only a thin layer of fascia and subcutaneous tissue protecting it — roughly 3–5 mm of soft tissue in an average adult. The nerve is tethered at its entry and exit from the spiral groove, making it susceptible to both compression and stretch injury (traction neurapraxia).

At the cellular level: hypoxia disrupts the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump in the axolemma, causing intra-axonal sodium accumulation and depolarization block. If compression persists beyond ~2 hours, the Schwann cells begin to separate from the myelin at the nodes of Ranvier. Demyelination slows or blocks saltatory conduction. Once pressure is removed, remyelination begins — Schwann cells proliferate and wrap new myelin at a rate of roughly 1–3 mm/day, which explains the typical 4–8 week recovery window for a mid-humeral lesion (~100–150 mm from compression site to target muscle).

### Differential diagnosis — don't miss these

1. **Posterior interosseous nerve (PIN) syndrome:** Compression at the arcade of Frohse (proximal forearm). Presents with finger drop but NO wrist drop — the extensor carpi radialis longus is preserved because it's innervated before the PIN branch. This is a key clinical distinction.
2. **C7 radiculopathy:** Herniated disc or foraminal stenosis at C6–C7. Usually includes neck pain, triceps weakness (which radial nerve palsy at the spiral groove does not), and sensory changes in the C7 dermatome (middle finger).
3. **Lead neuropathy:** Classic cause of bilateral radial nerve palsy ("wrist drop of painters"). Lead disrupts heme synthesis; check blood lead level if bilateral, especially in renovators, shooters, or battery workers.
4. **Mononeuritis multiplex:** Multiple individual nerve palsies — think vasculitis, diabetes, Lyme disease, or leprosy (globally).
5. **Hereditary neuropathy with liability to pressure palsies (HNPP):** PMP22 gene deletion. Recurrent compression neuropathies at multiple sites. Suspect if this isn't the first unexplained nerve palsy.

### Diagnostic workup

| Test | What it shows | When to order |
|------|---------------|---------------|
| Clinical exam | Wrist drop, finger drop at MCP, sensory loss dorsal hand/thumb, preserved triceps. Brachioradialis reflex may be diminished. | Always — first step |
| Nerve conduction studies (NCS) | Conduction block or slowing across spiral groove. Reduced compound muscle action potential (CMAP) amplitude in radial-innervated muscles compared to unaffected side. | If no improvement at 4–6 weeks |
| Electromyography (EMG) | Fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves (denervation) appear 2–3 weeks after injury. Motor unit recruitment is reduced. Helps distinguish neuropraxia from axonotmesis and localizes lesion. | Same as NCS — they're done together |
| Ultrasound (high-res) | Nerve swelling at compression site. Cross-sectional area >12 mm² suggests pathology. Can visualize structural causes (ganglion cyst, callus). | If structural lesion suspected or NCS unclear |
| MRI | Denervation edema in radial-innervated muscles (T2 hyperintensity). Rules out mass lesions, humeral fracture sequelae. | Red flag features, failed conservative management |
| Blood lead level | Elevated in lead neuropathy (usually >40 μg/dL for neurological symptoms). | Bilateral presentation or occupational exposure |

### Evidence summary

**Natural history:** The largest case series (Friedenberg & Pfeffer, 1947; n=93) reported 90% spontaneous recovery in compression palsies. More recent prospective data (Bsteh et al., 2013) confirm >85% full recovery at 6 months for atraumatic compression with conservative management alone. No RCTs of surgical vs. conservative management exist for compressive radial neuropathy — the evidence base is observational.

**Splinting:** No RCTs comparing splint types exist, but clinical consensus (American Society for Surgery of the Hand, 2019 guidelines) recommends a static cock-up splint maintaining 20–30° wrist extension. Dynamic splints (rubber-band-assisted extension) may improve function during recovery but do not accelerate nerve healing.

**Physical therapy:** A 2018 Cochrane review found insufficient evidence for or against any specific rehabilitation protocol in peripheral nerve injuries, but expert consensus recommends passive ROM from day 1 to prevent contracture, transitioning to active-assisted exercises as motor function returns. Electrical stimulation for denervated muscle: a 2020 meta-analysis (Gordon et al.) showed modest benefit in maintaining muscle mass (mean difference 8% cross-sectional area vs. control at 6 months) but no acceleration of reinnervation.

**Corticosteroids:** No evidence for oral steroids in compressive neuropathy. One small RCT (n=44) of perineural corticosteroid injection for PIN syndrome showed no benefit over placebo at 12 weeks.

**Surgical timing:** The 3–6 month watch-and-wait threshold comes from the biology of Wallerian degeneration. If the nerve is axonotmetic (grade II) rather than neuropraxic (grade I), the proximal stump must regenerate at ~1 mm/day. For a mid-humeral lesion (~150 mm to target), 150 days = 5 months. Surgery before this window risks operating on nerves that would recover on their own. Surgery after this window risks irreversible motor endplate degeneration. The sweet spot for exploration is 4–6 months with no clinical or electrodiagnostic improvement.

### Surgical options (if indicated)

- **External neurolysis:** Freeing the nerve from surrounding scar or fibrous bands. Most common procedure for compression neuropathy with partial recovery.
- **Nerve grafting:** If a segment is irreversibly damaged (e.g., from fracture callus or laceration), sural nerve autograft bridges the gap.
- **Tendon transfers:** For long-standing palsy (>12–18 months) where motor endplates have degenerated. Classic procedure: pronator teres → extensor carpi radialis brevis (restores wrist extension), flexor carpi ulnaris → extensor digitorum communis (restores finger extension), palmaris longus → extensor pollicis longus (restores thumb extension). The Merle d'Aubigné transfer remains the standard.

### Open questions / active research

- Does early surgical decompression (at 6 weeks rather than 4 months) improve outcomes in patients with complete conduction block on NCS? No RCT exists.
- Role of high-resolution ultrasound as a prognostic tool — cross-sectional area ratio >2.0 at the compression site may predict slower recovery, but thresholds aren't validated.
- Can electrical stimulation protocols accelerate remyelination in humans as seen in rodent models? Translation gap remains.

## ::timeline::
- time: Day 1–3
  text: Remove compression source. Start wrist splint. Avoid positions that re-compress the nerve.
- time: Week 1–2
  text: Brachioradialis (the muscle that flexes your elbow with palm facing sideways) often returns first — try it and see. Numbness may start fading.
- time: Week 3–6
  text: Wrist extension begins to return. Finger extension follows. Physiotherapy starts: passive range of motion, then active exercises as strength returns.
- time: Week 6–12
  text: Most patients reach near-full recovery. Fine motor control is the last to return.
- time: 3+ months
  text: If no improvement by now, see a neurologist. Nerve conduction studies can confirm the diagnosis and rule out other causes (lead poisoning, mononeuritis multiplex, C7 radiculopathy).
