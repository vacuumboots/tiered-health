## ::gist::
### What is sinusitis?

The air-filled pockets in your face — your sinuses — are swollen and inflamed. This traps mucus, making your face feel full and sore. Sinusitis usually starts from a cold virus. Most people get better without antibiotics in 7–10 days.

### What to do right now

1. **Flush your nose with saline.** A neti pot or saline spray washes out mucus and germs. Use distilled or boiled (cooled) water only.
2. **Drink lots of fluids.** Water and warm tea thin the mucus so it drains better.
3. **Rest and sleep with your head elevated.** Propping up pillows helps your sinuses drain while you sleep.
4. **Try a decongestant spray** (oxymetazoline) — but stop after 3 days or it backfires and makes congestion worse.
5. **Take acetaminophen or ibuprofen** for face pain and headache.

::danger::
#### ⚠️ Go to the emergency room if:

- Your eye is swollen shut, red, or painful to move
- You have sudden vision changes or double vision
- You can't bend your chin to your chest (stiff neck)
- You have a high fever (over 39°C / 102°F) with severe headache
- You feel confused or unusually sleepy
::end::

::callout::
#### Good news

The vast majority of sinusitis (90%+) is caused by viruses, not bacteria. Antibiotics won't help viral sinusitis — they're only needed if you're still sick after 10 days or you got better then suddenly got worse again. Your body can handle this on its own with simple home care.
::end::

## ::adult::
### What's happening in your body

Your paranasal sinuses are four pairs of air-filled cavities in the bones of your face and skull: the **maxillary** (behind your cheekbones), **frontal** (above your eyebrows), **ethmoid** (between your eyes), and **sphenoid** (deep behind your nose). They're lined with a mucous membrane that normally produces a thin layer of mucus that traps particles and drains through small openings (ostia) into your nasal cavity.

When a virus (or, less commonly, bacteria) infects this lining, the membrane swells. The swelling blocks the narrow sinus openings — each about 2–3 mm wide — trapping mucus inside. Trapped mucus becomes a perfect breeding ground for bacteria and creates pressure inside the sinus cavity. This pressure is what causes the hallmark pain and tenderness.

The most common trigger is the **rhinovirus** (the common cold). About 1 in 30 colds leads to acute sinusitis. Allergies, dental infections, smoking, and structural issues (deviated septum, nasal polyps) increase your risk by promoting ongoing inflammation or obstructing drainage.

### Symptoms

- **Facial pressure and pain:** worse when you lean forward. Usually over the cheekbones (maxillary) or above the eyes (frontal). May feel like a toothache — the maxillary sinus roots sit right above your upper teeth.
- **Nasal congestion and discharge:** thick, colored (yellow/green) mucus. *Green mucus does not mean bacterial infection* — it's just an immune cell byproduct that happens in both viral and bacterial cases.
- **Headache:** a dull, pressure-type headache, often worse in the morning after lying flat all night.
- **Loss of smell (hyposmia):** from swelling blocking the olfactory cleft.
- **Cough:** especially at night, when postnasal drip triggers your cough reflex.
- **Fever:** low-grade (under 38.5°C / 101°F) in viral cases; higher fever suggests bacterial involvement.
- **Fatigue and malaise:** your immune system is working overtime.

### Treatment

- **Saline nasal irrigation:** This is the single most effective home treatment. A 2012 Cochrane review found strong evidence that daily saline irrigation reduces symptoms and speeds recovery. Use a neti pot or squeeze bottle with 240 mL distilled (or boiled-then-cooled) water mixed with 1 teaspoon of non-iodized salt and a pinch of baking soda. Clean the device after every use.
- **Intranasal corticosteroids:** Sprays like fluticasone (Flonase) or mometasone (Nasonex) reduce inflammation in the sinus openings. They work best when started early and used consistently. They're the first-line medical treatment recommended by the AAO-HNS guideline.
- **Decongestants:** Oral (pseudoephedrine) or topical (oxymetazoline). Topical works faster but — critical — **do not use for more than 3 days**. Longer use causes rebound congestion (rhinitis medicamentosa) that's worse than the original problem. Oral decongestants don't cause rebound but may raise blood pressure and heart rate.
- **Analgesics:** Acetaminophen or ibuprofen for pain and fever.
- **Antibiotics:** Only if bacterial sinusitis is diagnosed. The AAO-HNS guideline recommends watchful waiting for 7 days in mild cases before considering antibiotics. When prescribed, amoxicillin-clavulanate is first-line for 5–7 days in adults.

::callout::
#### What NOT to do

- Don't ask for antibiotics unnecessarily — they don't work for viral sinusitis and contribute to antibiotic resistance, which is a serious public health problem.
- Don't use decongestant spray for more than 3 days in a row. The rebound congestion is miserable.
- Don't use antihistamines unless you have proven allergies — they dry up mucus, making it thicker and harder to drain.
- Don't fly if you can avoid it — pressure changes during takeoff and landing can be extremely painful with blocked sinuses.
- Don't smoke or vape — smoke irritates the sinus lining and delays healing.
::end::

## ::timeline::
- time: Day 1–3
  text: Cold symptoms begin: runny nose, sneezing, mild sore throat. Congestion and facial pressure typically start around day 3.
- time: Day 4–7
  text: Peak sinus symptoms. Facial pain, thick nasal discharge, and headache are worst here. Start saline irrigation and intranasal steroids. Fever, if present, is usually low-grade.
- time: Day 7–10
  text: Viral sinusitis starts improving. Symptoms should be noticeably better by day 10. If you got better then suddenly got worse with new fever — this is the "double-sickening" pattern that suggests bacterial superinfection.
- time: Day 10–14
  text: If symptoms are still present and severe, or if the double-sickening pattern occurred, see a doctor. They may diagnose acute bacterial rhinosinusitis and prescribe a 5–7 day course of antibiotics.
- time: 2–4 weeks
  text: Most people are fully recovered. A lingering cough or mild congestion can persist for a few more weeks as the sinus lining heals. If symptoms last beyond 4 weeks, you may have subacute or chronic sinusitis.

## ::full::
### Pathophysiology

Acute rhinosinusitis is an inflammatory condition of the sinonasal mucosa involving the nasal cavity and at least one paranasal sinus. The initiating event is typically a **viral upper respiratory tract infection** — most commonly rhinovirus, influenza virus, parainfluenza virus, respiratory syncytial virus, or coronavirus. The virus infects the ciliated columnar epithelial cells of the sinonasal mucosa, triggering an innate immune response via **toll-like receptors (TLR3, TLR7)** and **RIG-I-like receptors**, which activate NF-κB and IRF signaling cascades. This leads to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines — IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α, and IL-8 (CXCL8) — which recruit neutrophils and cause vasodilation with increased capillary permeability.

The resulting **mucosal edema** obstructs the sinus ostia (the narrow 2–3 mm diameter drainage pathways). Obstruction creates a closed environment with **mucociliary stasis** — the ciliated epithelial cells can no longer clear mucus against the pressure gradient. Oxygen within the sinus cavity is consumed by resident immune cells, creating a **hypoxic, acidic environment (pH 6.5–7.0)** that impairs neutrophil phagocytic function and ciliary beat frequency.

In approximately **0.5–2%** of viral URIs, bacterial superinfection occurs. The most common pathogens in acute bacterial rhinosinusitis (ABRS) are **Streptococcus pneumoniae (30–40%)**, **Haemophilus influenzae (20–30%)**, **Moraxella catarrhalis (10–15%)**, and **Streptococcus pyogenes (5–10%)**. Anaerobic organisms are more common in chronic sinusitis or odontogenic sources. The diagnosis of ABRS rests not on culture but on clinical criteria — either persistent symptoms beyond 10 days without improvement, severe symptoms (fever ≥39°C + purulent discharge + facial pain for 3–4 consecutive days), or a double-sickening (worsening) pattern.

**Chronic rhinosinusitis (CRS)** — symptoms lasting ≥12 weeks — represents a different disease process. CRS is subclassified into CRSwNP (with nasal polyps) and CRSsNP (without polyps). The pathophysiology involves a dysregulated immune response with eosinophilic inflammation, type 2 cytokine skewing (IL-4, IL-5, IL-13), tissue remodeling, and biofilm formation by bacteria such as *Staphylococcus aureus*. CRS is not simply acute sinusitis that didn't resolve.

### Differential diagnosis

1. **Viral URI (common cold):** Symptoms peak at 1–3 days and improve by day 7–10. Facial pressure is mild or absent. Sore throat and cough are more prominent. Differentiating viral from bacterial rhinosinusitis is the most important clinical challenge — and guidelines emphasize that time course, not mucus color, is the key.
2. **Allergic rhinitis:** Itchy, watery eyes, sneezing paroxysms, clear thin discharge. Symptoms are seasonal or triggered by specific allergens. No fever or facial pressure. Eosinophils on nasal smear.
3. **Migraine (with autonomic symptoms):** Unilateral throbbing headache with nasal congestion, rhinorrhea, and lacrimation on the same side (cranial autonomic symptoms). The key distinction: migraine pain is pulsatile and episodic, sinusitis pain is constant pressure. Up to 40% of patients presenting to ENT with "sinus headache" actually have migraine.
4. **Odontogenic (dental) infection:** Maxillary sinusitis from an infected upper molar or premolar. The roots of the maxillary teeth project into the floor of the maxillary sinus. Unilateral foul-smelling discharge, unilateral pain, and a recent dental history are clues. Requires dental evaluation and treatment of the source.
5. **Invasive fungal sinusitis:** A medical emergency seen almost exclusively in immunocompromised patients (uncontrolled diabetes with ketoacidosis, neutropenia, transplant recipients). Caused by Mucorales or Aspergillus species. Presents with fever, facial pain, black necrotic eschar on the turbinates. Tissue invasion can extend to the orbit and brain within hours. Mortality >50% without urgent surgical debridement.
6. **Nasal polyps:** Bilateral translucent grape-like masses in the nasal cavity. Cause chronic congestion, anosmia, and facial pressure. Associated with asthma, NSAID-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD/N-ERD), and cystic fibrosis. Seen on nasal endoscopy.
7. **Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) rhinorrhea:** Clear, watery unilateral discharge that tests positive for beta-2 transferrin. Usually follows head trauma or skull base surgery. The fluid is thin and salty — not thick like sinus mucus.

### Diagnostic workup

| Test | What it shows | When to order |
|------|---------------|---------------|
| Clinical history and exam | Key criteria: symptom duration >10 days, double-sickening, severe presentation. Nasal endoscopy may show purulence at the middle meatus. | Every patient — this alone is sufficient for diagnosis in most cases |
| Anterior rhinoscopy (speculum exam) | Mucosal edema, discharge color/consistency, polyp visualization | Quick bedside assessment |
| Nasal endoscopy (rigid or flexible scope) | Purulence from the middle meatus or sphenoethmoidal recess (gold standard for confirming sinus origin). Identifies polyps, structural abnormalities. | Patients with recurrent or chronic symptoms, uncertain diagnosis, or who fail initial management |
| CT sinuses (non-contrast, limited coronal) | Mucosal thickening (>4 mm), air-fluid levels, complete sinus opacification, bony erosion. Incidental sinus findings are common — up to 40% of asymptomatic people have mucosal thickening on CT. | NOT for uncomplicated acute sinusitis (AAO-HNS strongly recommends against). Reserve for: suspected complications, chronic sinusitis, pre-surgical planning, or atypical presentations |
| Ultrasonography of sinuses | Fluid levels or mucosal thickening in maxillary/frontal sinuses | Rarely used in clinical practice — operator-dependent and less sensitive than CT |
| Sinus aspiration / culture (maxillary antral tap) | Identifies specific bacterial pathogen and sensitivities | Reserved for: immunocompromised patients, treatment failure after two antibiotic courses, orbital/intracranial complications, or nosocomial sinusitis |
| Allergy testing (skin prick or specific IgE) | Identifies atopic triggers | Recurrent acute sinusitis or chronic rhinosinusitis with suspected allergic component |
| Sweat chloride test / CFTR genetic testing | Elevated chloride (>60 mmol/L) or CFTR mutation | CRS in a child or young adult, especially with nasal polyps, recurrent pneumonia, or male infertility |
| ESR / CRP | Markers of systemic inflammation. CRP >10 mg/L is more suggestive of bacterial than viral illness | When distinguishing viral from bacterial disease in equivocal cases |

### Evidence summary

**Clinical guidelines:** The AAO-HNS Clinical Practice Guideline: Adult Sinusitis (Rosenfeld et al., 2015; updated 2025) is the most widely adopted North American guideline. It strongly recommends saline irrigation and intranasal corticosteroids as first-line management. It recommends against imaging for uncomplicated acute sinusitis. It recommends watchful waiting (7 days) before considering antibiotics for mild cases. The IDSA guideline (Chow et al., 2012) uses stricter criteria for ABRS diagnosis (persistent symptoms ≥10 days, or severe onset, or double worsening) and recommends amoxicillin-clavulanate as first-line therapy. The EPOS2020 guideline (Fokkens et al., 2020) takes a similar approach with a strong emphasis on symptom duration and severity to guide antibiotic prescribing.

**Antibiotics for acute sinusitis:** The Cochrane review by Lemiengre et al. (2012, update 2018) analyzed 15 RCTs (n=3,273) and found that antibiotics provide a modest benefit — number needed to treat (NNT) for cure at 7–15 days was 18. Compared to 1 in 18 patients benefiting, the number needed to harm from adverse effects was 8. In other words, antibiotics cause more side effects than they cure in the average patient with acute sinusitis. Subgroup analysis showed greater benefit in patients with confirmed bacterial infection (purulence on endoscopy + symptoms >10 days).

**Intranasal corticosteroids:** A Cochrane review by Zalmanovici Trestioreanu & Yaphe (2013) included 6 RCTs (n=2,523) and found that intranasal corticosteroids increased the rate of symptom resolution or improvement compared to placebo (RR 1.11, 95% CI 1.04–1.18). The effect was modest but consistent, with no significant adverse effects. They are recommended as first-line medical therapy by both AAO-HNS and EPOS guidelines.

**Saline irrigation:** A Cochrane review by Harvey et al. (2007) found that saline irrigation improves symptom scores in chronic sinusitis and is associated with improved quality of life. For acute sinusitis, a 2012 meta-analysis found a significant reduction in symptom severity with daily saline irrigation. It is the most widely recommended non-pharmacological intervention.

**Decongestants:** A systematic review by Hox et al. (2010) found limited evidence from small trials supporting the use of topical decongestants for symptom relief in acute sinusitis. However, the risk of rhinitis medicamentosa with prolonged use is well-documented. The AAO-HNS guideline suggests a max of 3 days for topical decongestants. No high-quality evidence supports oral decongestants specifically for sinusitis — they are an adjunctive option for nasal congestion.

### Surgical options (if indicated)

Surgery is rarely indicated for acute bacterial rhinosinusitis. Indications for surgical management include:

- **Orbital or intracranial complication:** Abscess (subperiosteal, orbital, epidural, subdural, brain) requiring surgical drainage alongside intravenous antibiotics. This is an emergency — the most common route of spread is through the lamina papyracea (the thin bone separating the ethmoid sinus from the orbit).
- **Acute bacterial rhinosinusitis refractory to maximal medical therapy:** Failure of two courses of guideline-directed antibiotics in a patient with CT-confirmed sinusitis. Functional endoscopic sinus surgery (FESS) opens the obstructed sinus ostia and drains purulent material.
- **Chronic rhinosinusitis (CRS) after failed medical therapy:** FESS with ethmoidectomy, maxillary antrostomy, and/or frontal sinusotomy after 8–12 weeks of maximal medical therapy (intranasal corticosteroids, saline irrigation, ± oral corticosteroids, ± antibiotics). FESS improves quality of life in CRS patients — a multicenter RCT (Smith et al., 2010) showed clinically meaningful improvement in SNOT-22 scores at 6 months (mean reduction 28 points vs. 12 points with continued medical therapy alone).
- **Invasive fungal sinusitis:** Urgent endoscopic debridement of all necrotic tissue combined with systemic antifungal therapy.
- **Antrochoanal polyp:** A solitary polyp originating from the maxillary sinus and extending into the choana. Requires endoscopic removal of both the polyp and its antral origin.

### Open questions / active research

- Can biomarkers (nasal nitrite, IL-8, CRP, procalcitonin) accurately distinguish viral from bacterial sinusitis at the point of care? Procalcitonin-guided algorithms show promise in respiratory infections broadly, but specific data for sinusitis is limited.
- What is the optimal duration of antibiotic therapy for ABRS? Current guidelines recommend 5–7 days (AAO-HNS) to 5–10 days (IDSA), but shorter courses may be equally effective with fewer adverse effects and lower resistance pressure.
- Does saline irrigation prevent recurrence in patients with recurrent acute sinusitis? Biologically plausible, but prospective data are lacking.
- What is the role of the sinonasal microbiome in the transition from acute to chronic rhinosinusitis? Loss of microbial diversity (dysbiosis) may be a driver, but causal evidence is missing.
- Are biologic therapies (dupilumab, omalizumab, mepolizumab) effective for CRS without polyps? They show clear benefit for CRSwNP (dupilumab is FDA-approved), but evidence for CRSsNP is mixed and ongoing.
