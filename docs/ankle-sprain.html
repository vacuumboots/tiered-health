🦶 Ankle Sprain

Also called: twisted ankle · rolled ankle · lateral ankle sprain

Anatomy

Grade 6 reading level

What is an ankle sprain?

Ligaments on the outside of your ankle stretched or tore when your foot rolled inward too far. It's the most common sports injury — about 2 million people in the US twist an ankle every year. Most heal fine with rest and time.

What to do right now

  1. Stop and rest. Don't walk on it if it hurts. You can't "walk it off."
  2. Ice it. Wrap ice in a towel and hold it on the sore spot for 15–20 minutes every 2–3 hours. This helps with swelling and pain.
  3. Wrap it. An elastic bandage (ACE wrap) gives support. Wrap from the toes upward, not too tight. If your toes turn blue or go numb, it's too tight.
  4. Elevate it. Prop your ankle up on pillows above heart level when sitting or lying down. This drains swelling.
  5. Take ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) if you can take it safely — it helps both pain and swelling.
⚠️ Go to the emergency room if:
  • You cannot put any weight on your ankle at all
  • The ankle looks bent or twisted wrong
  • You heard a "pop" and felt something tear at the moment of injury
  • The foot is cold, blue, or numb (could be a dislocation)
  • You have severe pain that gets worse over hours despite rest and ice
Good news

Most ankle sprains (80–90%) heal with simple home care and time. Grade I sprains feel better in 1–3 weeks. Even grade III (complete tear) sprains often heal without surgery. What matters most is doing rehab exercises once the pain allows — this cuts your risk of spraining it again in half.

Grade 12 reading level

What's happening in your body

Your ankle is a hinge joint formed by three bones: the tibia (shin bone), fibula (the thin bone beside it), and talus (a block-shaped bone in your foot). Three ligaments on the outside — the anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL), calcaneofibular ligament (CFL), and posterior talofibular ligament (PTFL) — keep the ankle from rolling too far inward.

When you roll your ankle (an inversion injury), the foot turns inward and the outside ligaments get stretched beyond their limit. The ATFL is almost always the first to go — it's the weakest and takes the most force when the foot is pointed down (plantarflexed). If the force continues, the CFL tears next, and in the worst cases the PTFL goes too.

Doctors grade sprains by how badly the ligaments are damaged:

  • Grade I (mild): The ligament is stretched but not torn. You have mild pain and slight swelling. You can walk but it hurts. Recovery: 1–3 weeks.
  • Grade II (moderate): The ligament is partially torn. Moderate pain, noticeable swelling and bruising. Walking is painful and you may limp. The joint feels a little loose. Recovery: 3–6 weeks.
  • Grade III (severe): The ligament is completely torn. Severe pain, major swelling, and bruising that spreads down into the foot. You can't bear weight. The joint feels unstable. Recovery: 8–12 weeks.

Symptoms

  • Pain on the outside of the ankle: worst directly over the ligament, especially when you press on it or try to turn your foot inward.
  • Swelling: develops within minutes to hours. The ankle looks puffy and feels tight.
  • Bruising (ecchymosis): blood from torn vessels seeps into the tissue. Bruising often spreads down toward the heel and toes over the first few days.
  • Difficulty bearing weight: from mild discomfort (grade I) to impossible (grade III).
  • A popping or tearing sensation at the time of injury — this often means a grade II or III tear.
  • Feeling of instability: the ankle feels "loose" or like it might give way, especially on uneven ground.

Treatment

  • RICE protocol (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation): the standard first-aid for the first 48–72 hours. Ice reduces pain and limits swelling. Compression (ACE wrap) supports the joint and controls edema. Elevation uses gravity to drain fluid.
  • Functional bracing: Ankle braces (lace-up, stirrup, or semi-rigid) are better than a rigid cast or walking boot for most sprains. They protect the ligaments while allowing early movement, which leads to faster recovery and less stiffness. Studies show functional treatment beats immobilization.
  • NSAIDs: Ibuprofen or naproxen reduce pain and inflammation. Take them for 3–7 days as needed. Topical NSAID gels (diclofenac) work well with fewer stomach side effects.
  • Weight-bearing as tolerated: As pain allows, start putting weight on the ankle. Early weight-bearing promotes healing and prevents muscle wasting. Crutches can help initially for grade II–III sprains.
  • Rehabilitation exercises: Once the acute pain subsides (usually after a few days), start range-of-motion exercises (spelling the alphabet with your toes), then balance training (standing on one foot), then strengthening (resistance bands). This is the most important step for preventing re-injury.
What NOT to do
  • Don't use heat in the first 72 hours — it increases swelling and bleeding.
  • Don't keep walking on a sprained ankle hoping it will go away. This delays healing and can turn a grade I into a grade II by repeatedly re-injuring the stretched ligament.
  • Don't skip rehab. People who don't do balance exercises are 5 times more likely to re-injure the same ankle within a year.
  • Don't drink alcohol — it worsens swelling and impairs healing.
  • Don't assume "it's just a sprain" — a broken fibula (malleolus fracture) can look identical to a bad sprain. If you can't bear weight, get an X-ray.

Recovery Timeline

Day 1–3
Acute phase. Rest, ice, compression, elevation. Avoid weight-bearing if painful. Start NSAIDs. A brace or elastic wrap provides support.
Day 4–7
Swelling peaks and begins to subside. Start gentle range-of-motion exercises (ankle pumps, alphabet ABCs). Begin partial weight-bearing as tolerated.
Week 2–3
Most pain and swelling gone for grade I. Start balance training (single-leg stance) and resistance band strengthening. Gradually return to walking.
Week 3–6
Grade II sprains reach this stage. Progress to sport-specific exercises, jogging on flat surfaces, and agility drills. Most people return to full activity.
Week 8–12
Grade III sprains recover by this point. Full strengthening and proprioceptive training. Brace during sports for 3–6 months to prevent re-injury.
Best current evidence

Pathophysiology

An ankle sprain is an acute ligamentous injury caused by a supination-inversion mechanism. When the foot is plantarflexed and forcibly inverted, the lateral ligament complex is stressed beyond its tensile limits. The anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL) is the first structure injured — it's the weakest of the three lateral ligaments (mean ultimate tensile load ~139 N) and is maximally stressed when the ankle is in plantarflexion, which is the joint position at heel strike during gait.

If the deforming force continues, the calcaneofibular ligament (CFL) is next (mean tensile load ~346 N). The posterior talofibular ligament (PTFL) rarely tears unless the ankle is dislocated, making an isolated PTFL injury extremely uncommon.

At the cellular level: the injury triggers an acute inflammatory cascade. Damaged endothelial cells and extravasated platelets release bradykinin, prostaglandins, and histamine, causing vasodilation and increased capillary permeability. Neutrophils migrate to the site within 6–12 hours, followed by macrophages that phagocytose debris. The ligament enters the proliferative phase at roughly 48–72 hours, with fibroblasts depositing type III collagen (weaker, more disorganized than type I). Over the next 6–12 weeks, the matrix remodels toward type I collagen alignment along lines of tensile stress — but the healed ligament never regains its original strength, reaching at most 80–90% of pre-injury tensile properties.

Recurrent sprains lead to ligamentous laxity and chronic ankle instability (CAI). Mechanical instability (ligamentous looseness) combines with proprioceptive deficits — damage to mechanoreceptors (Ruffini endings, Pacinian corpuscles, Golgi tendon organs) in the ligament disrupts the afferent feedback loop to the peroneal muscles, delaying the evertor reflex by ~5–15 ms. This delay is enough to miss the protective window during a landing or cutting motion, creating a vicious cycle of recurrent injury. Up to 40% of first-time ankle sprains develop CAI.

Differential diagnosis

  1. Lateral malleolus fracture (Weber A/B): Tenderness directly over the fibula bone, not the ligament. Ottawa Ankle Rules help distinguish: X-ray needed if there is bone tenderness at the posterior edge of the distal 6 cm of the fibula or tibia, or inability to bear weight for 4 steps.
  2. Syndesmotic (high) ankle sprain: Injury to the tibiofibular syndesmosis. Pain is above the ankle joint, not on the lateral ligaments. Worse with forced dorsiflexion and external rotation (squeeze test, external rotation test). Much longer recovery — typically 6–12 weeks.
  3. Peroneal tendon subluxation/dislocation: A snap or pop felt laterally, with tenderness behind the fibula (retromalleolar groove). The peroneal tendons dislocate over the fibula during ankle dorsiflexion-eversion. Presents with lateral snapping and weakness.
  4. Osteochondral lesion of the talus (OLT): Bruised or fractured cartilage on the talar dome from the impaction at injury. Presents as persistent deep ankle pain and catching/locking weeks after the initial sprain. Requires MRI for diagnosis.
  5. Avulsion fracture of the base of the 5th metatarsal: Caused by peroneus brevis pulling off its attachment during the inversion injury. Tenderness at the lateral midfoot, not the ankle ligaments.
  6. Gout / pseudogout: Can mimic ankle sprain. Key clues: no injury mechanism, redness and warmth disproportionate to swelling, rapid onset over hours, history of flares. Check serum uric acid and joint aspiration.

Evidence summary

Incidence and epidemiology: Ankle sprain is the most common sports injury, accounting for 10–15% of all athletic injuries. The meta-analysis by Doherty et al. (2014, n=181 prospective studies) reported an incidence of 11.55 per 1,000 exposures in high-quality studies, with females at higher risk than males (13.6 vs 6.94 per 1,000 exposures). Indoor/court sports had the highest rates at 7 per 1,000 exposures. The lifetime prevalence is estimated at 25–30% of the physically active population.

Functional treatment vs. immobilization: The Cochrane review by Kerkhoffs et al. (2002) comparing functional treatment (early mobilization, bracing, exercises) with immobilization (cast or splint) found that functional treatment led to faster return to work (mean difference 4.4 days), less persistent swelling, and greater patient satisfaction. There was no difference in long-term mechanical instability. A 2018 update confirmed these findings.

NSAIDs: A systematic review by van den Bekerom et al. (2012) found strong evidence that topical NSAIDs are as effective as oral NSAIDs for acute ankle sprain with fewer gastrointestinal side effects. Oral NSAIDs (including COX-2 inhibitors) provided superior pain relief to placebo in the first 3–7 days.

Rehabilitation and re-injury prevention: Exercise-based rehabilitation reduces re-injury prevalence at 12 months by 40% compared to usual care (OR 0.60, 95% CI 0.36–0.99), according to a 2022 meta-analysis of 14 RCTs (n=2,182). The Doherty et al. (2017) overview of systematic reviews found strong evidence for bracing and moderate evidence for neuromuscular training in preventing recurrence.

Clinical practice guidelines: The 2022 PRISMA systematic review of CPGs by Ruiz-Sánchez et al. evaluated 7 clinical practice guidelines and identified 6 recommendations with sufficient evidence to be applied in practice: Ottawa Ankle Rules for triage, manual therapy, cryotherapy, functional supports, early ambulation, short-term NSAIDs, and rehabilitation.

Ligament healing biology: Histological studies (Hubbard & Hicks-Little, 2008) show that ligament fibroblasts proliferate maximally at 3 weeks and remodel for up to 12 months. The healed ligament contains a higher proportion of type III collagen (less organized, weaker) than uninjured ligament.

Surgical options

Surgery is rarely needed for acute ankle sprains. Indications for lateral ligament repair (Broström procedure or modified Broström-Gould) include:

  • Failed conservative management: Persistent mechanical instability after 3–6 months of supervised rehabilitation.
  • High-grade (III) sprain in a high-level athlete: Some evidence supports acute repair in elite athletes to shorten return-to-sport time, but this is controversial and most return-to-sport outcomes are equivalent with functional rehabilitation.
  • Concomitant injuries: Osteochondral lesion requiring debridement, peroneal tendon pathology, or syndesmotic widening.
  • Recurrent instability (CAI): The modified Broström-Gould procedure has a 90–95% success rate in returning patients to pre-injury activity levels (recurrence rate 5–10%). Anatomic repair is preferred over tenodesis (Evans, Watson-Jones, Chrisman-Snook) because it preserves normal ankle kinematics better.

Post-operative rehabilitation includes 2 weeks in a splint, then functional bracing with progressive weight-bearing, returning to sport at 4–6 months.

Open questions / active research

  • What is the optimal dose of rehabilitation exercise (frequency, duration, type) for preventing CAI? Current data shows a 40% reduction in re-injury with exercise, but the dose-response gradient is unknown.
  • Do biologic augmentations (platelet-rich plasma, stem cells) improve ligament healing in grade III sprains? Early RCTs are inconclusive — PRP did not improve functional outcomes over placebo in a 2021 trial.
  • Can high-resolution ultrasound predict which patients will develop CAI? Preliminary studies show ATFL cross-sectional area >20 mm² at 6 weeks predicts chronic instability, but thresholds lack external validation.
  • What is the optimal brace-wearing protocol after return to sport? Evidence supports 3–6 months during high-risk activity but wearing protocols are not standardized.
  • Does early weight-bearing change ligament remodeling at the histological level in humans? Animal models suggest mechanotransduction improves collagen alignment, but human biopsy data is absent.
