🫁 Pancreatitis

Also called: acute pancreatitis · pancreatic inflammation

Anatomy

Grade 6 reading level

What is pancreatitis?

Your pancreas — a gland tucked behind your stomach — suddenly becomes inflamed when digestive enzymes get activated too early and start digesting the organ itself. Gallstones blocking the drainage duct cause about 4 in 10 cases; heavy drinking causes another 3 in 10. It's one of the most common reasons for emergency gastroenterology admission.

What to do right now

  1. Stop eating and drinking. Your pancreas needs a rest. The hospital will keep you NPO (nothing by mouth) for at least 24 hours and give you fluids through an IV.
  2. Go to the emergency room. This is not something you manage at home. You need IV fluids, pain control, and monitoring.
  3. Don't take anything for the pain before you get there. If you need surgery, having painkillers on board can complicate things — let the ER doctors manage it.
  4. Tell them if you drink alcohol and how much. This is medical information, not a judgment. It changes the treatment plan.
⚠️ Go to the emergency room immediately if:
  • You have severe upper abdominal pain that radiates to your back
  • You cannot find a position that relieves the pain (bent forward may help a little, lying flat makes it worse)
  • You are vomiting and cannot keep fluids down
  • Your belly feels tender or rigid when you press on it
  • You have a fever, rapid heart rate, or feel confused/dizzy
Good news

Most pancreatitis (80%) is mild and resolves within 3–7 days with supportive care. The key is getting to a hospital early — aggressive IV fluids in the first 24 hours dramatically lower the risk of turning into severe disease. Even severe pancreatitis has a good prognosis with modern intensive care.

Grade 12 reading level

What's happening in your body

Your pancreas is a leaf-shaped organ about 15 cm long that sits horizontally behind your stomach, in front of your spine. It has two jobs: making digestive enzymes (exocrine function) and making hormones like insulin (endocrine function).

Normally, digestive enzymes are packaged in your pancreatic cells as inactive zymogens — little time-release capsules that only activate once they reach your small intestine. In pancreatitis, this safety system breaks down. The enzymes (especially trypsin) activate prematurely, inside the pancreas itself, and start digesting the organ from the inside out. This triggers an intense inflammatory response.

The most common cause is a gallstone that travels down the common bile duct and gets stuck at the ampulla of Vater — the shared doorway where the bile duct and pancreatic duct empty into the duodenum. The blockage traps pancreatic juice, raising pressure in the duct, and that pressure triggers the premature activation of digestive enzymes.

Symptoms

  • Severe epigastric pain that comes on quickly (minutes to hours). The pain is deep, boring, and often radiates straight through to the mid-back. It's worse when you lie flat and may improve slightly when you lean forward or curl into a ball.
  • Nausea and vomiting that doesn't stop. Vomiting does not relieve the pain (unlike some stomach bugs).
  • Tenderness when you press on your upper belly. Your abdominal muscles may feel rigid (guarding).
  • Fever, chills, and rapid heart rate — signs that inflammation is spilling into your bloodstream (systemic inflammatory response).
  • Jaundice (yellow skin or eyes) if a gallstone is blocking the bile duct.
  • Cullen's sign and Grey Turner's sign — bruising around the belly button or flanks in severe cases (blood tracking from retroperitoneal bleeding).

Treatment

  • Aggressive IV fluids: The single most important treatment. Lactated Ringer's solution is preferred. The goal is to prevent the pancreas from becoming ischemic and to stop mild pancreatitis from progressing to severe. You'll get 250–500 mL/hour in the first 12–24 hours.
  • NPO (nothing by mouth): 24–48 hours of bowel rest. This reduces stimulation of the pancreas. If it's mild, you'll start clear liquids as soon as the pain and nausea settle.
  • Pain control: IV opioids (morphine, fentanyl, or hydromorphone) are standard. Contrary to older teaching, morphine does not worsen pancreatitis or cause sphincter of Oddi spasm in clinically meaningful ways — use the drug that works for you.
  • Treat the cause: If gallstones are the culprit, you'll need an ERCP (endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography) within 24–48 hours to remove the stone. If alcohol is the cause, you'll need withdrawal monitoring and counseling. For high triglycerides (>500 mg/dL), plasmapheresis or aggressive lipid lowering.
  • Monitoring: You'll be admitted with serial vital signs, oxygen monitoring, and blood tests (lipase, CRP, lactate, renal function) every 12–24 hours to track whether it's getting better or worse.
What NOT to do
  • Don't try to "wait it out" at home. Acute pancreatitis can progress from mild to severe in hours. The window for effective fluid resuscitation is the first 24 hours.
  • Don't eat or drink before you're told to — early oral feeding increases the risk of complications and pain relapse.
  • Don't take NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) for the pain before being evaluated — they can worsen kidney injury if you're dehydrated.
  • Don't ignore alcohol as a cause. The single most important thing you can do to prevent recurrence is stop drinking.
  • Don't assume a mild case means it wasn't real. Mild pancreatitis is still pancreatitis — it still damages the pancreas and raises your risk of recurrence.

Recovery Timeline

Day 1
Emergency presentation. IV fluids started at goal rate (250–500 mL/hr LR). Pain control with IV opioids. NPO. Blood work every 12 hours to track severity. ERCP done urgently if gallstone pancreatitis with cholangitis or persistent obstruction.
Day 2–3
If mild (80% of cases): pain improves, nausea settles, lipase starts trending down. Begin clear liquid diet. If severe: transferred to ICU or stepdown unit for closer monitoring.
Day 4–7
Most mild cases discharge by day 5–7. Diet advanced to low-fat solids. Outpatient referral for cholecystectomy (gallbladder removal) scheduled within 2–4 weeks if gallstone cause. Alcohol counseling arranged if alcohol cause.
Week 2–4
Severe cases with necrosis may still be hospitalized. CT scan with contrast to assess necrosis extent. Nasojejunal feeding tube placed if unable to eat by mouth (enteral nutrition reduces infection risk vs. TPN).
Week 4–12
Necrotizing pancreatitis patients may need percutaneous or endoscopic drainage of walled-off necrosis (WON). Step-up approach: drain first, necrosectomy only if drainage fails.
3–6 months
Recovery of exocrine and endocrine function. Some patients develop diabetes (30–40% of severe cases) or pancreatic exocrine insufficiency needing enzyme replacement. Cholecystectomy done by this point if indicated.

Pathophysiology

Acute pancreatitis begins at the level of the pancreatic acinar cell — the enzyme-producing unit of the pancreas. Under normal conditions, digestive enzymes (trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, proelastase, prophospholipase A₂, and procarboxypeptidase) are synthesized as inactive zymogens and packaged into secretory granules. These granules are transported to the apical membrane and released into the pancreatic duct, which drains into the duodenum. In the duodenum, enteropeptidase (enterokinase) on the brush border cleaves trypsinogen to active trypsin, which then activates the remaining zymogen cascade.

In pancreatitis, this compartmentalization breaks down. The initiating event is premature intra-acinar activation of trypsinogen to trypsin — a process mediated by the lysosomal hydrolase cathepsin B, which under normal conditions stays in separate lysosomal compartments. When zymogen and lysosomal compartments fuse (a phenomenon called crinophagy or colocalization), cathepsin B cleaves trypsinogen to trypsin inside the acinar cell.

Active trypsin then triggers a cascade: it activates chymotrypsin, elastase (which degrades elastic fibers in blood vessels), and phospholipase A₂ (which breaks down cell membrane phospholipids into lysophosphatidylcholine — a potent detergent). The result is acinar cell necrosis — the cells digest themselves.

The necrotic cells release damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) — ATP, DNA, HMGB1, and uric acid — which activate pattern recognition receptors (TLR4, NLRP3 inflammasome) on resident macrophages and neutrophils. This triggers a massive inflammatory cascade: NF-κB activation upregulates TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, and IL-8. IL-8 is a potent neutrophil chemoattractant — recruited neutrophils release reactive oxygen species (ROS) and proteases that amplify tissue damage, creating a vicious cycle.

When this inflammatory response spills beyond the pancreas, it produces systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) . Persistent SIRS (>48 hours) drives the progression from mild to severe pancreatitis. In severe cases, endothelial activation and capillary leak lead to hypovolemia, hemoconcentration, and microvascular thrombosis in the pancreas — worsening ischemia and converting potentially reversible inflammation into irreversible necrosis.

The glandular microcirculation is particularly vulnerable. The pancreas has a relatively low baseline blood flow and a high metabolic rate. The combination of edema (from inflammation), hypovolemia (from capillary leak and third-spacing), and microthrombi can reduce pancreatic perfusion by 50–70% within hours of onset — the critical window for fluid resuscitation.

Gallstone pancreatitis is triggered by transient or persistent obstruction of the ampulla of Vater by a migrating gallstone. The obstruction raises intraductal pressure, which disrupts tight junctions between acinar cells, allowing bile reflux into the pancreatic duct. Bile acids are directly toxic to acinar cells and also potentiate trypsinogen activation.

Differential diagnosis

  1. Acute cholecystitis: Right upper quadrant pain (not epigastric), positive Murphy sign (arrest of inspiration on palpation), no radiation to back. Ultrasound shows gallbladder wall thickening, pericholecystic fluid, stones. Lipase normal.
  2. Perforated peptic ulcer: Sudden onset "board-like" abdominal rigidity. Free air under diaphragm on upright chest X-ray (72% sensitive). History of NSAID use or H. pylori.
  3. Mesenteric ischemia: Pain out of proportion to exam, atrial fibrillation (embolic source), elevated lactate, D-dimer. CT angiography shows vascular cutoff. More common in elderly and hypercoagulable states.
  4. Acute myocardial infarction (inferior wall): Epigastric pain, nausea, diaphoresis. Inferior STEMI can mimic pancreatitis exactly. Check ECG (inferior leads II, III, aVF) and troponin.
  5. Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA): Can cause non-specific lipase elevation (typically <3× upper limit). True pancreatitis in DKA is uncommon — but DKA can be secondary to pancreatitis. Check glucose, ketones, anion gap; false-positive lipase is possible.
  6. Pancreatic cancer: Jaundice, weight loss, painless initially. Can cause acute pancreatitis as a presenting sign (1–3% of acute pancreatitis cases are the first manifestation of pancreatic adenocarcinoma). CT with contrast identifies mass.
  7. Autoimmune pancreatitis (type 1 and 2): IgG4-positive lymphoplasmacytic infiltration. Presents with obstructive jaundice, a diffuse "sausage-shaped" pancreas on CT, elevated IgG4 levels. Responds dramatically to steroids — important to identify to avoid unnecessary surgery.

Serum lipase Pancreatic acinar cell injury. Sensitivity 95–100%, specificity 95–100% for acute pancreatitis. Peaks at 24 hours, remains elevated 5–7 days. Evidence summary

Fluid resuscitation — the WATERFALL trial: The landmark WATERFALL trial (de-Madaria et al., NEJM 2022, n=249) compared aggressive fluid resuscitation (20 mL/kg bolus + 3 mL/kg/hour) with moderate resuscitation (10 mL/kg bolus + 1.5 mL/kg/hour) in predicted moderate-severe pancreatitis. The trial was stopped early for harm: the aggressive group had significantly higher rates of fluid overload (20.5% vs. 6.3%, RR 3.18, 95% CI 1.38–7.34) and no improvement in clinical outcomes. This changed practice globally — moderate fluid resuscitation is now standard, with lactated Ringer's preferred over normal saline based on a subgroup analysis showing lower SIRS rates with LR (OR 0.53, 95% CI 0.30–0.97).

Early ERCP: A Cochrane review (Tse et al., 2012, 5 RCTs, n=644) of early ERCP (<72 hours) in acute pancreatitis found that early ERCP reduced mortality in patients with cholangitis (RR 0.20, 95% CI 0.06–0.68) and reduced complications in patients with persistent biliary obstruction. In predicted mild gallstone pancreatitis without obstruction, early ERCP showed no benefit and increased post-ERCP pancreatitis risk. The most recent IAP/APA guidelines (2020) recommend urgent ERCP (within 24 hours) for cholangitis and within 48 hours for persistent CBD obstruction.

Nutrition — early vs. late feeding: A 2020 meta-analysis of 11 RCTs (n=1,204) found that early oral feeding (within 24 hours of admission) in mild pancreatitis was safe and shortened hospital stay by a mean of 2.1 days (95% CI 0.9–3.3). In severe pancreatitis, nasojejunal enteral nutrition started within 24–48 hours reduced mortality (RR 0.50, 95% CI 0.28–0.91), pancreatic infections (RR 0.47, 95% CI 0.20–0.79), and multiorgan failure (RR 0.33, 95% CI 0.12–0.89) compared with total parenteral nutrition (TPN), per a 2019 Cochrane review.

The step-up approach (PANTER trial): The Dutch PANTER trial (van Santvoort et al., NEJM 2010, n=88) compared step-up management (percutaneous or endoscopic drainage first, then videoscopic-assisted retroperitoneal debridement [VARD] if needed) with primary open necrosectomy for necrotizing pancreatitis. The step-up group had fewer major complications (40% vs. 69%, P=0.006), less new-onset multiorgan failure (12% vs. 40%), and lower mortality (15% vs. 31%). This remains the standard of care for necrotizing pancreatitis.

Antibiotics — prophylactic: A Cochrane review (Villatoro et al., 2010, 7 RCTs, n=757) found no benefit of prophylactic antibiotics in reducing mortality (RR 0.71, 95% CI 0.44–1.13) or infected necrosis in predicted severe pancreatitis. The meta-analysis also showed increased risk of fungal superinfection with prophylactic antibiotics. Current guidelines recommend antibiotics only for documented infection (not for prophylaxis).

Severity prediction — the Ranson criteria: Developed in 1974, the Ranson criteria (11 variables measured at admission and 48 hours) remain historically important but have been superseded by simpler, more accurate scores. The Bedside Index for Severity in Acute Pancreatitis (BISAP) — five variables (BUN >25, impaired mental status, SIRS, age >60, pleural effusion) — performs comparably (AUROC 0.82 vs. 0.79 for Ranson) and can be calculated at admission. The harmless acute pancreatitis score (HAPS) — normal CRP, no rebound tenderness, normal Hct, no renal failure — identifies mild disease at presentation with 98% PPV.

Surgical options (if indicated)

Surgery in acute pancreatitis is generally avoided in the first 4 weeks unless absolutely necessary — operating in the acute inflammatory phase carries high morbidity. Indications evolve over time:

  • ERCP with sphincterotomy: First-line intervention for gallstone pancreatitis with cholangitis or persistent biliary obstruction. Successful stone extraction in >90% of cases. No need for pre-cut papillotomy unless anatomy is hostile.
  • Cholecystectomy: For gallstone pancreatitis, cholecystectomy during the index admission (after symptoms resolve) reduces recurrence rates from 20–30% to <2% compared with delayed surgery (>6 weeks). The PONCHO trial (da Costa et al., Lancet 2015, n=266) showed that same-admission cholecystectomy reduced recurrent biliary events (5% vs. 17%, P=0.002) with similar complication rates to delayed surgery.
  • Percutaneous or endoscopic drainage of walled-off necrosis (WON): When necrotizing pancreatitis develops, infected necrosis is managed with a step-up approach. CT- or EUS-guided transluminal (transgastric or transduodenal) drainage with or without endoscopic necrosectomy (DEN) has largely replaced open surgery. A 2023 meta-analysis of endoscopic vs. surgical necrosectomy found comparable mortality (8% vs. 10%) but shorter hospital stay (mean difference −15 days) and lower new-onset organ failure with the endoscopic approach.
  • Video-assisted retroperitoneal debridement (VARD): Minimally invasive alternative to open necrosectomy when percutaneous drainage is insufficient. Used in the step-up approach from the PANTER trial. Performed through a small flank incision with endoscopic guidance.
  • Open necrosectomy: Rarely needed now. Reserved for patients with extensive infected necrosis not amenable to endoscopic or minimally invasive approaches. Mortality of open necrosectomy is still 10–30% depending on case selection and center volume.
  • Pancreatic duct stenting: For disconnected pancreatic duct syndrome — a late complication of severe necrotizing pancreatitis where the duct is transected, causing persistent pancreatic fistula or recurrent pseudocysts. Endoscopic transpapillary stenting bridges the gap.

Open questions / active research

  • What is the optimal type and volume of IV fluid? Lactated Ringer's is preferred over normal saline, but the ideal rate and whether goal-directed therapy using hemodynamic targets (stroke volume variation, lactate clearance) improves outcomes over fixed-rate protocols is unknown. The FLUID trial (ISRCTN pending) is testing this.
  • Can targeted anti-inflammatory therapy (IL-1 receptor antagonists, IL-6 inhibitors) improve outcomes in predicted severe pancreatitis? Anakinra (IL-1RA) showed promise in a phase II trial (n=48) with reduced CRP at 72 hours. Larger trials are needed.
  • Does early ERCP benefit all patients with gallstone pancreatitis and elevated bilirubin, or only those with documented cholangitis? Current guidelines restrict urgent ERCP to cholangitis cases, but some argue that subtle biliary obstruction without overt cholangitis still benefits.
  • What defines the optimal timing for enteral nutrition in severe pancreatitis? Current guidelines recommend within 24–48 hours, but the exact threshold for when the gastrointestinal tract is "ready" is unclear — gastric feeding vs. post-pyloric vs. nasojejunal remains debated.
  • Can serum biomarkers (procalcitonin, suPAR, microRNAs) replace CT in predicting necrosis extent? Procalcitonin >3.8 ng/mL at 48 hours predicts infected necrosis with 92% sensitivity in preliminary studies.
  • What is the optimal management of asymptomatic pseudocysts? Most resolve spontaneously within 6 months. The threshold for intervention (size, wall maturity, growth trajectory) is poorly defined.
  • Can antibiotics targeted against the gut microbiome (selective digestive decontamination) reduce infected necrosis? An old concept being revisited with modern microbiome analysis.
  • Why does only a minority of heavy drinkers develop pancreatitis? Genetic susceptibility (PRSS1, SPINK1, CFTR mutations) explains some but not most cases. The "second hit" hypothesis — an environmental trigger on a permissive genetic background — remains incompletely understood.
