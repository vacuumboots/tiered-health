🤢 Peptic Ulcer Disease

Also called: PUD, stomach ulcer, duodenal ulcer

Anatomy

Grade 6 reading level

What is peptic ulcer disease?

A peptic ulcer is a sore in the lining of your stomach or the first part of your small intestine (duodenum). Most are caused by a bacterial infection (H. pylori) or from taking certain painkillers (NSAIDs like ibuprofen, naproxen, aspirin). About 10% of people develop an ulcer at some point in their lives.

What to do right now

  1. Stop NSAIDs — if you've been taking ibuprofen, naproxen, or aspirin regularly, stop immediately (ask your doctor about alternatives).
  2. Take antacids — over-the-counter options like Tums, Pepto-Bismol, or Gaviscon can calm the pain while you figure out the cause.
  3. See your doctor — they can test for H. pylori (breath test or stool test) and prescribe the right treatment. Most ulcers heal with medication.
  4. Eat small, frequent meals — large meals stretch the stomach and can worsen the pain. Avoid spicy, acidic, or fried foods until you feel better.
⚠️ Go to the emergency room if:
  • You vomit blood (looks like coffee grounds or bright red)
  • You pass black, tarry, or bloody stools (melena)
  • You have sudden, sharp stomach pain that doesn't go away
  • Your stomach feels hard and tender when touched
  • You feel dizzy, lightheaded, or faint (signs of internal bleeding)
Good news

Peptic ulcers are treatable. With the right antibiotics for H. pylori (if that's the cause) or acid-reducing medication, most ulcers heal in 4 to 8 weeks. The key is getting tested and treated properly — don't just mask the pain with antacids.

Grade 12 reading level

What's happening in your body

Your stomach lining has a natural defense system — a layer of mucus, bicarbonate, and good blood flow that protects it from its own acidic digestive juices (pH 1–2). A peptic ulcer forms when this defense breaks down, allowing acid to eat through the lining and create an open sore.

There are two types of ulcers:

  • Gastric ulcers — in the stomach itself. Often cause pain during or shortly after eating.
  • Duodenal ulcers — in the duodenum, the first part of the small intestine. Usually cause pain 2–3 hours after eating or at night, and eating often relieves the pain.

Two things cause most ulcers:

  1. H. pylori infection — This spiral-shaped bacterium burrows into the stomach lining, producing enzymes (urease, phospholipase) and toxins that break down the protective mucus layer. Your immune response adds inflammation, weakening the lining further. H. pylori causes about 70% of duodenal ulcers and 50% of gastric ulcers worldwide.
  1. NSAIDs (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs) — Ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac, and aspirin block enzymes called COX-1 and COX-2. COX-1 produces prostaglandins that protect the stomach lining by maintaining mucus production and good blood flow. When you block COX-1 with NSAIDs, the lining loses its protection and acid can damage it. The risk is highest in people over 60, those on high doses or multiple NSAIDs, and people who also take blood thinners or steroids.

Symptoms

  • Burning or gnawing stomach pain: in the upper middle abdomen (epigastric area). Comes and goes, often in cycles of days to weeks.
  • Pain at night: wakes you from sleep, especially with duodenal ulcers
  • Pain that's worse or better with food: duodenal ulcers typically feel better after eating; gastric ulcers often feel worse
  • Bloating, burping, feeling full quickly: nonspecific but common
  • Heartburn or acid reflux: can overlap with GERD
  • Nausea or vomiting: especially with gastric ulcers

Some people (especially the elderly or those on NSAIDs) have "silent ulcers" — no pain at all until the ulcer starts bleeding.

Treatment

  • H. pylori eradication: If you test positive, you'll get a 14-day course of "triple therapy": a proton pump inhibitor (PPI — omeprazole, pantoprazole, lansoprazole) + clarithromycin + amoxicillin. If you have a penicillin allergy or live in an area with high antibiotic resistance, "bismuth quadruple therapy" is an alternative (PPI + bismuth subsalicylate + metronidazole + tetracycline). Eradication success is 85–95% with first-line therapy.
  • PPIs alone: If your ulcer is NSAID-related and H. pylori-negative, PPIs heal most ulcers in 4–8 weeks. You'll need to stop NSAIDs for the ulcer to heal.
  • H2 blockers: An older but still effective option (famotidine, nizatidine). They reduce acid less powerfully than PPIs.
  • Antacids and cytoprotective agents: Sucralfate coats the ulcer like a bandage. Antacids (calcium carbonate, magnesium hydroxide) neutralize acid quickly for symptom relief.
  • Follow-up endoscopy: If you had a gastric ulcer, your doctor will want a repeat endoscopy after 8–12 weeks to make sure it's healed — gastric ulcers can sometimes be malignant.
What NOT to do
  • Don't keep taking NSAIDs without protecting your stomach — even low-dose aspirin can cause ulcers in susceptible people. If you need pain relief, ask about acetaminophen or a COX-2 selective inhibitor (celecoxib) with a PPI.
  • Don't smoke — smoking delays ulcer healing by reducing blood flow to the stomach lining and increasing acid production.
  • Don't ignore black stools or vomiting blood — these are signs of a bleeding ulcer and require immediate medical attention.
  • Don't rely on antacids alone — they mask symptoms but don't heal the underlying cause.
  • Don't drink alcohol excessively — alcohol irritates the stomach lining and can interfere with healing.

Recovery Timeline

Week 1–2
Start PPI therapy (with or without antibiotics). Pain typically improves within 2–3 days. If H. pylori positive, you'll complete your 14-day antibiotic course. Stool softeners may help with constipation from bismuth-based therapies.
Week 3–4
Pain should be largely resolved. Continue PPI alone if you were on triple therapy. The ulcer surface begins to re-epithelialize with healthy mucosal cells. You can gradually return to normal eating.
Week 4–8
Most uncomplicated ulcers heal completely. If H. pylori-positive, confirmation of eradication should be done with a stool antigen test or urea breath test at least 4 weeks after completing antibiotics (and while off PPIs for 2 weeks).
Week 8–12
Follow-up endoscopy for gastric ulcers to confirm healing and rule out malignancy. If the ulcer hasn't healed, your doctor will investigate other causes (Zollinger-Ellison syndrome, Crohn's, NSAID surreptitious use, resistant H. pylori).
6+ months
If H. pylori was eradicated and NSAIDs are avoided, recurrence rate is very low (under 5% per year). Annual re-testing for H. pylori is not needed unless symptoms return.
Best current evidence

Pathophysiology

Peptic ulcer disease results from an imbalance between aggressive factors (gastric acid, pepsin, H. pylori, NSAIDs) and mucosal defense factors (mucus-bicarbonate barrier, epithelial tight junctions, prostaglandins, nitric oxide, mucosal blood flow, and cellular restitution).

Gastric acid and pepsin: The stomach produces 2–3 liters of gastric juice daily. Parietal cells secrete hydrochloric acid via the H⁺/K⁺-ATPase proton pump, stimulated by histamine (from enterochromaffin-like cells via H₂ receptors), acetylcholine (vagal nerve via M₃ receptors), and gastrin (G cells via CCK₂ receptors). Chief cells secrete pepsinogen, which is activated to pepsin at pH < 5. Acid and pepsin are the final common pathway of mucosal injury — without acid, ulcers don't form, even with H. pylori or NSAIDs.

H. pylori pathogenesis: Helicobacter pylori is a Gram-negative, microaerophilic, spiral-shaped bacterium that colonizes the gastric mucosa. It produces urease, which hydrolyzes urea to ammonia — neutralizing the acidic microenvironment around the bacterium and allowing it to survive in the stomach. Key virulence factors include:

  • CagA (cytotoxin-associated gene A): injected into gastric epithelial cells via a type IV secretion system. It disrupts intracellular signaling (SHP-2 phosphorylation), leading to cytoskeletal rearrangement, cellular proliferation, and increased IL-8 production — promoting inflammation and carcinogenesis. CagA-positive strains are associated with more severe inflammation and higher ulcer risk.
  • VacA (vacuolating cytotoxin): forms membrane pores that cause vacuolation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and apoptosis of epithelial cells. VacA also inhibits T-cell activation, helping the bacterium evade immune clearance.
  • BabA (blood-group-antigen-binding adhesin): mediates adherence to Lewis⁵ antigens on gastric epithelium, enhancing colonization density.

The resulting chronic active gastritis is predominantly antral in duodenal ulcer disease (sparing the acid-producing body, leading to increased gastrin and acid output) and pangastritis with corpus atrophy in gastric ulcer disease (reduced acid but severe mucosal damage). The difference in gastritis pattern is the key determinant of whether a person develops a duodenal ulcer, gastric ulcer, or MALT lymphoma.

NSAID pathogenesis: NSAIDs cause injury by two mechanisms. (1) Topical: weak acids that become trapped in gastric epithelial cells (ion trapping), causing direct cellular damage. (2) Systemic: inhibition of cyclooxygenase (COX)-1 and COX-2. COX-1 is constitutively expressed in the gastric mucosa and produces prostaglandins (PGE₂, PGI₂), which stimulate mucus and bicarbonate secretion, maintain mucosal blood flow, and promote epithelial cell proliferation and restitution. COX-2 is induced at sites of inflammation. Non-selective NSAIDs block both, depriving the mucosa of prostaglandin-mediated protection. COX-2 selective inhibitors (coxibs) spare COX-1 and have lower ulcer risk but still carry some risk, especially in patients on aspirin.

Risk factors for NSAID ulcer: Age > 65, high-dose NSAIDs, concomitant anticoagulants or corticosteroids, prior ulcer history, H. pylori co-infection, and serious systemic illness.

Differential diagnosis

  1. GERD / reflux esophagitis: Retrosternal burning rather than epigastric pain. Often responds to PPIs but H. pylori testing is negative. Key distinction: pain is substernal, not epigastric.
  2. Functional dyspepsia: Epigastric pain or discomfort without ulcer on endoscopy. Accounts for 50–60% of patients with ulcer-like symptoms. No mucosal break seen. The Rome IV criteria define it by chronic symptoms with normal endoscopy.
  3. Gastric malignancy (adenocarcinoma, lymphoma, GIST): Persistent ulcerating lesion that may look benign. Any gastric ulcer should have biopsies at initial endoscopy to exclude malignancy. Lack of healing at 8–12 week follow-up endoscopy is suspicious.
  4. Pancreatitis: Epigastric pain that radiates to the back, relieved by leaning forward. Elevated lipase and amylase. CT shows pancreatic inflammation.
  5. Cholelithiasis / biliary colic: Right upper quadrant or epigastric pain, colicky, often after fatty meals. Ultrasound shows gallstones. No H. pylori link.
  6. Zollinger-Ellison syndrome (gastrinoma): Severe, recurrent, or multiple ulcers in atypical locations (post-bulbar duodenum, jejunum). Caused by a gastrin-secreting neuroendocrine tumor (usually in the duodenum or pancreas). Suspect if: ulcers recur after H. pylori eradication, there's diarrhea, hypercalcemia, or multiple ulcers. Check fasting gastrin level (>1,000 pg/mL is diagnostic; 200–1,000 requires secretin stimulation test).
  7. Crohn's disease: Can cause ulcers anywhere in the GI tract including the stomach and duodenum. Look for oral ulcers, diarrhea, weight loss, fistulas, or perianal disease. Biopsies show non-caseating granulomas.
  8. CMV or HSV esophagitis/gastritis: In immunocompromised patients (HIV, transplant, chemotherapy). Endoscopy shows punched-out ulcers; biopsy shows viral inclusions.

Evidence summary

Prevalence and epidemiology: The lifetime prevalence of PUD is approximately 5–10% in Western populations. H. pylori infection accounts for 60–80% of duodenal ulcers and 40–60% of gastric ulcers (Malfertheiner et al., 2022). The global prevalence of H. pylori infection remains approximately 40–50%, though rates are declining in developed nations due to improved sanitation and antibiotic use. NSAID/aspirin use accounts for 20–30% of ulcers, with the risk rising dramatically in combination with anticoagulants (HR 4–7 for GI bleeding with NSAID + DOAC vs. either alone).

H. pylori eradication: The Maastricht VI/Florence consensus report (Malfertheiner et al., 2022) recommends standard triple therapy (PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin) as first-line only in regions where clarithromycin resistance is < 15%. In high-resistance areas, bismuth quadruple therapy (PPI + bismuth + metronidazole + tetracycline) or concomitant therapy (PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin + metronidazole) is preferred. A Cochrane review (Ford et al., 2016, 76 RCTs, n=16,383) found that triple therapy for 14 days achieved superior eradication rates compared to 7 days (OR 1.6, 95% CI 1.4–1.9). Eradication of H. pylori reduces duodenal ulcer recurrence from 67% to 3% at 12 months (Hopkins et al., 1996 meta-analysis).

PPI vs. H2 blocker for ulcer healing: A landmark meta-analysis (Howden & Hunt, 1991) found that PPIs heal a higher proportion of duodenal ulcers at 4 weeks (80–90% with omeprazole vs. 60–70% with ranitidine). Gastric ulcer healing at 8 weeks: 85–90% with PPI vs. 65–75% with H2 blockers. PPIs maintain intragastric pH > 4 for 12–18 hours/day vs. 8–10 hours with H2 blockers.

NSAID ulcer prevention: The VIGOR study (Bombardier et al., 2000, NEJM) showed a 50% relative risk reduction in clinical upper GI events with rofecoxib (COX-2 inhibitor) vs. naproxen. The CLASS study (Silverstein et al., 2000, JAMA) showed celecoxib had lower ulcer complication rates than non-selective NSAIDs (0.76% vs. 1.45% at 6 months). The current standard of care for high-risk patients requiring NSAIDs is a COX-2 inhibitor plus a PPI — this combination reduces ulcer risk by 80–90% compared to NSAID alone (Chan et al., 2002).

ACG Guidelines (Laine et al., 2019): Key recommendations include: (1) Test for H. pylori in all patients with active PUD, (2) Use PPI-based triple therapy for 14 days in first-line treatment, (3) Bismuth quadruple therapy is first-line alternative, (4) Gastric ulcers require follow-up endoscopy with biopsy at 8–12 weeks, (5) Test of cure (stool antigen or urea breath test) is mandatory — serology should not be used.

Bleeding ulcer management: Endoscopic hemostasis (combination therapy: injection + thermal or mechanical) reduces rebleeding from 25% to 10–12%, mortality from 10% to 5% (ASGE guideline, 2021 meta-analysis). High-dose IV PPI (80 mg bolus + 8 mg/hour infusion) for 72 hours post-endoscopy reduces rebleeding (RR 0.49, 95% CI 0.37–0.65) compared to placebo or H2 blocker (Lau et al., 2000; Sung et al., 2009).

Surgical options

Surgery for peptic ulcer disease has become rare since the advent of PPIs and H. pylori eradication. Indications today include:

  • Perforated ulcer: Surgical closure (omentopexy/Graham patch) is the standard. Laparoscopic repair is preferred when feasible (lower wound infection, shorter hospital stay). A 2020 Cochrane review confirmed equivalence to open repair for selected patients.
  • Bleeding ulcer refractory to endoscopic therapy: Angiographic embolization is increasingly preferred over surgery. When surgery is needed, gastrotomy with under-running of the bleeding vessel (ligation of the gastroduodenal artery) is the emergency procedure. Mortality for emergency ulcer surgery remains 15–25%.
  • Gastric outlet obstruction: From chronic duodenal or pyloric channel ulcer scarring (Bouveret's syndrome). Balloon dilation via endoscopy is first-line (success 70–80%); failure may require pyloroplasty or gastrojejunostomy.
  • Refractory ulcer (rare): Ulcers that fail to heal after 12 weeks of PPI with confirmed H. pylori eradication and NSAID avoidance. Acid-reducing surgery (highly selective vagotomy or vagotomy + antrectomy) is an option, though rarely performed now. Antrectomy with Billroth I or II reconstruction is reserved for complicated cases with gastric outlet obstruction or concern for malignancy.
  • Gastric devascularization: For severe recurrent bleeding from portal hypertensive gastropathy or diffuse gastric antral vascular ectasia (GAVE); very rare.

Open questions / active research

  • What is the optimal second-line regimen after failed bismuth quadruple therapy? Rifabutin-based and levofloxacin-based salvage therapies are available but resistance data are limited.
  • Does H. pylori eradication reduce gastric cancer risk? Cohort studies suggest a 30–50% relative risk reduction, but absolute benefit depends on the presence of pre-neoplastic changes (atrophy, intestinal metaplasia). Long-term RCT data are still maturing.
  • Are vonoprazan (potassium-competitive acid blocker, P-CAB) therapies superior to PPI-based regimens for H. pylori eradication? Vonoprazan-based triple therapy showed non-inferiority in a 2020 phase 3 trial (Maruyama et al.) and may be superior in clarithromycin-resistant strains, but long-term safety data are limited.
  • What is the optimal duration of PPI after endoscopic hemostasis? Current practice is 72-hour high-dose IV then 8-week oral PPI, but shorter courses are being studied.
  • Can fecal microbiota transplantation or probiotics improve H. pylori eradication rates or reduce side effects? Current meta-analyses show modest benefit (5–10% improvement with specific probiotic strains) but the quality of evidence is low.
  • Role of H. pylori in extra-gastric diseases (idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura, iron deficiency anemia, vitamin B12 deficiency) — evidence for benefit of eradication in these conditions is growing but not yet guideline-standard.
