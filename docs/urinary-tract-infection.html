🚽 Urinary Tract Infection

Also called: UTI, bladder infection, cystitis

Anatomy

Grade 6 reading level

What is a urinary tract infection (UTI)?

Bacteria (usually E. coli from your gut) get into your bladder and cause inflammation. It burns when you pee, you feel like you need to go all the time, and your pee may look cloudy or smell funny. UTIs are very common — about 1 in 2 women will have at least one in her lifetime. Men get them too, but less often.

What to do right now

  1. Drink water. Peeing often helps flush bacteria out of your bladder. Aim for a full glass every hour or two.
  2. Call a doctor or clinic. You will likely need a prescription for antibiotics. UTI bacteria don't just go away on their own in most people.
  3. Take over-the-counter pain relief. Phenazopyridine (AZO, Uristat) numbs your bladder lining. Ibuprofen helps with inflammation. Use both if you need to.
  4. Put a hot water bottle on your lower belly and lower back. The warmth eases the cramping feeling.
  5. Pee right after sex if you're sexually active — this pushes bacteria out before they can climb into your bladder.
⚠️ Go to the emergency room if:
  • You have a fever over 101°F (38.3°C) with chills or shaking
  • You have pain in your side or mid-back (flank pain) — could be a kidney infection
  • You are vomiting and can't keep down fluids or medicine
  • You see blood in your pee (pink, red, or cola-colored)
  • You are confused or disoriented (especially in older adults)
Quick facts

A simple bladder infection (cystitis) is different from a kidney infection (pyelonephritis). Cystitis is common, easy to treat, and rarely dangerous with antibiotics. Pyelonephritis means the infection has moved up to your kidneys — it's more serious and may need IV antibiotics. The warning signs are fever, back pain, and nausea. Most people with cystitis feel much better within 48 hours of starting antibiotics.

Grade 12 reading level

What's happening in your body

Your urinary tract is a one-way drainage system: urine is made in your kidneys, travels down the ureters (thin tubes) into the bladder (a stretchy storage sac), and exits through the urethra. Normally urine is sterile — no bacteria live in it.

A UTI starts when bacteria from your gut or skin get into the urethra and swim up into the bladder. The most common culprit is Escherichia coli (E. coli) — it causes about 80% of uncomplicated UTIs. These bacteria have hair-like projections (fimbriae) that help them stick to the bladder wall. Once attached, they multiply and trigger an immune response: the bladder lining becomes inflamed, red, and swollen.

This inflammation explains your symptoms: the burning (dysuria) comes from raw, irritated tissue when urine passes over it. The constant urge to pee (urinary frequency and urgency) happens because inflamed stretch receptors in the bladder wall send "full!" signals to your brain even when the bladder is nearly empty. The suprapubic pressure (ache just above your pubic bone) is from the inflamed bladder muscle cramping.

UTIs are classified as uncomplicated (healthy person with normal urinary tract) or complicated (pregnancy, kidney transplant, catheter, diabetes, structural abnormality in the urinary tract, male sex, or recent hospitalization). The treatment and urgency are different for each.

Symptoms

  • Dysuria: pain or burning when you pee. This is the most common and often the first symptom.
  • Urinary frequency: needing to pee much more often than usual, sometimes every 15–30 minutes.
  • Urgency: a sudden, strong urge to pee that's hard to delay.
  • Suprapubic pain: a dull ache or pressure low in the belly, just above the pubic bone.
  • Cloudy or foul-smelling urine: bacteria and white blood cells change the look and smell of your pee.
  • Hematuria (blood in urine): urine may look pink, red, or cola-colored. Not everyone gets this.
  • Incomplete emptying: feeling like you still need to pee right after you've gone.

Treatment

  • Antibiotics — this is the main treatment. For uncomplicated cystitis in women, the standard options are:
  • Nitrofurantoin (Macrobid): 100 mg twice daily for 5 days. Excellent for cystitis. Well-tolerated. Don't use if you have low kidney function.
  • Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX / Bactrim / Septra): one double-strength tablet twice daily for 3 days. Only use if local E. coli resistance rates are below 20% (in many areas resistance is higher).
  • Fosfomycin trometamol (Monurol): a single 3-gram sachet — convenient but slightly less effective than the other options.
  • Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin): highly effective but generally reserved because of side effect concerns (tendon, nerve, CNS). Used more for complicated UTIs or pyelonephritis.
  • Pain relief:
  • Phenazopyridine (AZO, Uristat): a urinary analgesic that numbs the bladder lining. Take for 1–2 days at most — it turns your pee bright orange and can stain clothes.
  • Ibuprofen or naproxen: reduce the inflammation in the bladder wall. Can be used alongside antibiotics.
  • Hydration: drinking extra water during treatment helps dilute urine and may speed symptom relief. There's limited evidence that increased water intake reduces UTI recurrence in women who drink less than 1.5 L/day.
What NOT to do
  • Don't wait more than 24 hours to see a doctor if you have UTI symptoms. The infection can spread to your kidneys (pyelonephritis).
  • Don't drink cranberry juice to treat an active UTI — it doesn't work once bacteria are already in your bladder. (Cranberry may help prevent UTIs in some people, but it won't clear one you already have.)
  • Don't stop antibiotics early when you feel better. Finish the full course to reduce the chance of resistance.
  • Don't use vaginal douches, scented wipes, or bubble baths — they irritate the urethra and can make symptoms worse.
  • Don't hold your pee — go when you feel the urge, even if it burns.

Recovery Timeline

Day 1
Symptoms start — burning, frequency, urgency. Seek medical care. Urine dipstick at clinic or doctor's office. Start antibiotics same day if positive.
Day 2–3
Most people feel significant improvement within 24–48 hours. Pain and burning should be much less. Keep taking antibiotics. Stay hydrated.
Day 4–7
Symptoms fully resolved for most. Finish the antibiotic course (don't stop early). Repeat urine culture if symptoms persist.
Day 7–14
If symptoms are still present, the bacteria may be resistant to the antibiotic. Return for a urine culture and sensitivity test. May need a different antibiotic.
3–6 months
About 25–30% of women will have a second UTI within 6 months. If you get 3 or more in a year, talk to your doctor about prevention strategies (prophylactic antibiotics, postcoital antibiotic, vaginal estrogen if postmenopausal).

Pathophysiology

A urinary tract infection begins when uropathogenic bacteria, most commonly uropathogenic Escherichia coli (UPEC), colonize the periurethral area and ascend through the urethra into the bladder. UPEC accounts for 75–85% of community-acquired uncomplicated UTIs. Other causative organisms include Staphylococcus saprophyticus (5–10%, especially in younger women), Klebsiella pneumoniae, Proteus mirabilis, and Enterococcus faecalis.

Adhesion and invasion. UPEC expresses type 1 fimbriae — hair-like adhesins that bind specifically to mannosylated uroplakin receptors on the surface of bladder umbrella cells. This binding triggers bacterial internalization into the epithelial cells, where UPEC forms intracellular bacterial communities (IBCs) — biofilm-like colonies protected from host immune defenses and antibiotics. This intracellular reservoir explains both the high recurrence rate (up to 30% within 6 months) and the variability in treatment response. A second fimbrial type, P fimbriae, binds to glycolipid receptors on kidney epithelial cells and is strongly associated with pyelonephritis.

Host inflammatory response. Bacterial lipopolysaccharide (LPS) and flagellin activate Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) on bladder epithelial cells, triggering NF-κB-mediated transcription of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, IL-8). Neutrophils infiltrate the bladder wall within hours, producing the characteristic pyuria (white blood cells in urine) seen on dipstick. The inflammatory cascade damages bladder epithelial cells — they exfoliate (slough off) as a defense mechanism, which exposes underlying cells but also removes the protective glycosaminoglycan (GAG) layer that normally prevents bacterial adherence.

Ascending infection. If not cleared by the immune system or antibiotics, bacteria ascend the ureters against the flow of urine. Ureteral peristalsis normally pushes urine down, but bacterial endotoxins can impair this motility. Once bacteria reach the renal pelvis and invade the kidney interstitium, the result is acute pyelonephritis — a more serious infection characterized by tubular inflammation, microabscess formation, and the risk of bacteremia (bacteria entering the bloodstream) and sepsis.

Complicated vs. uncomplicated. The distinction matters for treatment:

  • Uncomplicated: occurs in a premenopausal, non-pregnant woman with a normal urinary tract. Host defenses are intact. Short-course antibiotics (3–5 days) work well.
  • Complicated: occurs in the setting of an anatomic or functional abnormality (catheter, obstruction, stones, pregnancy, diabetes, immunosuppression, renal transplant, or male sex). Treatment requires 7–14 days of antibiotics and often a broader spectrum agent.

Differential diagnosis

  1. Acute cystitis (uncomplicated UTI): Dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain, no fever. Positive dipstick for leukocyte esterase and/or nitrites. The classic presentation.
  2. Pyelonephritis: Same urinary symptoms PLUS fever (>38°C/100.4°F), flank pain, costovertebral angle tenderness, nausea/vomiting. Requires a urine culture and blood cultures if febrile. Needs a longer treatment course (7–14 days).
  3. Asymptomatic bacteriuria (ASB): Positive urine culture with NO urinary symptoms. Common in pregnancy, elderly, catheterized patients. Does NOT need treatment except in pregnancy and before urological procedures. Overtreatment contributes to antibiotic resistance.
  4. Urethritis (STI-related): Dysuria and discharge caused by Neisseria gonorrhoeae, Chlamydia trachomatis, or Mycoplasma genitalium. Key clues: recent new sexual partner, vaginal discharge in women, penile discharge in men, and a negative urine dipstick. Treat with doxycycline ± ceftriaxone.
  5. Vaginitis: Candida (yeast), bacterial vaginosis, or Trichomonas. Dysuria is external (urine passing over inflamed labia), not internal. Accompanied by vaginal discharge, itching, or odor.
  6. Interstitial cystitis / bladder pain syndrome: Chronic pelvic pain with urinary frequency but NO bacteria on culture. Symptoms wax and wane over months to years. No response to antibiotics.
  7. Nephrolithiasis (kidney stones): Flank pain radiating to groin, hematuria, but NO fever or dysuria unless there's a superimposed infection. CT scan shows the stone.

Evidence summary

Epidemiology: UTI is one of the most common bacterial infections in ambulatory care. Foxman (2014) reported that nearly 1 in 2 women will experience at least one UTI in their lifetime, with an estimated annual incidence of 12.6% among women aged 18 or older. Recurrence is common — 27–44% of women experience a second UTI within 6–12 months of the first. In the US, UTIs account for approximately 7 million office visits and 1 million emergency department visits annually, with direct costs exceeding $3.5 billion per year (Flores-Mireles et al., 2015).

IDSA guidelines (Gupta et al., 2011): The Infectious Diseases Society of America and the European Society for Microbiology and Infectious Diseases published joint guidelines for the treatment of acute uncomplicated cystitis and pyelonephritis. For cystitis, the recommended first-line agents are nitrofurantoin (5 days), TMP-SMX (3 days if local resistance <20%), and fosfomycin (single dose). Fluoroquinolones are reserved as second-line due to adverse effects and ecological collateral damage. For pyelonephritis, empiric treatment with a fluoroquinolone (ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID for 7 days) or an intravenous cephalosporin (ceftriaxone 1 g daily) is recommended, with step-down to oral therapy once culture results confirm susceptibility.

Nitrofurantoin vs. TMP-SMX (Hooton, NEJM 2012): Hooton's clinical practice review summarized that nitrofurantoin remains highly effective for uncomplicated cystitis with resistance rates of less than 5% worldwide — a major advantage given the rising resistance to TMP-SMX in many regions. In areas where TMP-SMX resistance exceeds 20% (much of the southern US, Europe, Asia), nitrofurantoin is the preferred empiric choice.

Antibiotic resistance crisis: Global surveillance studies document alarmingly high rates of fluoroquinolone resistance in UPEC (>20–40% in many countries) and rising extended-spectrum beta-lactamase (ESBL) production. The World Health Organization has listed fluoroquinolone-resistant E. coli as a high-priority pathogen for which new antibiotics are urgently needed. Nitrofurantoin resistance, however, has remained stable at <5% over decades of use — a phenomenon attributed to its multiple mechanisms of action (inhibition of multiple bacterial enzymes) and its lack of systemic tissue penetration (acting primarily in the urine).

Cranberry for prevention and treatment: A 2023 Cochrane review of cranberry products for the prevention of UTIs (50 studies, n=8,851) found that cranberry juice/products reduced the risk of UTI in women with recurrent UTIs (RR 0.74, 95% CI 0.55–0.98) and in children (RR 0.46, 95% CI 0.32–0.65), but not in elderly people, pregnant women, or catheterized patients. For treatment of an active UTI, cranberry has NO role — studies consistently show no benefit over placebo.

Water for prevention: A landmark 2018 RCT by Hooton et al. (JAMA Intern Med) found that premenopausal women with recurrent UTIs who increased their daily water intake by 1.5 L had a 47% reduction in UTI episodes compared to the control group over 12 months. This is one of the few non-pharmacological interventions with high-quality evidence.

Open questions / active research

  • Oral vs. non-oral therapy for pyelonephritis: Can early transition from IV to oral antibiotics be accelerated to 24–48 hours in stable patients? Current practice (IV then step-down at 72 hours) may be overly conservative given oral bioavailability of fluoroquinolones and TMP-SMX.
  • UTI microbiome: The discovery of a resident urinary microbiome (distinct from the gut microbiome) raises questions about how commensal bacteria in the bladder may protect against or predispose to UTI. Early studies show Lactobacillus species in asymptomatic women may compete with UPEC for adherence sites.
  • Vaccine development: Multi-epitope vaccines targeting UPEC adhesins (FimH, FimCH) and siderophore receptors have shown promise in phase II trials but none have yet reached regulatory approval. An oral whole-cell vaccine (Uro-Vaxom) is available in some European countries with modest efficacy (RR ~0.6 for recurrence) but limited penetration in North America.
  • Non-antibiotic prophylaxis: D-mannose (a simple sugar that blocks type 1 fimbriae binding) showed promise in an early 2014 RCT but subsequent trials have been mixed. Methenamine hippurate (which produces formaldehyde in acidic urine) is being re-evaluated as a prophylaxis alternative — a 2022 RCT in non-pregnant women found it non-inferior to daily antibiotics for preventing recurrent UTI.
  • Phage therapy: Bacteriophages specific to UPEC are in early clinical development as a targeted alternative to broad-spectrum antibiotics. A 2024 phase I/II trial in Belgium showed safety and preliminary efficacy in treatment-resistant UTIs.
